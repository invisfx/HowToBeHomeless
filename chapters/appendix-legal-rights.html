Your Legal Rights

Every legal claim in this book, backed by case law, statute, or constitutional amendment.

This appendix documents the legal basis for every rights-related claim made in this book. It is organized by topic. Each entry includes the legal citation, what it establishes, where it applies, and any limitations you should know about. This is not legal advice. It is legal information. If you need legal help, contact Legal Aid (lawhelp.org) or call 211.

Laws change. Court decisions get overturned. Local ordinances vary by city. The information here was accurate as of March 2026. Always verify current local law before relying on any of these protections.

1. Right to Exist in Public Space

The Eighth Amendment

Citation: U.S. Constitution, Amendment VIII

Text: "Excessive bail shall not be required, nor excessive fines imposed, nor cruel and unusual punishments inflicted."

What it means for you: The government cannot punish you for being homeless. However, the scope of this protection was significantly narrowed in 2024.

Martin v. City of Boise (2018)

Citation: Martin v. City of Boise, 920 F.3d 584 (9th Cir. 2019)

What it established: Cities cannot enforce criminal penalties against homeless people for sleeping outdoors on public property when no adequate shelter is available. Criminalizing involuntary conduct (sleeping when you have nowhere else to go) violates the Eighth Amendment.

Where it applies: Originally binding in the 9th Circuit (Alaska, Arizona, California, Hawaii, Idaho, Montana, Nevada, Oregon, Washington). Effectively overturned by Grants Pass in 2024 (see below).

City of Grants Pass v. Johnson (2024)

Citation: City of Grants Pass v. Johnson, 603 U.S. ___ (2024), No. 23-175

What it established: The Supreme Court ruled 6-3 that anti-camping ordinances do not constitute "cruel and unusual punishment" under the Eighth Amendment. Cities CAN now fine and arrest people for sleeping outdoors, even when no shelter beds are available.

Where it applies: Nationwide. This is Supreme Court precedent.

What it does NOT do: It does not require cities to criminalize homelessness. It gives them permission to. Many cities have chosen not to enforce aggressively. Check your city's current policy.

Practical impact: Within 6 months of this ruling, 100+ cities adopted new camping bans. Arrests increased 68% in Los Angeles, doubled in San Diego, and nearly tripled in Sacramento.

2. Panhandling and Free Speech

The First Amendment

Citation: U.S. Constitution, Amendment I

What it means for you: Peaceful panhandling is protected speech under the First Amendment. Asking for money is a form of expression. Cities can regulate the time, place, and manner of panhandling but cannot ban it outright.

Reed v. Town of Gilbert (2015)

Citation: Reed v. Town of Gilbert, 576 U.S. 155 (2015)

What it established: Content-based restrictions on speech are presumptively unconstitutional. Laws that single out panhandling signs or verbal requests for money (while allowing other speech in the same location) are content-based and subject to strict scrutiny.

Where it applies: Nationwide.

Loper v. New York City Police Department (1992)

Citation: Loper v. New York City Police Department, 999 F.2d 699 (2d Cir. 1993)

What it established: Begging is protected speech under the First Amendment. A New York City law banning all begging was struck down as unconstitutional.

Limitations You Should Know

  • Aggressive panhandling is NOT protected. Blocking someone's path, following them, touching them, or threatening them can be criminalized.
  • Buffer zones are often upheld. Cities can prohibit panhandling within a certain distance of ATMs, bus stops, schools, or building entrances.
  • Vehicle approaches can be restricted. Many cities ban approaching vehicles on roadways for safety reasons.
  • Panhandling bans have expanded 103% nationwide since 2006. Living-in-vehicles bans have expanded 213%.

3. Property Rights

The Fourth Amendment

Citation: U.S. Constitution, Amendment IV

What it means for you: You have the right to be secure in your "persons, houses, papers, and effects" against unreasonable searches and seizures. This applies even if your "house" is a tent or your "effects" are in a shopping cart.

The Fourteenth Amendment (Due Process)

Citation: U.S. Constitution, Amendment XIV, Section 1

What it means for you: No state shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." The government cannot destroy your belongings without notice and an opportunity to retrieve them.

Lavan v. City of Los Angeles (2012)

Citation: Lavan v. City of Los Angeles, 693 F.3d 1022 (9th Cir. 2012)

What it established: The City of Los Angeles violated the Fourth and Fourteenth Amendment rights of homeless individuals by seizing and destroying their personal property left temporarily unattended on public sidewalks. Even momentarily unattended property that has not been abandoned is constitutionally protected.

Where it applies: 9th Circuit. Persuasive authority elsewhere.

Practical meaning: During a sweep, the city must provide notice (usually 48-72 hours), must store confiscated property for a reasonable period (typically 60-90 days), and must provide a way for you to claim it. If they destroy your property without notice, that is a constitutional violation.

4. Police Interactions

Terry v. Ohio (1968)

Citation: Terry v. Ohio, 392 U.S. 1 (1968)

What it established: Police can briefly stop and frisk a person if they have "reasonable suspicion" that the person is involved in criminal activity and may be armed. But they cannot stop you just for being homeless or looking out of place.

What this means for you: If stopped, you must provide your name in most states (stop-and-identify statutes). You do NOT have to consent to a search. Say clearly: "I do not consent to a search." You do NOT have to answer questions beyond identifying yourself.

Miranda v. Arizona (1966)

Citation: Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U.S. 436 (1966)

What it established: If you are arrested, police must inform you of your right to remain silent and your right to an attorney before questioning you. Anything you say before Miranda warnings may be inadmissible.

What to do: If arrested, say: "I want a lawyer." Then stop talking. Do not explain, do not argue, do not apologize. Everything you say will be used against you.

Key Phrases to Memorize

  • "Am I free to go?" — Establishes whether you are being detained.
  • "I do not consent to a search." — Protects your Fourth Amendment rights.
  • "I am invoking my right to remain silent." — Stops questioning.
  • "I want a lawyer." — Requires police to stop interrogation.

5. Right to Shelter

Callahan v. Carey (1979) — New York City

Citation: Callahan v. Carey, No. 79-42582 (N.Y. Sup. Ct. 1979), consent decree 1981

What it established: New York City must provide shelter to every person who requests it. This is the only major city with a legal right to shelter, established through a consent decree.

Current status: Still in effect but actively being limited. 30-day shelter limits have been imposed for some populations. The city houses 85,000+ people nightly.

Massachusetts Right to Shelter

Citation: Massachusetts General Laws, Chapter 23B

What it established: Massachusetts is the only state with a statutory right to shelter for families with children.

Current status: Being limited. Governor Healey imposed caps on family shelter stays. The right exists but is narrowing.

Other Jurisdictions

Washington, D.C. has a right to shelter during hypothermia season (November–March) when temperatures drop below 32°F. No other major city or state has a blanket right to shelter.

6. Education Rights (If You Have Children)

McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act

Citation: 42 U.S.C. § 11431 et seq. (Title VII-B, reauthorized under Every Student Succeeds Act 2015)

What it establishes:

  • Homeless children and youth have the right to immediate enrollment in school, even without records, immunization, proof of residence, or a legal guardian present.
  • Children can remain in their school of origin even if they move to a different district, and the district must provide transportation.
  • Every school district must designate a McKinney-Vento liaison whose job is to help homeless families. Call the school district and ask for theirs by name.
  • The definition of "homeless" includes children living in shelters, motels, cars, parks, campgrounds, doubling up with other families, or in substandard housing.

Where it applies: Nationwide. This is federal law.

Critical fact: Being homeless is NOT grounds for CPS removal. Homelessness alone is not child neglect under any state law. If anyone threatens CPS action solely because you are homeless, they are wrong.

7. Healthcare Rights

EMTALA (Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act)

Citation: 42 U.S.C. § 1395dd (1986)

What it establishes: Any hospital that participates in Medicare (virtually all hospitals) must:

  • Provide a medical screening examination to anyone who comes to the emergency department, regardless of ability to pay, insurance status, or immigration status.
  • Stabilize any emergency medical condition before discharge or transfer.
  • Cannot refuse treatment, cannot require payment before treatment, and cannot "dump" patients to other facilities to avoid treating them.

Where it applies: Nationwide. Any Medicare-participating hospital.

What it does NOT cover: Non-emergency care, dental care (unless life-threatening infection), mental health (unless immediate danger), ongoing treatment or prescriptions.

If you are denied: File a complaint with CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) at 1-800-633-4227.

Hill-Burton Act (Free/Reduced Care Obligation)

Citation: 42 U.S.C. § 291 et seq. (1946)

What it establishes: Hospitals that received federal construction funding are obligated to provide a reasonable volume of free or reduced-cost care. Over 140 facilities still have this obligation.

How to find one: Call 1-800-638-0742 or visit hrsa.gov/get-health-care/affordable/hill-burton.

Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs)

Citation: 42 U.S.C. § 254b (Public Health Service Act, Section 330)

What they are: Community health centers that receive federal funding to provide care on a sliding fee scale based on ability to pay. They cannot turn you away for inability to pay.

How to find one: findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov

8. Voting Rights

National Voter Registration Act (1993)

Citation: 52 U.S.C. § 20501 et seq. (Motor Voter Act)

What it means for you: You do NOT need a traditional address to register to vote. You can use:

  • A shelter address
  • A street intersection where you sleep
  • A description of where you stay (e.g., "under the overpass at 5th and Main")
  • A day center or social service agency address

Where it applies: Nationwide.

ID requirements vary by state. If you lack photo ID, many states accept alternative forms. Contact your county elections office or call 1-866-OUR-VOTE for help.

9. Benefits and Services

SNAP (Food Stamps) Without an Address

Citation: 7 U.S.C. § 2011 et seq.; 7 C.F.R. § 273.1(b)

Your right: You can apply for SNAP without a fixed address. Write "homeless" on the address line. Use USPS General Delivery or a shelter address for mail. You also qualify for expedited benefits (food within 7 days) if you have less than $150 in liquid assets and less than $100 in monthly income.

Medicaid Without an Address

Citation: 42 U.S.C. § 1396 et seq.; ACA Medicaid Expansion (2010)

Your right: In the 40 states that expanded Medicaid, any adult earning under 138% of the federal poverty level qualifies. Homeless individuals with zero income qualify. You do not need an address to apply. Apply at healthcare.gov or call 1-800-318-2596.

Unemployment Insurance

Citation: Federal Unemployment Tax Act, 26 U.S.C. § 3301 et seq.; state unemployment statutes

Your right: If you were laid off (not fired for cause), you are entitled to unemployment benefits. File the day you lose your job. Benefits typically replace 40-50% of your previous wages for 26 weeks. You need a mailing address (General Delivery works) and a bank account or prepaid card for direct deposit.

10. Employment Protections

Fair Chance Hiring Laws (Ban the Box)

What they are: Laws that prohibit employers from asking about criminal history on initial job applications. Over 37 states and 150+ cities have adopted some version.

What they do NOT do: They do not prevent employers from running background checks later in the hiring process. They delay the question, not eliminate it.

Homeless Anti-Discrimination Laws

Where they exist:

  • Rhode Island — R.I. Gen. Laws § 34-37.1. Prohibits discrimination based on housing status in employment, housing, and public accommodations.
  • Connecticut — Conn. Gen. Stat. § 46a-64c. Homeless status is a protected class.
  • Illinois — 775 ILCS 45/ (Bill of Rights for the Homeless Act). Protects rights to use public spaces, privacy, equal treatment by government agencies.
  • Washington, D.C. — D.C. Human Rights Act amendment. Homelessness is a protected class. You can file discrimination complaints with the D.C. Office of Human Rights.

In most states, housing status is NOT a protected class. An employer can legally refuse to hire you because you are homeless, unless your state or city has specific protections.

11. Vehicle Dwelling

Desertrain v. City of Los Angeles (2014)

Citation: Desertrain v. City of Los Angeles, 754 F.3d 1147 (9th Cir. 2014)

What it established: Los Angeles's vehicle-dwelling ordinance (LAMC 85.02) was struck down as unconstitutionally vague. The law criminalized "using a vehicle as living quarters" but failed to define what that meant, making it impossible for people to know what conduct was prohibited.

Current status: Many cities have since rewritten their vehicle-dwelling ordinances with more specific language. Check your city's current code. Vehicle-dwelling bans have expanded 213% since 2006.

12. Encampment Sweeps

Your Rights During a Sweep

Based on Lavan v. City of Los Angeles (p. 450) and subsequent case law:

  • Notice required: Most jurisdictions require 48-72 hours written notice before clearing an encampment. Some require longer.
  • Property storage: Confiscated property must be stored for a reasonable period (typically 60-90 days) and you must be told where and how to claim it.
  • Documentation: Photograph everything before a sweep. Document what you own, where it was, and what was taken. This is evidence if your rights are violated.
  • Illegal sweeps: If property is destroyed without notice or storage, contact the ACLU (aclu.org), National Homelessness Law Center (homelesslaw.org), or local Legal Aid.

Post-Grants Pass reality: Cities now have more latitude to clear camps. But property protections under the 4th and 14th Amendments still apply. They can tell you to move. They cannot destroy your belongings without due process.

13. Identification

REAL ID Act Accommodations

Citation: REAL ID Act of 2005, Pub. L. 109-13

What it means for you: The REAL ID Act requires specific documents for compliant IDs, but most states have accommodations for homeless individuals, including:

  • Fee waivers for state ID cards (available in most states — ask at the DMV)
  • Alternative proof of residency (shelter letter, caseworker letter, social service agency letter)
  • Same-day ID issuance in some states for people experiencing homelessness

14. Where to Get Legal Help

  • Legal Aid: lawhelp.org — free civil legal help based on income. Handles evictions, benefits denials, ID issues, discrimination.
  • ACLU: aclu.org — civil rights violations, unconstitutional sweeps, free speech.
  • National Homelessness Law Center: homelesslaw.org — policy advocacy, know-your-rights resources, legal research.
  • Law school clinics: Many law schools operate free clinics for homeless individuals. Call your nearest law school.
  • Public defenders: If you are charged with a crime and cannot afford an attorney, you have a constitutional right to a public defender (Gideon v. Wainwright, 372 U.S. 335, 1963).
  • Homeless courts: Some cities operate special courts for homeless individuals that focus on resolving outstanding warrants, fines, and minor charges without jail time. Ask your caseworker or call 211.
