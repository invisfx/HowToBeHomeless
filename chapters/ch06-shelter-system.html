Specialized Shelters

  • Domestic violence shelters: Confidential locations, typically restricted to survivors and their children. Require intake through a hotline.
  • Family shelters: Separate from general population shelters. Usually have longer stays and more services. Demand far exceeds capacity.
  • Veteran shelters: Operated by VA or partner organizations. Access through VA referral or 211.
  • Youth shelters: For ages 18–24 (sometimes younger). Different rules, different culture, often better resourced.

▶ Your Brain Right Now: Identity Distancing

Researchers Snow and Anderson found that people in shelters use three identity strategies: distancing ("I am not like the others here"), embracement (accepting a homeless identity), and fictive storytelling (constructing an alternative narrative). Distancing is the most common early response, and it is protective — it preserves your sense of self. But it can also isolate you from the very people who understand your situation best. Notice which strategy you are using. There is no wrong answer, but awareness gives you choice.

The Rules They Tell You

  • Curfew: Most shelters lock doors between 7pm and 9pm. Miss it and you lose your bed.
  • Sobriety: Most shelters are dry. Some have breathalyzer checks at the door.
  • Chores: Expect assigned tasks — cleaning, kitchen duty, common area maintenance.
  • Check-in/check-out: Daily sign-in sheets. Miss too many and you are flagged for removal.

Key Takeaways
  • Know the types. Emergency, transitional, and specialized shelters serve different needs.
  • Intake is strategic. Be honest but deliberate about what you share.
  • Learn both rule sets. The posted rules and the social rules are equally important.
  • Guard your belongings. Keep documents and phone on your body at all times.
  • Work the waitlists. Get on every list, check in regularly, and do not let them drop you.
