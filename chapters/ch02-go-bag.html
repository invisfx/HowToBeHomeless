The Go-Bag

What to take, what to leave, and what to get before you lose access.

Everything you own is about to be reduced to what you can carry on your back. That is a hard sentence to read, but the sooner you accept it, the better your decisions will be. The go-bag is not a camping trip packing list. It is the difference between recovering from homelessness in weeks versus months.

The biggest mistake people make is packing too much. You grab sentimental items, extra clothes, things that feel important in the moment. Then you are hauling two overstuffed bags through a bus station at 11 PM, exhausted, with a broken zipper. Pack for function, not comfort.

The second biggest mistake is packing too late. If you are reading this chapter and you still have housing, start assembling your go-bag today. Not tomorrow. Today. Put it in a closet where it is ready to grab.

This chapter covers the three categories that matter: documents, survival gear, and dignity items. Documents come first because losing them creates a bureaucratic nightmare that can stall your recovery for months. Gear keeps you alive. Dignity items keep you human.

One backpack. One duffel or tote at most. If you cannot carry it for a mile without stopping, you have packed too much. Everything else gets stored, sold, or left behind.

The Non-Negotiable Documents

Without these documents, you cannot get a job, apply for benefits, enter most shelters, or prove who you are. Losing your ID while homeless is one of the worst things that can happen to you. Protect these like your life depends on them — because in many practical ways, it does.

  • Photo ID (driver’s license or state ID)
  • Social Security card (or at minimum, your number memorized)
  • Birth certificate (or a certified copy)
  • Medical records and prescription documentation
  • Any court documents (custody orders, restraining orders, probation paperwork)
  • Proof of income or last pay stubs
  • Insurance cards (health, auto)

Put originals in a waterproof bag. Zip-lock freezer bags work fine. Keep this bag on your body at all times — not in your backpack, on your body. A neck pouch or inside jacket pocket is ideal.

What to Do RIGHT NOW If You Do Not Have ID

Replacing identification documents after losing housing is catastrophically difficult. Without an address, without a birth certificate to get an ID, without an ID to get a birth certificate, you enter a bureaucratic loop that can stall your recovery for months.

  • Step 1: Request your birth certificate from your state of birth — this usually requires a mailed request and a fee ($10–$30).
  • Step 2: Use your birth certificate to get a state ID — some states accept shelter addresses as valid.
  • Step 3: With state ID, you can replace your Social Security card at any SSA office.
  • Step 4: Many cities have ID assistance programs through legal aid or homeless services — locate them now.
🎒 Gear Note: Essential Document Kit

Before you lose your address, assemble these in a single waterproof bag or zip-lock:

  • Photo ID, Social Security card, birth certificate
  • Medical records, prescription list, insurance cards
  • Any court or custody documents
  • Two passport-sized photos of yourself (shelters, agencies, and replacement ID applications need them)
  • A written list of important phone numbers (in case your phone dies permanently)

Photocopy everything. Store copies digitally (email them to yourself) and physically (with a trusted person). Originals in a waterproof bag on your person at all times.

Packing for Survival vs. Packing for Dignity

There are two layers to packing: what keeps you alive and what keeps you employable. You need both. The instinct is to pack everything. The reality is you can carry one bag comfortably, maybe two.

Think of it this way: survival gear gets you through tonight. Dignity gear gets you out of homelessness. You cannot skip either layer.

The Survival Layer

  • Weather-appropriate clothing (layers, not bulk)
  • Phone + charger + portable battery
  • Medications (30-day supply if possible)
  • Cash (withdraw what you can)
  • Hygiene basics: toothbrush, deodorant, wipes
  • Water bottle (refillable, durable)

The Dignity Layer

  • One set of clean, presentable clothes (for interviews, appointments, shelter intake)
  • Basic grooming: comb, razor, small mirror
  • A book, journal, or something that is yours — not survival, but sanity

Pack the dignity layer in a separate bag or a compression sack inside your main bag. Keep it clean and dry at all times. You will need it for job interviews, benefit appointments, and shelter intake.

Where to Store What You Cannot Carry

You own more than fits in a backpack. That does not mean you have to lose it all. Before you leave, figure out where the rest goes.

  • Storage units: $30–$80/month for a small unit. Worth it if you have furniture or valuables you will need when rehoused.
  • Trusted people: Family or friends who will hold boxes. Get agreement in writing or text.
  • Hidden caches: Last resort. Waterproof containers in secure, accessible locations. Risk of loss is high.

Complete Packing List by Priority Tier

Not every emergency gives you the same amount of time. Here is what to grab based on how much time you have.

Tier 1: Grab in 5 Minutes

The house is on fire, the locks are being changed, you are leaving a dangerous situation right now. Grab these and go.

  • Phone + charger
  • Wallet (ID, debit card, cash)
  • Medications you take daily
  • Keys (car, storage, anything you still need access to)
  • Jacket or coat (whatever is nearest the door)
  • Document bag if you already assembled one (see page 16)

Tier 2: Grab in 1 Hour

You have a little time. Add these to Tier 1.

  • All identity documents (ID, Social Security card, birth certificate, passport)
  • Portable battery bank + charging cables
  • One full change of clothes (including underwear, socks)
  • One set of presentable clothes (interview-ready)
  • Hygiene kit: toothbrush, toothpaste, deodorant, soap, razor, wet wipes
  • Water bottle (refillable)
  • Any cash you can find in the house
  • Medications (full supply, not just today’s dose)
  • Phone numbers written on paper
  • Glasses or contacts (with solution)

Tier 3: If You Have a Day

You know you are leaving tomorrow. Add these to Tiers 1 and 2.

  • Sleeping bag or blanket (compact if possible)
  • Small first aid kit (bandages, antiseptic, ibuprofen, antidiarrheal)
  • Rain gear (poncho or waterproof jacket)
  • Flashlight or headlamp
  • Multitool or pocket knife
  • Small padlock (for shelter lockers)
  • Laundry bag or compression sack
  • Notebook and pen
  • One comfort item (book, journal, small photo)
  • Earbuds or headphones
  • Snacks that do not spoil (granola bars, nuts, dried fruit)

Document Checklist: Where to Get Replacements

If you do not have these documents, here is exactly where to get them. Do this now. Every one of these gets harder to obtain after you lose housing.

  • Birth certificate: Request from the vital records office in the state where you were born. Most accept online or mail requests. Cost: $10–$30. Processing: 2–8 weeks. Website: cdc.gov/nchs/w2w/index.htm lists each state’s office.
  • Social Security card: Free replacement at any SSA office or online at ssa.gov/myaccount. You need a photo ID to request one. Limit: 3 replacements per year, 10 in a lifetime.
  • State ID or driver’s license: Visit your local DMV. Cost: $5–$35 depending on state. Bring birth certificate + Social Security card + proof of address (a shelter address works in most states). Some states offer free IDs for homeless individuals — ask.
  • DD-214 (veterans): Request through eVetRecs at archives.gov/veterans or call the National Personnel Records Center at 314-801-0800. Free. Processing: 10 days to several months.
  • Passport: Not critical for domestic survival, but if you have one, keep it. Replacement costs $130+.
  • Medical records: Call your doctor’s office and request a summary. Most will provide it free or for a small copy fee. Get your prescription list, diagnosis codes, and vaccination records.
  • Court documents: Custody orders, divorce decrees, probation paperwork — contact the clerk of court in the jurisdiction that issued them. Copies are usually $1–$5 per page.

How to Digitize Everything

Digital copies do not replace originals, but they are your backup when originals are lost, stolen, or destroyed. Do this before you lose access to a scanner or stable internet.

  • Scan or photograph every document listed above. Use your phone camera. Make sure the image is clear and legible.
  • Email them to yourself at a free email address (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo). Create a folder called “Documents.” This way you can access them from any device, anywhere.
  • Upload to Google Drive or iCloud. Free storage (15 GB on Google, 5 GB on iCloud). Create a folder and upload everything.
  • Store the password somewhere safe. Write your email password on the paper you keep in your document bag. If your phone dies, you can log in from a library computer.
  • Do not post documents on social media or share them in group chats. Identity theft compounds every problem you already have.
✔ Practical Tip

Take a photo of yourself in clean, presentable clothes and save it digitally. Some benefit applications, shelter intakes, and ID replacements require a photo. Having one ready saves time and avoids the stress of trying to look presentable in crisis.

Storage Options with Costs

You own more than fits in a backpack. Here are your options, ranked by cost and reliability.

  • Ask a friend or family member. Cost: free. Best option if available. Get agreement in writing via text or email — “Thanks for holding my boxes until I get back on my feet.” This creates a record.
  • Self-storage unit. A 5x5 unit (closet-sized) runs $30–$60/month. A 5x10 (half a garage) runs $50–$100/month. A 10x10 runs $75–$150/month. Public Storage, CubeSmart, and Life Storage are nationwide chains. Many offer the first month free or at $1.
  • Church or nonprofit storage programs. Some churches and homeless services organizations offer free temporary storage. Call 211 and ask specifically about storage assistance.
  • What fits in a car. If you have a vehicle, the trunk and back seat hold 2–4 boxes plus your bags. Use a roof rack or cargo carrier for more. Keep valuables hidden — a car full of visible belongings is a break-in target.
  • What to leave behind. Furniture, large appliances, cheap kitchenware, most books, most clothing. If it costs less than the storage unit, let it go. You can replace a $20 toaster. You cannot replace the $50/month you spent storing it.

What to Sell and Where

Sell things that are worth more as cash than as possessions. Start 2–3 weeks before you leave if possible. Desperate last-day sales get 50% less than planned sales.

What Retains Value

  • Power tools: 50–70% of retail. Sell on Facebook Marketplace or Craigslist.
  • Electronics: Laptops, tablets, gaming consoles. 40–60% of retail. Facebook Marketplace, OfferUp, Swappa (for phones).
  • Musical instruments: 40–60% of retail. Reverb.com for higher-end gear, Craigslist for quick local sales.
  • Jewelry: Gold and silver hold value. Pawn shops give 20–40% of melt value. Private sales through Facebook or Craigslist get more.
  • Vehicles: If you have a second car, sell it. Carvana, CarMax, or private sale. Private sales get 20–30% more than dealer trade-ins.

What Does Not Retain Value

  • Furniture: Almost worthless used. Give it away or leave it. Exception: solid wood or antique pieces.
  • Clothing: Thrift stores pay nothing. Consignment takes weeks. Donate or give to friends.
  • Books: Sell textbooks on Amazon. Everything else goes to a Little Free Library or donation bin.
  • Kitchen items: Donate. Nobody buys used pots on Marketplace.

Where to Sell

  • Facebook Marketplace: Best for local sales. Free to list. Meet in public places.
  • OfferUp: Similar to Marketplace. Good for electronics and furniture.
  • Craigslist: Still works for tools, cars, and higher-value items. Meet at a police station parking lot for safety.
  • Pawn shops: Expect 20–40% of value. Fast cash, no waiting for a buyer. Bring ID — pawn shops are required to record transactions.
  • Decluttr / Gazelle: Mail-in buyback for phones, tablets, and gaming consoles. Lower prices but zero effort.

Phone Setup: Your Most Important Survival Tool

Your phone is not a luxury. It is your connection to jobs, benefits, shelter, maps, and people. Protect it like the survival tool it is.

Free and Low-Cost Phone Service

  • Lifeline program: Federal subsidy of $9.25/month toward phone or internet service. You qualify if you receive SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, Federal Public Housing Assistance, or Veterans Pension. Apply at lifelinesupport.org or through your carrier.
  • Assurance Wireless: Free phone and free monthly service (unlimited talk/text, data varies by state). Enrollment through assurancewireless.com. You need proof of benefit enrollment or income below 135% of federal poverty level.
  • SafeLink Wireless: Similar to Assurance. Free phone and service. Check safelinkwireless.com.
  • Cheap prepaid plans: If you do not qualify for free service, Mint Mobile starts at $15/month. Tello starts at $5/month for minimal use. Visible offers unlimited for $25/month.

Essential Apps to Install Now

  • Google Maps — Download offline maps of your city (Settings → Offline Maps → Select Your Own Map). Works without internet.
  • findhelp.org (formerly Aunt Bertha) — Search by zip code for food, shelter, health, employment, and financial assistance programs near you.
  • Gmail or Outlook — Free email. Required for job applications, benefit accounts, and communication.
  • Your bank’s app — Check balance, transfer funds, find ATMs.
  • Indeed or LinkedIn — Job searching from your phone.
  • Notes app — Built into every phone. Use it to track service providers, schedules, and your plan.
  • Camera — Document everything: your apartment condition at move-out, receipts, forms you fill out.

Battery Conservation

  • Buy a portable battery bank: 10,000 mAh ($15–$25) gives you 2–3 full charges. 20,000 mAh ($25–$40) gives you 4–5.
  • Turn off Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, and location services when not actively using them.
  • Set screen brightness to minimum readable level.
  • Close all background apps.
  • Enable low-power mode permanently.
  • Free charging spots: libraries, coffee shops (buy a $1 coffee), day centers, hospital waiting rooms, airport terminals, some parks and bus stations with USB outlets.
Key Takeaways
  • Documents first. ID replacement is the single hardest logistics problem in homelessness. Secure originals and digital copies now.
  • Pack by tier. Know what to grab in 5 minutes, 1 hour, and 1 day.
  • Digitize everything. Email yourself photos of every document. Accessible from any device.
  • Store what you cannot carry. Friends are free. Storage units are $30–$60/month. If it costs less than storage, sell it.
  • Sell strategically. Tools and electronics hold value. Furniture and clothes do not. Start selling 2 weeks early.
  • Protect your phone. Apply for free service (Lifeline, Assurance Wireless). Buy a battery bank. Download offline maps.

Storage Unit Survival Guide

If you have a storage unit, you need to understand exactly what happens when you stop paying. Most facilities auction your belongings after 30 to 60 days of missed payments. State lien sale laws govern the exact timeline, and it varies. But the clock starts the day your payment is late. Not the day they notify you. The day you miss.

The Notification Trap

Storage facilities are required to notify you by certified letter before selling your belongings. They send it to the address on file. If you moved and did not update your address with the facility, you will not get the notice. You will find out your stuff is gone when you show up to a unit with a new lock on it. Update your mailing address with every storage facility every time it changes. Use a shelter address, a PO Box, or General Delivery — anything you actually check.

Before You Miss a Payment

Call the facility. Do it before the due date, not after. Explain your situation. Many managers will grant 2 to 4 weeks of grace. Some will negotiate a temporarily reduced rate. Some will let you downsize to a smaller unit at a lower price. Being proactive works. Managers deal with abandonment constantly. A tenant who calls and communicates is a tenant they want to keep. A tenant who goes silent is a tenant whose unit gets auctioned.

If You Are Going to Lose the Unit

Go immediately. Do not wait. Pull out everything irreplaceable: identity documents, photos, sentimental items, anything worth more than the cost of the unit itself. Do this before the lock gets cut. Once the facility puts their lock on your door, you lose access. Everything inside becomes their property under lien sale law. Get in there while you still can and grab what matters most.

Alternatives to Paid Storage

  • A trusted friend's garage. Free. The best option if available. Get agreement in writing via text.
  • Church storage programs. Some churches and nonprofits offer free temporary storage. Call 211 and ask specifically about storage assistance in your area.
  • Your car trunk. Limited space, but free. Keep valuables hidden and locked.

The rule of thumb: only use paid storage for items worth more than 3 months of rent on the unit. If your unit costs $60/month and the contents are worth $100, you are losing money every two months. Sell the contents, cancel the unit, and put that $60/month toward something that gets you housed.

The Auto-Pay Trap

If you set up auto-pay on a credit or debit card and that card expires, gets declined, or gets cancelled, you may not realize payments are failing. Late fees accumulate. The lien process starts. You think you are covered because you set up auto-pay months ago. You are not. Check your storage account monthly. Verify the payment went through. A 5-minute check prevents a $2,000 loss.
