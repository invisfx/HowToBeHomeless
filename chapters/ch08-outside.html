Chapter 8

Outside

Tent camping, rough sleeping, and surviving without walls.

Sleeping outside is the version of homelessness that most people picture. It is also the most dangerous. Exposure kills more homeless people each year than violence, drugs, or disease. If you are sleeping outside — whether by choice or because every other option has closed — your number one job is managing the environment that is trying to kill you.

This is not camping. Recreational campers choose their location, bring the right gear, and go home when the weather turns bad. You do not have those options. What you do have is the ability to learn the skills that keep you alive, find the locations that tolerate your presence, and build the systems that make outdoor sleeping survivable for as long as it takes.

The biggest mistake new rough sleepers make is treating their situation as temporary and refusing to invest in doing it properly. They sleep in doorways with a blanket instead of finding a real spot and setting up correctly. They skip ground insulation because they think they will be inside by next week. That mindset gets people hypothermic, robbed, or arrested.

Accept where you are. Set up properly. Learn the rules of the area you are in. The faster you adapt, the safer you will be and the more energy you will have to work on getting off the street.

This chapter covers urban camping, rural camping, encampment dynamics, the post-Grants Pass legal landscape, and how to survive a sweep. Read it before your first night outside. It could save your life.

Urban Camping

Urban camping means sleeping outside within city limits. Every city handles it differently. Some have designated areas where camping is tolerated or even sanctioned. Others enforce anti-camping laws aggressively. Your first task is figuring out which kind of city you are in.

Where It Is Tolerated

Enforcement varies block by block, not just city by city. Talk to other homeless people in the area. They know which spots are safe and which ones get swept. Outreach workers can also tell you where enforcement is light.

  • Under highway overpasses and bridges (visibility varies by city enforcement)
  • Wooded areas within city limits — parks, greenbelts, rail corridors
  • Industrial areas with low foot traffic after business hours
  • Near 24-hour establishments where your presence is less conspicuous

Where It Gets You Arrested

Downtown commercial districts, residential neighborhoods, school zones, and public parks after posted closing times are high-enforcement areas in most cities. Private property is always a trespassing risk. If a security guard or owner tells you to move, move. An argument turns a warning into an arrest.

Camp Setup

Your camp setup determines whether you sleep or suffer. Get this right from the first night. You do not need expensive gear. You need the right priorities.

What Matters

  • Ground insulation: More important than the sleeping bag. Cold ground will drain your body heat faster than cold air. A foam pad or even flattened cardboard makes a critical difference.
  • Water management: Tarp above, tarp below. Rain gets in everywhere. Slope your overhead tarp so water runs off, not pools.
  • Sleeping bag rating: Buy for 20 degrees below the lowest temperature you expect. Ratings are optimistic.

What Does Not Matter

Brand names, tent color, ultralight gear marketed to backpackers — none of it matters in this context. Durability and warmth are everything. A $30 sleeping bag from a surplus store may outperform a $200 ultralight one. Check thrift stores, Craigslist free listings, and ask at shelters for donated gear.

♟ The Game: Camp Politics — Repeated Games and Cooperation

An encampment is a repeated game: the same players interact daily, which changes optimal strategy. In a one-shot interaction, defection (theft, freeloading) may pay. In a repeated game, tit-for-tat dominates — cooperate first, then mirror what others do. Camps that survive develop informal enforcement: shared watch schedules, contribution norms, expulsion of bad actors. When joining a camp, your first move should be cooperation (offer to watch someone's stuff, share a resource). This establishes reciprocity. But also: be willing to leave camps where defection is the norm. You cannot fix a broken equilibrium alone.

The Grants Pass Reality

The 2024 Supreme Court decision in City of Grants Pass v. Johnson changed everything for outdoor sleeping. Before this ruling, cities could not criminalize sleeping outside if they lacked adequate shelter beds. That protection is gone. Cities now have broader authority to enforce anti-camping ordinances even when shelter beds are unavailable.

This does not mean every city will crack down. Some cities still choose not to enforce aggressively. But the legal shield that protected outdoor sleepers is gone. Knowing your local enforcement climate is more critical than ever. Check with legal aid organizations in your area for current local policies.

Encampment Dynamics

Joining an Existing Camp

Encampments have social structures, whether formal or not. Approach with respect. Ask who runs things. Offer something — a skill, a watch shift, shared supplies. Do not set up without introduction. Walking in and pitching a tent without talking to anyone first is how conflicts start.

Going Solo

Solo camping is safer from interpersonal conflict but more dangerous from exposure, theft, and lack of backup. No one watches your stuff while you use the restroom. No one notices if you get sick in the night. The trade-off is real and personal. Weigh it based on your specific situation and the specific people around you.

Rural and Wilderness Camping

There is a hidden population of people living in national forests, BLM land, and rural wooded areas. This strategy offers solitude and freedom from enforcement, but it also means distance from services, jobs, and medical care. If you choose this route, understand the trade-off clearly: peace and privacy in exchange for isolation from everything that helps you get housed again.

  • BLM land: Free camping is often legal for up to 14 days in one spot. Then you must move.
  • National forests: Similar dispersed camping rules. Check local ranger district regulations.
  • Private land: Only with permission. Trespassing charges add to your problems.

The further you go from population centers, the harder it becomes to access food, water, and the services that could help you exit homelessness. Rural camping works best as a short-term strategy or for people with vehicles. On foot, you need to stay close enough to a town to walk for supplies.

The Sweep

A sweep is when the city clears your camp. It will happen. It is not a question of if, but when. When it happens, you lose everything you cannot carry in one trip. Knowing how sweeps work lets you prepare instead of panic.

  • Warning signs: Posted notices (usually 48–72 hours), increased police or outreach worker visits, media attention on your area.
  • Preparation: Keep a go-bag packed at all times. Documents, phone, medications, and cash should never leave your person.
  • After the sweep: Your belongings may be stored for 60–90 days (varies by city). Get the claim information if offered. Then find your next spot.
⚠ Warning

Never confront sweep crews or police during a clearing operation. Resistance escalates to arrest, and arrest means losing even more. Comply, document, and relocate. Fight it afterward through legal aid if your rights were violated.

Urban Camping: Site Selection

Where you sleep is the most important decision you make each day. A good site lets you rest. A bad site gets you robbed, arrested, or sick. Here is what to look for and what to avoid.

What Makes a Good Spot

  • Hidden from casual view. You do not need deep cover. You need to be invisible to people who are not looking for you. Behind a wall, in a tree line, in a recessed area below street level. If a person walking past at normal speed would not notice you, the spot works.
  • Not on private property. Trespassing charges complicate everything. Stick to public land or land with ambiguous ownership. Railroad property is federal — trespassing there is a federal offense. Avoid it.
  • Away from foot traffic. Walking paths, dog parks, and jogging trails bring people past your spot at all hours. You want a location where nobody has a reason to walk.
  • Near water. A stream, public fountain, or park restroom within a 10-minute walk. You need water for drinking, cooking, and cleaning. Carrying water long distances drains energy you do not have.
  • Near services but not too close. You want to be within 20 minutes of a bus stop, a food pantry, or a social services office. But do not camp on the doorstep of these places. Too close draws enforcement and competition for the spot.
  • On high ground. Water flows downhill. Rain collects in low spots. A depression that looks dry on a clear day becomes a puddle in a storm. Camp above the terrain around you, not in a dip.
  • Two exit routes. Never camp somewhere with only one way out. If your exit is blocked — by a person, an animal, a vehicle — you are trapped. Two paths out is minimum.

What Makes a Bad Spot

  • Flood zones. Under bridges near rivers or creeks. Storm channels. Dry riverbeds. People die every year when water rises while they sleep. If you see high-water marks on walls, you are in a flood zone. Leave.
  • Under bridges with traffic overhead. Exhaust fumes settle downward. Road debris falls. The noise makes it impossible to hear someone approaching. Bridges also attract other people — not all of them safe.
  • Near train tracks. Trains are quieter than you think until they are on top of you. Vibration, noise, and the risk of falling asleep too close to the rails. Also federal property with regular patrols.
  • Steep slopes. Sliding downhill in your sleep is not fun. Rolling gear is worse. If you camp on a slope, you will shift all night and wake exhausted.
  • Near known drug markets. Police patrol these areas frequently. The people there are not your allies. Violence is more common. Your gear will be stolen.

Camp Setup: Step by Step

Here is the exact sequence for setting up a survivable outdoor camp. Do it this way every time.

  1. Clear the ground. Remove rocks, sticks, and sharp objects from your sleeping area. A rock under your hip at 2am will keep you awake all night.
  2. Ground tarp first. Lay a plastic tarp or heavy-duty trash bag on the ground. This blocks moisture from wicking up into your bedding. Fold the edges under so rain does not collect on top of the tarp and run underneath you.
  3. Sleeping pad next. Closed-cell foam pad, $15 at Walmart or any outdoor store. This is your insulation from the ground. The ground will steal your body heat faster than the air. A pad rated R-2 or higher is the minimum. Cardboard works in an emergency — use four to six layers.
  4. Sleeping bag on the pad. Buy a bag rated 20 degrees below the lowest temperature your area reaches. Manufacturer ratings are optimistic by 10 to 15 degrees. A bag rated to 20°F actually keeps you comfortable at about 35°F. In winter in northern states, you need a 0°F or -20°F bag. Check thrift stores — sleeping bags are commonly donated.
  5. Rain tarp overhead. String a tarp between two trees or use trekking poles and rope. Angle it so water runs off, not pools. The tarp should extend at least a foot past your sleeping area on all sides. Wind-driven rain comes in sideways. Stake the windward side lower. Leave the leeward side open for ventilation.
  6. Gear organized and packed for fast exit. Keep everything in or on your bag. If you need to leave in 30 seconds, you grab the bag and go. Nothing should be scattered around the camp that you cannot afford to lose. Practice the pack-up in your head before you fall asleep.

Grants Pass: What It Actually Means for You

In June 2024, the Supreme Court ruled in City of Grants Pass v. Johnson that cities can enforce anti-camping laws even when there are no available shelter beds. Before this ruling, the Ninth Circuit's Martin v. Boise decision said that punishing someone for sleeping outside when no shelter existed was cruel and unusual punishment. That protection covered the entire western United States. It is gone now.

Here is what this means in practice:

  • Cities CAN ticket, fine, or arrest you for sleeping, sitting, or lying in public spaces, even if every shelter in the city is full.
  • Not all cities are enforcing. Many cities have chosen not to change their policies. Some are still following the spirit of Martin v. Boise even though they are no longer legally required to. Your city's stance matters more than the Supreme Court ruling.
  • Check your city's current policy. Call 211 or your local legal aid office and ask: "Is the city enforcing anti-camping ordinances? Where? When?" Enforcement often varies by neighborhood, time of day, and political cycle.
  • Fines compound. A $50 camping ticket you cannot pay becomes a $200 fine, then a warrant. If you are ticketed, contact legal aid immediately. Many cities have diversion programs for homeless individuals that dismiss tickets if you engage with services.
  • Documentation matters. If you are ticketed or arrested for sleeping outside and no shelter bed was available, document that fact. Write down the date, time, location, and that you attempted to access shelter. Legal challenges to post-Grants Pass enforcement are already in progress, and documented cases help.

Encampment Dynamics: The Full Picture

How to Join an Existing Camp

Encampments are not open-access public spaces. They are communities with norms, hierarchies, and boundaries. Walk in wrong and you will be told to leave — or worse.

  1. Approach during daylight. Never walk into an unfamiliar camp at night. You will frighten people and the response may be hostile.
  2. Ask who runs things. Every camp has a leader, whether official or not. Find that person and introduce yourself. Say where you have been sleeping and why you are looking for a new spot.
  3. Bring something to share. A pack of cigarettes, a jug of water, food, a lighter. It does not need to be expensive. The gesture signals cooperation.
  4. Offer to contribute. Watch shifts, trash pickup, sharing a tarp, keeping the area clean. Camps that maintain order last longer between sweeps. People who contribute are welcomed. People who consume without contributing are pushed out.
  5. Set up where they tell you. Do not pick the best-looking spot. Take what is offered. You earn a better spot over time.
  6. Be consistent. Show up every night. Keep your area clean. Follow the camp's norms. Trust takes time. Transient visitors are tolerated. Consistent members are protected.

How Camps Self-Govern

Camps that survive develop informal rules. Common ones: no stealing within the camp, no hard drug use in common areas, no inviting outsiders without clearing it, quiet after a certain hour, everyone contributes to cleanup. Violation of camp norms is handled by the group — warnings, then exclusion. This is not formal justice. It is survival governance. Respect it.

When to Leave a Camp

  • Drug activity is increasing. When harder drugs move into a camp, police attention follows. If meth or fentanyl use becomes open, enforcement action is coming. Leave before the sweep.
  • Violence is escalating. One fight can be an anomaly. Regular violence means the social structure has broken down. Your risk of being a victim increases with each incident.
  • A sweep is imminent. Posted notices, increased outreach visits, media coverage of the area — these are warnings. When you see them, start packing.
  • You do not feel safe. Trust your instincts. If something feels wrong, move. You can always find another spot. You cannot undo an assault or a theft.

Surviving a Sweep

A sweep is coming. It is always coming. The only question is when. Here is how to prepare so you lose as little as possible.

Warning Signs

  • Posted notices. Most cities are required to give 48 to 72 hours notice before a sweep. Look for notices on poles, fences, and trees near your camp. They are often in small print. Check daily.
  • Increased police or outreach visits. When outreach workers start visiting more frequently and asking about your "housing plan," a sweep is being planned. Outreach before a sweep is common — it gives the city cover to say they offered services before they cleared the camp.
  • Media attention. A news van in your area is a countdown timer. Once media covers an encampment, political pressure to clear it accelerates.
  • City council meetings. If your camp is near a residential area and neighbors are complaining, check your city council agenda. Encampment cleanups are often announced in public meetings before the notices go up.

What to Pack First

When a sweep is imminent, assume you will lose everything you cannot carry in one trip. Pack in this priority order:

  1. Documents. ID, Social Security card, birth certificate, any benefit paperwork. These should already be on your person at all times.
  2. Phone and charger. Your connection to services, people, and information.
  3. Medications. Prescription bottles with your name on them. Losing medications mid-prescription is a medical emergency.
  4. Cash. Whatever you have.
  5. Sleeping bag and pad. You will need to sleep somewhere tonight.
  6. Weather protection. Tarp, rain gear, warm layers.
  7. Everything else is replaceable. Let it go. Tents, cookware, extra clothing — they can be replaced. Your documents cannot.

Where Your Stuff Goes

Confiscated belongings are usually taken to a city storage facility. You have 60 to 90 days to claim them, depending on your jurisdiction. Ask the workers during the sweep where items are being taken. Get an address, a phone number, and a claim process. Some cities require you to identify your belongings within a certain window. Others let you pick up anything from the sweep. This varies — ask.

Your Rights During a Sweep

  • You have the right to collect your belongings before they are removed, if you are present and the notice period was given.
  • You have the right to not be assaulted by police or city workers. If force is used against you while you are complying, that may be excessive force. Document it.
  • You have the right to record. Take video on your phone if you can do so safely. This is protected by the First Amendment.
  • You do not have the right to resist. Do not chain yourself to a tree. Do not refuse to move. Comply, document, and fight it later through legal channels.
  • Contact legal aid after the sweep. The ACLU, your local legal aid society, and homeless advocacy organizations track sweep practices and may pursue legal action on behalf of individuals whose rights were violated.
⚠ Warning: Illegal Sweeps

Some sweeps happen without the legally required notice. Some cities destroy belongings instead of storing them. If this happened to you, contact the National Law Center on Homelessness & Poverty (nlchp.org) or your local ACLU chapter. Even after Grants Pass, cities still have legal obligations around how they conduct sweeps.

Weather: The Thing That Kills You

More homeless people die of exposure than any other cause. You need to take weather seriously every single day.

Cold Weather Survival

  • Layering system: Base layer (moisture-wicking, not cotton), insulating layer (fleece or wool), outer layer (wind and rain protection). Cotton kills — it absorbs moisture and loses all insulation value when wet.
  • Ground insulation is king. You lose more heat to the ground than to the air. Two pads are better than one. Cardboard under your pad adds another layer. Sleep on everything warm. Stuff extra clothing into your sleeping bag around your feet.
  • Hot water bottle. Fill a Nalgene or any sturdy water bottle with boiling water. Put it in your sleeping bag 10 minutes before you get in. It radiates heat for hours. Wrap it in a sock so it does not burn you.
  • Eat before sleeping. Your body generates heat by digesting food. A high-calorie meal before bed — peanut butter, oatmeal, anything with fat and carbohydrates — keeps your furnace running through the night.
  • Warming centers. When temperatures drop below 32°F, most cities activate warming centers. Call 211 to find them. Use them. Pride does not keep you warm.

Hot Weather Survival

  • Hydration. Drink water before you feel thirsty. By the time you feel thirst, you are already dehydrated. Carry at least two liters at all times in summer.
  • Shade. Direct sun can raise your body temperature dangerously fast. Find shade for midday hours. Libraries, malls, and cooling centers are free air conditioning.
  • Wet bandana. Soak a bandana in water and wear it around your neck. Evaporative cooling drops your skin temperature several degrees. Re-wet it every 30 minutes.
  • Cooling centers. When temperatures exceed 95°F, most cities open cooling centers. Same as warming centers — call 211.

Key Takeaways
  • Site selection saves your life. High ground, hidden, two exits, near water, not on private property.
  • Ground insulation first. A pad under you matters more than a bag over you.
  • Set up for fast exit. Everything in or on your bag. 30-second pack-up.
  • Post-Grants Pass: Cities can enforce, but not all do. Know your city's stance.
  • Cooperate first in camps. Bring something to share. Contribute. Earn your spot.
  • Prepare for sweeps. Documents, phone, meds on your body at all times. Know the claim process.
  • Weather kills. Layer correctly, insulate from the ground, eat before sleeping, use warming/cooling centers.
