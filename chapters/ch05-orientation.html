Chapter 5

Orientation

Learning the landscape of your new reality.

You survived night one. Now you need to learn how your new world works. Homelessness has its own geography, its own schedules, its own social rules, and its own power structures. The faster you learn the landscape, the faster you stop feeling lost and start operating effectively.

Think of the first 72 hours as basic training. You are not solving the big problems yet — housing, income, stability. You are learning where things are, who controls access, and how to move through the system without wasting time or burning bridges.

Everything feels overwhelming right now because everything is new. That feeling is temporary. Within a few days, you will know where to eat, where to charge your phone, where to shower, and where to sleep. The chaos is not the new normal — the learning curve is.

The people who get stuck in homelessness are often the ones who never orient. They bounce from crisis to crisis, never mapping the terrain, never building a routine. The people who get out are the ones who treat the first week like a job: show up, learn the system, build a schedule.

This chapter gives you the framework. Map your resources, learn the rules, find the right people, and build a daily routine. Do this in the first 72 hours and you will be operating at a level most people do not reach for weeks.

How To Be Homeless Chapter 5: Orientation

The First 72 Hours as a Learning Sprint

You do not know what you do not know. That is the most dangerous thing about your first few days without housing. Resources exist that you have never heard of, and the only way to find them is to actively look.

Treat the first three days as a reconnaissance mission. Your job is not to solve everything — it is to map the terrain. Every hour you spend learning the landscape now saves you days of stumbling later.

  • Map services: Where is the nearest shelter, food bank, day center, library, clinic?
  • Map routes: How do you get between these places? What bus lines connect them?
  • Map safe zones: Where can you sit undisturbed during the day? Where can you sleep with relative safety?
  • Map charging spots: Libraries, coffee shops, day centers, malls — where can you plug in?
  • Map food schedules: Which churches serve meals on which days? When does the food bank open?

Write all of this down. A map is useless if it is only in your head. Use your phone's notes app or a small notebook. Include addresses, hours, bus routes, and phone numbers. This list is your operating system.

How Homeless Infrastructure Actually Works

There is an entire system of services designed for people in your situation. It is underfunded and overcrowded, but it exists. Here is what the pieces are and what each one actually does.

  • Shelters: Provide a bed and usually a meal. Most have rules, curfews, and intake processes. Capacity is limited. See Chapter 6 (p. 63)
  • Day centers: Open during daytime hours. Offer showers, laundry, mail service, case management, phone charging. Often the most useful resource.
  • Outreach teams: Mobile workers who come to you. They can connect you to services, provide supplies, and help with intake. Look for them at known gathering spots.
  • Food infrastructure: Food banks (groceries to take), soup kitchens (sit-down meals), community fridges (24/7 access), church meals (usually specific days/times).
  • Clinics: Federally Qualified Health Centers cannot turn you away. Many day centers have on-site nurse practitioners.

The Survival Routine: Your Daily Schedule

Without a routine, your days will dissolve into aimless wandering. That sounds dramatic, but it happens to almost everyone. Routine is the single most important thing you can build in your first week. Here is an exact template.

6:00 AM — Wake

If you are in a shelter, check-out is usually 6–8 AM. Pack your bag. Take everything with you. Leave nothing behind.

6:30 AM — Hygiene

Day center shower, gym (Planet Fitness has a $10/month plan with showers in most locations), public restroom sink wash. Brush teeth, apply deodorant. Looking clean is not vanity — it is your ticket to being treated like a person at every appointment today.

7:00 AM — Breakfast

Shelter meal, church breakfast program, food from your bag. Eat something. Your brain does not work on an empty stomach.

8:00 AM — Charge Phone + Job Search

Go to the library. Plug in your phone. Use the computer to check job listings (Indeed, Craigslist jobs, local staffing agencies). Apply for 3–5 jobs. Update your email. Check for messages from caseworkers or benefit offices.

10:00 AM — Appointments and Benefits

This is when offices are open. DSS, SNAP office, Medicaid, unemployment office, Legal Aid. Schedule your critical appointments for morning hours when your energy and focus are highest.

12:00 PM — Lunch

Soup kitchen, day center meal, food bank groceries you prepared. Know which locations serve lunch and their hours.

1:00 PM — Afternoon Tasks

Follow up on job applications by phone. Visit day centers for case management. Pick up mail. Handle any errands that require being in a specific place during business hours.

3:00 PM — Rest and Maintenance

Library, park, day center. Read. Do laundry if you have access ($2–$4 at a laundromat). Organize your bag. Charge battery bank.

5:00 PM — Dinner

Church meal, soup kitchen, food bank groceries. Know the evening food schedule in your area.

6:00 PM — Secure Your Sleeping Spot

If shelter: get in line. Most shelters fill up by 6–7 PM. Do not be late. If car: drive to your parking spot before dark. If outside: reach your spot with enough daylight to set up safely.

9:00 PM — Sleep

You need sleep. Set an alarm. Tomorrow is another working day.

Mapping Your City

In your first 72 hours, you need to build a personal map of your survival infrastructure. This is not optional. Write it down in your phone or on paper. Here is what to map.

Free Wi-Fi Locations

  • Public libraries (best option — free Wi-Fi, computers, outlets, restrooms, quiet space)
  • McDonald’s, Starbucks, Panera Bread (free Wi-Fi, outlets, restrooms)
  • Community centers and recreation centers
  • Some parks and public plazas (check your city’s website)
  • Day centers and shelter lobbies

Public Restrooms

  • Libraries (best — clean, accessible during hours)
  • Fast food restaurants (walk in with confidence — most do not require a purchase)
  • Gas stations (varies — some require a key from the counter)
  • Parks and recreation areas
  • Hospital lobbies
  • Shopping malls

Charging Outlets

  • Libraries (many have USB charging stations now)
  • Coffee shops (buy a $1 coffee for access)
  • Day centers
  • Hospital waiting rooms
  • Airport terminals
  • Some bus stops and train stations have USB outlets
  • Shopping malls (check near seating areas and food courts)

Food Schedule

Build a weekly chart. Which organization feeds on which day and at what time. Example:

  • Monday: First Baptist Church, lunch 11:30 AM. Salvation Army, dinner 5:30 PM.
  • Tuesday: Catholic Charities, breakfast 7 AM. St. Mark’s, dinner 6 PM.
  • Wednesday: Community food bank, open 9 AM–2 PM (bring bags).

Call 211 to get the full food schedule for your area. Most cities have a printed guide available at day centers and libraries.

Water

  • Public water fountains (parks, libraries, recreation centers)
  • Fast food soda fountains (most have water cups available)
  • Day centers
  • Carry a refillable water bottle at all times. Dehydration impairs thinking and physical function.

Who to Approach and Who to Avoid

The people around you fall into two categories: those who can help your situation and those who will make it worse. Learning to tell the difference quickly is one of the most important skills you can develop.

Staff and Service Providers

  • Caseworkers: Your most valuable contact. Be honest, be on time, bring your documents. They have limited bandwidth — make it easy for them to help you.
  • Shelter staff: Follow the rules, even when they seem arbitrary. Staff who like you will advocate for you. Staff who do not will make your life harder.
  • Outreach workers: Actively looking for people who need help. Accept their cards and follow up.

Other Homeless People

  • Observe first. Spend a day watching the social dynamics before trusting anyone.
  • Look for competence. The person who has clean clothes, keeps their area organized, and knows the meal schedule is a better source of information than the person who offers you something for free.
  • Be cautious with generosity. People who offer you things immediately often want something in return.
  • Be honest but not oversharing. “I just lost my place” is enough. You do not owe your life story to anyone.
  • Trade information, not possessions. The most valuable currency in homelessness is knowledge. Share what you know about food schedules and services. It builds goodwill without costing you anything.
♟ The Game: Signaling and First Impressions

Your first interactions in a shelter, camp, or day center set your position for weeks. Game theory calls this signaling — the information you broadcast through behavior, appearance, and language tells others whether you are a target, an ally, or someone to leave alone. You do not need to be aggressive. You need to be legible: calm, aware, and not visibly desperate. People who signal competence attract better allies and fewer predators. This is not about performing toughness. It is about not broadcasting vulnerability.

The Social Rules Nobody Tells You

Every environment has rules that nobody explains but everyone enforces. Break them and you lose access to critical resources.

How Shelters Actually Work

  • Line up early. Beds go to people who show up first. If intake starts at 5 PM, be there at 4:30.
  • Be polite to staff. They are overworked, underpaid, and they control whether you get a bed. “Thank you” goes further than you think.
  • Never leave belongings unattended. Take your bag to the bathroom. Sleep with it next to you. Use the locker if there is one and bring your own padlock.
  • Do not touch other people’s stuff. Ever. Not even to move it out of your way.
  • Do not make noise after lights out. People who disrupt sleep get removed.
  • Do not complain publicly about the food. Complaining about free food is the fastest way to become unpopular with staff and residents.
  • Do not get into arguments. Even if you are right, you will be the one asked to leave. Let it go.
  • Follow the rules exactly. Curfew means curfew. Check-out time means check-out time. If the rule says no food in the sleeping area, no food in the sleeping area.

How to Talk to Other Homeless People

  • Be honest about your situation. Do not pretend you are “just passing through” or “here by choice.” People see through it.
  • Do not brag about what you had before. Nobody cares about your old salary or your old apartment. It alienates people who have been out here longer.
  • Ask questions and listen. “Where do you usually eat?” “Is there a shower I can use?” “Which staff person should I talk to?”
  • Offer what you can. Information is the most valuable thing you can share. If you found a new food program or a job lead, pass it along.

Building Your Information Network

Different people know different things. Your job in the first week is to figure out who knows what and build a network of information sources.

  • Outreach workers know which programs have openings, which benefits you qualify for, and how to navigate the application process. They know the system. Find them at day centers, shelters, or call 211 and ask for street outreach.
  • Long-term homeless people know the physical landscape: where to sleep safely, which churches feed well, which cops are aggressive, which areas to avoid at night. Their knowledge is local and practical.
  • Shelter staff know waitlists, bed availability, and which programs are accepting new clients. They are the gatekeepers. Be the person they want to help.
  • Other recently homeless people are going through the same learning curve. They are your peers. Share what you learn and you will both benefit.
  • Librarians know community resources, job search tools, and local programs. Many libraries have social workers on staff specifically to help people in crisis. Ask at the reference desk.

Apps and Websites You Need Immediately

Install these on your phone today. Each one serves a specific survival function.

  • findhelp.org (formerly Aunt Bertha) — Search by zip code for food, shelter, health care, employment, financial assistance, and legal help. The most comprehensive free resource database.
  • Google Maps — Download offline maps of your city NOW (Settings → Offline Maps). Works without internet. Navigate to shelters, food banks, bus stops, day centers.
  • Indeed / LinkedIn — Job searching from your phone. Apply directly. Set up email alerts for new postings in your skill area.
  • Gmail or Outlook — Free email account. Required for everything: job applications, benefit accounts, communication with caseworkers.
  • Notes app (built-in) — Your external brain. Track service providers, phone numbers, meal schedules, appointment dates, ideas, and your daily plan.
  • Camera (built-in) — Document everything. Take photos of forms you fill out, receipts, your apartment condition at move-out, any interactions with police or landlords.
  • Your bank’s app — Check your balance, find free ATMs, set up direct deposit for benefits.
  • Shelterlistings.org — Bookmark this in your browser. Nationwide directory of emergency shelters, searchable by state and city.

Websites to Bookmark

  • 211.org — National social services directory. Search by zip code.
  • benefits.gov — Benefit finder tool. Answer questions to find every federal program you qualify for.
  • lawhelp.org — Find free legal aid by state. Critical for eviction defense, custody, benefits denials.
  • needymeds.org — Find free and discounted medications. Search by drug name.
  • goodrx.com — Compare pharmacy prices and get coupons. Can cut prescription costs 50–80%.
  • fns.usda.gov/snap — SNAP (food stamps) information and state application links.
✔ Practical Tip

Keep a small notebook or use your phone’s notes app to record: service provider names and numbers, meal schedules, appointment dates, and things that worked or did not. Your memory is unreliable under stress. Externalize everything.

Crisis Numbers: Keep These Accessible

Write these on a piece of paper and keep it in your document bag. If your phone dies, you need to know these from any phone.

  • 211 — Social services (shelter, food, utility assistance, referrals). 24/7 in most areas.
  • 911 — Immediate danger.
  • 988 — Suicide & Crisis Lifeline. Call or text. 24/7.
  • 1-800-799-7233 — National Domestic Violence Hotline. 24/7.
  • 1-800-662-4357 — SAMHSA Helpline. Substance abuse and mental health referrals. 24/7.
  • 1-800-786-2929 — National Runaway Safeline. For youth (under 21). 24/7.
  • 988, press 1 — Veterans Crisis Line. Or text 838255.
▶ Your Brain Right Now: The One-Week Mark

After about a week, the acute stress response begins to subside and is replaced by something harder to fight: depression and apathy. The adrenaline that kept you moving the first few days is gone. You feel heavy, hopeless, and unmotivated. This is normal. It is your nervous system downshifting from crisis mode to conservation mode. The antidote is routine and small wins. Every time you complete a task — apply for a benefit, find a food source, charge your phone — your brain gets a small dopamine hit that counteracts the depression. Stack small wins.

Key Takeaways
  • Build a daily routine. Wake, hygiene, food, charge, jobs, appointments, dinner, secure sleep spot. Structure is survival.
  • Map your city. Wi-Fi, restrooms, charging, food schedules, water. Write it all down.
  • Learn the social rules. Line up early. Be polite to staff. Never leave belongings. No arguments.
  • Build an information network. Outreach workers know programs. Long-term homeless know locations. Librarians know everything else.
  • Install the apps. findhelp.org, Google Maps (offline), Indeed, Gmail, Notes. These are your survival tools.
  • Signal competence. Calm, clean, aware. First impressions set your position for weeks.
  • Stack small wins. Every completed task fights the depression that comes after the adrenaline fades.

First Week Setup Checklist

Day 1–2: Immediate Needs

  • Find reliable food source (meal program, food bank, pantry)
  • Find reliable water source (fountain, library, day center)
  • Find charging station (library, McDonald’s, day center)
  • Identify nearest public restroom
  • Learn shelter check-in times and rules

Day 3–4: Services

  • Visit a day center — talk to staff, get oriented
  • Get on shelter waitlist(s) — apply to multiple
  • Apply for SNAP if not done (see Chapter 14)
  • Apply for Medicaid if not done
  • Get a caseworker assigned — call 211 or visit shelter

Day 5–7: Routine

  • Establish a daily schedule (wake, hygiene, food, charge, tasks, sleep)
  • Map your city: WiFi spots, outlets, restrooms, food schedules
  • Set up online job applications (Indeed, Snagajob)
  • Get a library card (free WiFi, computers, address for mail)
  • Connect with at least one outreach worker or peer
  • Start income: plasma, day labor, gig app, or panhandling
  • Write down your plan. Even 3 bullet points. Put it on your phone.
