Chapter 1

Triage

Assessing your situation, timeline, and options before you lose the roof.

You are about to lose your housing. Maybe you have a week. Maybe you have a month. Maybe it already happened and you are reading this in a parking lot. Whatever the timeline, this chapter is about one thing: making the best possible decisions with the time you have left.

Most people waste the window before homelessness on denial, panic, or both. They spend their last dollars trying to save a situation that is already lost. They burn through favors on emotional calls instead of strategic ones. They lose documents they will desperately need later.

Triage means deciding what matters most when you cannot save everything. In an emergency room, doctors do not treat every patient equally. They treat the most critical cases first. You need to think the same way about your life right now.

This chapter will walk you through the assessment: How much time do you actually have? What money moves should you make right now? Who are your real resources? And which type of homelessness fits your situation best?

None of this is comfortable. But every hour you spend thinking clearly now saves you days of scrambling later. The difference between people who recover from homelessness quickly and those who do not often comes down to what they did in this window — before they lost the roof.

How Much Time Do You Actually Have?

The single most important thing to figure out right now is your actual deadline. Not when you think it will happen. Not when your landlord said it would happen. The legal, enforceable deadline when you must be out.

Most people overestimate how fast eviction works. The legal process has steps, and each step takes time. Understanding these steps does not make eviction okay — it gives you days or weeks you did not know you had.

Eviction Timelines by State

Every state has its own eviction process, but the general sequence is the same everywhere. Here is what to expect and how long each step typically takes.

  • Notice to quit: Typically 3–30 days depending on your state and situation.
  • Court filing: After the notice period, your landlord files in court. This buys you 1–4 weeks.
  • Hearing date: You have the right to appear. Even if you cannot win, showing up often buys time.
  • Writ of possession: The final step. The sheriff gives you 24–72 hours. This is the hard deadline.

Use every day of this process. Do not move out early because it feels inevitable. Legal eviction takes time, and that time is yours. Search your state's legal aid website for specific timelines.

The moment you start fighting the eviction on emotional grounds rather than strategic ones, you have already lost the triage.

▶ Your Brain Right Now: The Scarcity Tax

Research by Mullainathan and Shafir (Harvard/Princeton) found that financial scarcity consumes cognitive bandwidth equivalent to losing 13 IQ points. You are not getting dumber — your brain is allocating resources to the crisis, leaving less for everything else. This is measurable and temporary, but it affects every decision you make right now.

The practical takeaway: Simplify decisions, write things down, do not trust your memory right now. Your brain is rationing under load, not failing.

Financial Triage

Stop paying bills in the order you are used to. You are now in survival mode, and that means spending money in order of what keeps you alive and functional — not what is due first.

  1. Pay for shelter last. Counterintuitive, but rent is the biggest bill and the slowest to enforce. Pay for food, phone, and transportation first.
  2. Keep your phone alive. Your phone is your connection to jobs, services, people, and information.
  3. Stockpile ID documents. Get copies of everything while you still have an address and a printer.
  4. Cancel recurring charges. Subscriptions, memberships, auto-payments. Every dollar counts now.

This is not about being irresponsible. This is about recognizing that your old payment priorities no longer apply. Every dollar you spend now has to earn its place in your survival budget.

Financial Triage Priority List

Here is the exact order you should spend money right now. Every dollar needs to justify its place. If it does not keep you alive, employed, or connected, stop paying it.

  1. Food. You cannot think or function hungry. Budget $5–$10/day maximum. Rice, beans, peanut butter, bread. Food banks are free — find them now.
  2. Phone service. Your $15–$25/month prepaid plan keeps you connected to jobs, 211, shelters, and people. This is non-negotiable. If you qualify for SNAP or Medicaid, apply for the Lifeline program ($9.25/month subsidy) or Assurance Wireless (free phone and service).
  3. Medications. If you take daily medication, get a 90-day supply now. Ask your doctor for generic alternatives. GoodRx can cut prescription costs 50–80%. Walmart has a $4 generic list. Running out of psych meds or insulin on the street is a medical emergency.
  4. Transportation. Gas if you have a car. A bus pass if you do not. You need to get to appointments, jobs, and services. A monthly bus pass ($30–$75) is cheaper than rideshares.
  5. Hygiene basics. Dollar store: toothbrush, toothpaste, deodorant, soap, wet wipes. Under $10 total. Looking presentable keeps you employable.
  6. Storage unit. Only if you have belongings worth more than the monthly cost. A 5x5 unit runs $30–$60/month. If your stuff is worth less than three months of storage fees, sell it.

What to Stop Paying Immediately

  • Rent. If eviction is inevitable, stop paying. The legal process takes weeks to months. Use that money for survival.
  • Credit cards. A hit to your credit score means nothing when you are sleeping outside. Minimum payments on cards you will never pay off are wasted money.
  • Subscriptions. Netflix, Spotify, gym memberships, meal kits, cloud storage, app subscriptions. Cancel every single one today. That $150/month in subscriptions is two weeks of food.
  • Insurance premiums. Car insurance is the exception if you have a vehicle (driving uninsured creates legal problems). Health insurance — switch to Medicaid. Renter’s insurance — cancel.
  • Student loans. Apply for income-driven repayment or forbearance. Federal loans will not garnish you if you file the right paperwork. Call your servicer today.

The 30-Day Countdown

If you have 30 days of warning, you have time to set yourself up dramatically better than someone who finds out today. Do not waste this window. Here is your week-by-week checklist.

Week 1: Paperwork and Benefits

  • Gather all identity documents (ID, Social Security card, birth certificate, passport). Photocopy everything. Email copies to yourself.
  • Apply for SNAP (food stamps) online — processing takes 7–30 days, so file today.
  • Apply for Medicaid if you do not have health coverage. In expansion states, approval can be same-day.
  • File for unemployment if you lost your job. File the day you are laid off, not later. Every day you wait is money you lose.
  • Call 211 and explain your situation. Ask for a caseworker referral.
  • Contact your local Legal Aid office about your eviction. They often provide free representation.

Week 2: Money and Belongings

  • Cancel every subscription and recurring charge.
  • Sell high-value items you cannot carry: electronics, tools, furniture. Price them 20–30% below market for fast sales on Facebook Marketplace.
  • Withdraw cash in stages. Do not drain your account to zero — keep enough for a bank account to stay open (usually $25).
  • Arrange storage for items you want to keep. A trusted friend’s garage is free. A 5x5 storage unit is $30–$60/month.
  • Get a 90-day supply of any medications.
  • Get your car serviced if you have one. Oil change, tire check, brake check. Your car may become your home.

Week 3: Scouting and Connections

  • Visit shelters in person. Learn intake procedures, hours, rules. Ask about waitlists.
  • Locate food banks, soup kitchens, day centers. Write down addresses and schedules.
  • Establish a mailing address: USPS General Delivery (free), PO Box ($20–$50 for 6 months), or a shelter that accepts mail.
  • Tell the people who need to know. Use the scripts in Chapter 3 (p. 27).
  • Apply for the Lifeline phone program if you qualify.

Week 4: Final Preparations

  • Pack your go-bag (see Chapter 2, p. 15).
  • Clean your apartment for move-out inspection if applicable. Take photos of the condition for security deposit disputes.
  • Forward your mail to your new mailing address via USPS.
  • Download offline maps of your city on Google Maps.
  • Charge all devices. Buy a portable battery bank (10,000 mAh minimum, $15–$25).
  • Know exactly where you are going on night one. Do not leave this to chance.

The 7-Day Countdown

If you have a week, you do not have time for the full playbook. Here is the compressed version. Do these in order.

  1. Today: Gather all documents. Photocopy and email to yourself. Apply for SNAP online (request expedited processing — you qualify if you have less than $150 in liquid assets and less than $100 in monthly income).
  2. Today: Call 211. Say: “I am losing my housing in seven days. I need emergency shelter options and a caseworker.” Write down everything they tell you.
  3. Day 2: Cancel all subscriptions. Sell what you can on Facebook Marketplace — price low for same-day pickup.
  4. Day 3: Visit the nearest shelter in person. Learn intake procedures. Ask if they have beds available or a waitlist.
  5. Day 4: Pack your go-bag. Arrange storage or give belongings to someone you trust.
  6. Day 5: Apply for Medicaid. Get prescriptions filled. Download offline maps.
  7. Day 6: Set up mail forwarding. Charge all devices. Buy a battery bank if you do not have one.
  8. Day 7: Execute your plan. You know where you are going tonight.

Emergency Contacts to Call TODAY

These numbers work nationwide. Call them now, not when you are in crisis.

  • 211 — National social services hotline. Dial 2-1-1 or visit 211.org. Connects you to local shelters, food, utility assistance, and more. Available 24/7 in most areas.
  • Local Legal Aid — Free legal help for eviction defense, benefits applications, and tenant rights. Find yours at lawhelp.org.
  • Unemployment office — File the day you are laid off. Search “[your state] unemployment file claim” online.
  • SNAP office — Apply at fns.usda.gov/snap or your state’s DSS website. You do not need an address to apply.
  • LIHEAP (utility assistance) — May help pay utility bills or prevent shutoff. Call 211 or visit liheapch.acf.hhs.gov.
  • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — Call or text 988. If you are in emotional crisis, use this. Available 24/7.
  • National Domestic Violence Hotline — 1-800-799-7233. If you are fleeing abuse, they connect you to safe shelter immediately.
  • SAMHSA Helpline — 1-800-662-4357. Free referrals for substance abuse and mental health treatment. 24/7.
  • Veterans Crisis Line — Call 988, press 1. Or text 838255. If you are a veteran, VA homeless programs have separate funding and beds.

Eviction Timelines: Judicial vs. Non-Judicial States

Your state determines how long eviction takes. This matters because every extra day is a day you use for prepositioning.

Judicial Eviction States (most states)

In judicial states, your landlord must file a lawsuit and get a court order to evict you. This process has multiple steps, and each one takes time.

  • Notice period: 3–30 days depending on state and reason (nonpayment vs. lease violation vs. no-cause).
  • Court filing and summons: 1–3 weeks after notice expires.
  • Hearing: 1–4 weeks after filing. You have the right to appear. Show up. Even if you cannot win, judges sometimes grant extra time.
  • Judgment and writ of possession: If the landlord wins, the sheriff posts a notice. You typically get 24–72 hours after posting.
  • Total typical timeline: 30–90 days from first notice to sheriff lockout.

Non-Judicial Eviction States

A few states (e.g., parts of Arkansas, West Virginia) allow landlords to evict without a court order in some circumstances. Timelines are shorter — sometimes as little as 10–14 days. If you are in one of these states, you have less time. Look up your state’s specific process at nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/state-landlord-tenant-laws.

To find your exact timeline: search “[your state] eviction process timeline” or call your local Legal Aid office. They handle evictions daily and can tell you exactly how many days you have.

How to Negotiate with Your Landlord

Your landlord does not want to evict you. Eviction costs them money — court filing fees ($50–$200), lost rent during the process, turnover costs, and sometimes attorney fees. Use this leverage.

Cash for Keys

You agree to leave by a specific date. The landlord gives you cash — typically $500–$2,000 — and agrees not to file an eviction on your record. Get this in writing. This is often the best outcome for both sides: you get cash and a clean record, they get the unit back without court costs.

Voluntary Departure Agreement

Similar to cash for keys but without the cash. You agree to leave by a date in exchange for the landlord not filing a formal eviction. The value: no eviction on your record. This matters because an eviction filing shows up on tenant screening reports and makes it harder to rent in the future.

Payment Plan

If you owe back rent and have some income coming (unemployment, new job), propose a payment plan in writing. Many landlords will accept partial payment over a formal eviction. Be realistic — do not promise what you cannot pay.

What to Do About Your Lease

You have a lease. You cannot pay. You have two options, and each has different consequences.

Option 1: Break the Lease

  • You owe the remaining rent on the lease term — but most states require the landlord to mitigate damages by finding a new tenant.
  • The landlord can send the balance to collections. This hurts your credit score, but credit damage is recoverable. Being homeless longer because you stayed and drained your cash is not.
  • Some states allow early termination for job loss, domestic violence, or military deployment. Check your state’s tenant protection laws.
  • Get any agreement to release you from the lease in writing.

Option 2: Ride Out the Lease

  • If the lease expires soon (30–60 days), it may be worth staying and saving cash during that time.
  • You still owe rent during this period, but your landlord cannot remove you until the lease expires (and then must follow formal eviction if you do not leave).
  • Use this time for full prepositioning — the 30-day countdown above.

What to Tell Future Landlords

When you apply for housing in the future, landlords will ask about your rental history. Be honest but strategic.

  • If you negotiated a voluntary departure: “I left voluntarily due to a job loss. My previous landlord can confirm I left the unit in good condition.”
  • If you were formally evicted: “I went through a financial hardship. I have since stabilized and can provide references from [shelter, caseworker, employer].”
  • An eviction filing stays on your record for 7 years in most states. A voluntary departure does not. This is why negotiating matters.
♟ The Game: Information Asymmetry

Before you lose housing, you have an information advantage over the systems you will need. You can research shelters, learn eligibility rules, and gather documentation while you still have internet, a mailing address, and cognitive bandwidth. Once you are in crisis, every bureaucratic interaction becomes a blind negotiation where the caseworker knows the rules and you do not.

The caseworker knows the rules; you do not. Preposition now: learn what you qualify for, what documentation you need, and who the gatekeepers are.

Mapping Your Resources

Before you can plan, you need to know what you are working with. Sit down and make a brutally honest inventory of every resource available to you right now.

Resource Inventory

  • People who would let you stay for a night or two
  • People who would lend you money (and how much)
  • Cash, savings, sellable assets
  • Skills that translate to immediate income
  • Access to a vehicle
  • Geographic advantages or constraints
  • Benefits you may already qualify for
⚠ Warning

Do not tell everyone you are about to be homeless. Some people will help. Others will distance themselves, judge you, or use the information against you. Be strategic about disclosure.

The Sunk Cost Trap

You have already spent money trying to keep your housing. Rent you could not afford, late fees, utility catch-up payments. Your brain will tell you to keep spending because you have already put so much in. This is the sunk cost trap, and it will drain your last resources if you let it. The money is gone. The question now is what you do with what is left.

Scenario
Minneapolis, January. Two kids, ages 6 and 9. Eviction in 5 days. $220 in checking.
  • Below zero temps
  • Two minor children
  • 5-day deadline
  • $220 cash
  • No local network

Call 211 immediately. Tell them you have minor children and five days until eviction. Family shelters prioritize children, especially in extreme cold. If 211 cannot place you tonight, ask for the Salvation Army, Catholic Charities, or any faith-based family shelter in the metro area.

The key insight: with children, the decision tree changes completely. Family shelters have different rules, different capacity, and different pathways.

Decision Tree: What Kind of Homeless?

Not all homelessness looks the same. Your first strategic decision is choosing which type best fits your situation.

Option A: Shelter

Best for: people without a vehicle, in cities with good shelter infrastructure. The Shelter System (p. 63)

Key Takeaways
  • Know your timeline. Eviction is a process, not an event.
  • Triage financially. Phone, food, transportation first. Rent last.
  • Gather documents now. ID replacement is catastrophically difficult later.
  • Choose your path. Shelter, vehicle, couch, or outside.
  • Your brain is under load. Write things down.
