Chapter 1

Triage

Assessing your situation, timeline, and options before you lose the roof.

You are about to lose your housing. Maybe you have a week. Maybe you have a month. Maybe it already happened and you are reading this in a parking lot. Whatever the timeline, this chapter is about one thing: making the best possible decisions with the time you have left.

Most people waste the window before homelessness on denial, panic, or both. They spend their last dollars trying to save a situation that is already lost. They burn through favors on emotional calls instead of strategic ones. They lose documents they will desperately need later.

Triage means deciding what matters most when you cannot save everything. In an emergency room, doctors do not treat every patient equally. They treat the most critical cases first. You need to think the same way about your life right now.

This chapter will walk you through the assessment: How much time do you actually have? What money moves should you make right now? Who are your real resources? And which type of homelessness fits your situation best?

None of this is comfortable. But every hour you spend thinking clearly now saves you days of scrambling later. The difference between people who recover from homelessness quickly and those who do not often comes down to what they did in this window — before they lost the roof.

How Much Time Do You Actually Have?

The single most important thing to figure out right now is your actual deadline. Not when you think it will happen. Not when your landlord said it would happen. The legal, enforceable deadline when you must be out.

Most people overestimate how fast eviction works. The legal process has steps, and each step takes time. Understanding these steps gives you days or weeks you did not know you had.

Judicial Eviction States (Most States)

In judicial states, your landlord must file a lawsuit and get a court order to evict you. Each step takes time:

  • Notice to quit: 3–30 days depending on state and reason (nonpayment vs. lease violation vs. no-cause).
  • Court filing and summons: 1–3 weeks after notice expires.
  • Hearing: 1–4 weeks after filing. You have the right to appear. Show up. Even if you cannot win, judges sometimes grant extra time.
  • Writ of possession: If the landlord wins, the sheriff posts a notice. You typically get 24–72 hours. This is the hard deadline.
  • Total typical timeline: 30–90 days from first notice to sheriff lockout.

Non-Judicial Eviction States

A few states (e.g., parts of Arkansas, West Virginia) allow landlords to evict without a court order in some circumstances. Timelines are shorter — sometimes as little as 10–14 days. Look up your state’s specific process at nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/state-landlord-tenant-laws.

Use every day of this process. Do not move out early because it feels inevitable. Legal eviction takes time, and that time is yours. Search your state’s legal aid website or call your local Legal Aid office for specific timelines.

The moment you start fighting the eviction on emotional grounds rather than strategic ones, you have already lost the triage.

How to Negotiate with Your Landlord

Your landlord does not want to evict you. Eviction costs them money — court filing fees ($50–$200), lost rent during the process, turnover costs, and sometimes attorney fees. Use this leverage.

Cash for Keys

You agree to leave by a specific date. The landlord gives you cash — typically $500–$2,000 — and agrees not to file an eviction on your record. Get this in writing. This is often the best outcome for both sides.

Voluntary Departure Agreement

Similar to cash for keys but without the cash. You agree to leave by a date in exchange for the landlord not filing a formal eviction. The value: no eviction on your record. This matters because an eviction filing shows up on tenant screening reports for 7 years and makes it harder to rent in the future.

Payment Plan

If you owe back rent and have some income coming (unemployment, new job), propose a payment plan in writing. Many landlords will accept partial payment over a formal eviction. Be realistic — do not promise what you cannot pay.

What to Do About Your Lease

Option 1: Break the Lease

  • You owe the remaining rent on the lease term — but most states require the landlord to mitigate damages by finding a new tenant.
  • The landlord can send the balance to collections. This hurts your credit score, but credit damage is recoverable in 2–3 years.
  • Some states allow early termination for job loss, domestic violence, or military deployment. Check your state’s tenant protection laws.
  • Get any agreement to release you from the lease in writing.

Option 2: Ride Out the Lease

  • If the lease expires soon (30–60 days), it may be worth staying and saving cash during that time.
  • You still owe rent during this period, but your landlord cannot remove you until the lease expires.
  • Use this time for full prepositioning — the countdown checklist later in this chapter.

What to Tell Future Landlords

  • If you negotiated a voluntary departure: “I left voluntarily due to a job loss. My previous landlord can confirm I left the unit in good condition.”
  • If you were formally evicted: “I went through a financial hardship. I have since stabilized and can provide references from [shelter, caseworker, employer].”

▶ Your Brain Right Now: The Scarcity Tax

Research by Mullainathan and Shafir (Harvard/Princeton) found that financial scarcity consumes cognitive bandwidth equivalent to losing 13 IQ points. You are not getting dumber — your brain is allocating resources to the crisis, leaving less for everything else. This is measurable and temporary, but it affects every decision you make right now.

The practical takeaway: Simplify decisions, write things down, do not trust your memory right now. Your brain is rationing under load, not failing.

Financial Readiness

You are now in survival mode. Stop paying bills in the order you are used to. Spend money in order of what keeps you alive and functional — not what is due first.

What to Spend On (In Priority Order)

  1. Food. You cannot think or function hungry. Budget $5–$10/day maximum. Rice, beans, peanut butter, bread. Food banks are free — find them now.
  2. Phone service. Your $15–$25/month prepaid plan keeps you connected to jobs, 211, shelters, and people. If you qualify for SNAP or Medicaid, apply for the Lifeline program ($9.25/month subsidy) or Assurance Wireless (free phone and service).
  3. Medications. Get a 90-day supply now. Ask your doctor for generic alternatives. GoodRx can cut prescription costs 50–80%. Walmart has a $4 generic list.
  4. Transportation. Gas if you have a car. A bus pass if you do not. A monthly bus pass ($30–$75) is cheaper than rideshares.
  5. Hygiene basics. Dollar store: toothbrush, toothpaste, deodorant, soap, wet wipes. Under $10 total. Looking presentable keeps you employable.
  6. Storage unit. Only if you have belongings worth more than the monthly cost. A 5x5 unit runs $30–$60/month. If your stuff is worth less than three months of storage fees, sell it.

What to Stop Paying Immediately

  • Rent — if eviction is inevitable. The legal process takes 30–90 days. Redirect that $800–$1,800/month to your exit fund. You will owe the back rent and it will go to collections. Your credit score will drop. None of that matters right now. Credit scores recover in 2–3 years.
  • Credit cards. Minimum payments on cards you will never pay off are wasted money.
  • Subscriptions. Netflix, Spotify, gym memberships, meal kits, cloud storage. Cancel every single one today. That $150/month in subscriptions is two weeks of food.
  • Insurance premiums. Car insurance is the exception if you have a vehicle. Health insurance — switch to Medicaid. Renter’s insurance — cancel.
  • Student loans. Apply for income-driven repayment or forbearance. Federal loans will not garnish you if you file the right paperwork. Call your servicer today.

The Exit Number: How Much Cash to Have When You Leave

Every dollar you have when you become homeless is a dollar of runway before you hit zero. Here is the math.

  • $500 or less: Danger zone. You have days, not weeks. One unexpected cost wipes you out.
  • $1,000: Minimum viable. Gives you 2–4 weeks of food, phone, transportation. Tight but survivable.
  • $1,500–$2,000: The sweet spot. Gives you 4–8 weeks of runway. Enough to job search, apply for benefits, and stabilize without panic spending.
  • $2,500–$3,000: Ideal. Gives you 2–3 months and enough for a security deposit when you find housing.
  • $3,000+: You have options most homeless people do not. Protect this aggressively. Do not spend it on motels ($60–$100/night burns through $3,000 in a month).

If eviction is inevitable, the fastest way to build exit cash is to stop paying rent and redirect that money to savings. If your rent is $1,200 and you have 60 days before the sheriff comes, that is $2,400 — the difference between the danger zone and the sweet spot.

The Benefits Trap: Asset Limits

  • SNAP: Most states eliminated the asset test. In states that still have it, the limit is $2,750 ($4,250 for households with elderly/disabled). Your car does NOT count.
  • Medicaid: No asset test in expansion states (40 states). Income-based only.
  • General Assistance (GA): Varies widely. Some states cap at $200–$1,000 in assets.
  • SSI: $2,000 asset limit ($3,000 for couples). Very strict.
  • Expedited SNAP: Requires less than $150 in liquid assets for the 7-day fast track.
♟ The Game: Cash vs. Benefits

Having $2,000 in cash keeps you stable but may disqualify you from some programs. The strategic play: keep enough cash to survive 4–6 weeks ($1,500), apply for benefits immediately, and spend down strategically on things that do not count as assets (prepaid phone cards, gear, non-perishable food, a used vehicle). A car is not counted as an asset for SNAP in most states. Spending $1,000 on a running vehicle converts cash into a non-countable asset that also serves as shelter.

Where to Keep Your Cash

  • Bank account: Keep the bulk here. Chime, Cash App, or any no-fee account. Access via debit card. Harder to steal than physical cash.
  • On your body: $50–$100 for daily needs. In a front pocket, money belt, or sock. Never in a back pocket or bag.
  • Emergency stash: $100–$200 hidden separately. Taped inside your sleeping bag, sewn into a jacket lining, or in a sealed bag inside your pack. This is your last-resort money.

Accessing Retirement Savings Early

If you have a 401(k), IRA, or other retirement account, that money could be the difference between stabilizing and spiraling. Yes, there are penalties. Yes, it affects your future. But you cannot retire if you freeze to death this winter. Here is exactly how it works.

Roth IRA — Your Best Option (No Penalty on Contributions)

You can withdraw your contributions (the money you put in, not the growth) at any time, for any reason, with zero penalty and zero taxes. Log into your brokerage (Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab), request a distribution, specify “return of contributions.” Money hits your bank account in 3–5 business days.

401(k) From a Previous Employer

  1. Roll it to a Roth IRA, then withdraw contributions. Triggers income tax on the rollover but no 10% penalty. Best for amounts over $5,000.
  2. Direct withdrawal (cash out). Income tax (10–37%) PLUS 10% early withdrawal penalty if under 59½. On $10,000, expect to receive $5,300–$8,000.
  3. 401(k) loan (if still employed). Borrow up to 50% or $50,000. If you lose your job, the loan becomes due immediately.

The Age 55 Rule

If you are 55+ and separated from your employer (laid off, fired, or quit), you can withdraw from THAT employer’s 401(k) without the 10% penalty. You still pay income tax. Only applies to the 401(k) from the employer you just left.

SECURE 2.0 Act — New Emergency Options (2024+)

  • Emergency Personal Expense Distribution: Up to $1,000/year from 401(k) or IRA. No 10% penalty. Self-certified. Repayable within 3 years.
  • Domestic Abuse Distribution: Up to $10,000 (or 50% of vested balance). No 10% penalty. Repayable within 3 years.
  • Disaster Distribution: Up to $22,000 without penalty for federally declared disasters.

Hardship Withdrawal (401k Only)

“Preventing eviction” IS a qualifying hardship. If you are behind on rent and facing eviction, this is your legal basis. You still pay income tax and the 10% penalty in most cases, but you get the money.

⚠ The Real Math on Early Withdrawal

A $10,000 early 401(k) withdrawal at 22% tax bracket: you lose $2,200 in income tax + $1,000 penalty = $3,200. You receive $6,800. That hurts. But $6,800 is 4–5 months of survival. The question is not “should I take the penalty?” The question is “what happens to me without this money?”

Who to Call

  • Your 401(k) plan administrator: Phone number on your statement. Say: “I need to discuss an early distribution or hardship withdrawal.”
  • Brokerages (for IRAs): Fidelity (800-343-3548), Vanguard (800-662-7447), Schwab (800-435-4000).
  • IRS helpline: 800-829-1040. They explain penalty exceptions for your situation.
  • Free tax help (VITA): irs.gov/vita.

The Decision Framework

  1. Access Roth IRA contributions first — zero penalty, zero tax.
  2. Use the SECURE 2.0 emergency distribution ($1,000) — no penalty.
  3. If over 55 and just laid off, use the Rule of 55 — no penalty.
  4. Hardship withdrawal to prevent eviction — penalty applies but you get the money.
  5. Full cash-out as last resort — expect to lose 30–40%.

Do not let anyone shame you for accessing your own money during a crisis.

Cashing Out Life Insurance

If you have a life insurance policy, there may be money you can access right now.

Which Policies Have Cash Value

Whole life and universal life policies build cash surrender value over time. Term life has ZERO cash value — it only pays out if you die. If your policy is term life, skip this section.

How to Check

Call your insurance company (number on your policy or annual statements). Say: “What is the cash surrender value of my policy?” If you do not know which company holds your policy, check old bank statements for premium payments or call your state’s insurance department.

What to Expect

After 5+ years of premiums, expect 50–80% of what you have paid in. A policy with $12,000 in total premiums might have $6,000–$9,600 in cash value. Processing takes 2–4 weeks — start early.

The Decision

Cashing out cancels the policy and the death benefit disappears. If you have dependents who rely on that benefit, think carefully. If you are single with no dependents, the cash today matters more than a future payout that only helps if you are dead.

Other Options

  • Partial surrender: Withdraw part of the cash value without canceling. You keep a reduced death benefit.
  • Policy loans: Borrow against your cash value at 5–8% interest. No credit check. The loan reduces your death benefit but keeps the policy active. If you never repay, the loan plus interest is deducted from the death benefit.

The Countdown: Your Action Timeline

Whether you have 30 days or 7, use this single framework. Start at whatever point matches your timeline and work forward.

30 Days Out — Paperwork and Benefits

  • Gather all identity documents (ID, Social Security card, birth certificate, passport)
  • Photocopy everything. Email copies to yourself
  • Collect medical records, prescription list, court orders
  • Save tax returns from last 2 years
  • Apply for SNAP online (processing takes 7–30 days)
  • Apply for Medicaid
  • File for unemployment the day you are laid off
  • Call 211 — ask for caseworker referral
  • Contact your local Legal Aid office about your eviction
  • Check Roth IRA contributions and life insurance cash value
  • Open a Chime or Cash App account (no-fee banking)

21 Days Out — Money and Belongings

  • Cancel every subscription and recurring charge
  • Sell high-value items you cannot carry (price 20–30% below market for fast sales)
  • Arrange storage: friend’s garage (free) or 5x5 unit ($30–$60/month)
  • Get a 90-day supply of all medications
  • Get your car serviced if you have one (oil, tires, brakes)
  • Apply for the Lifeline phone program if you qualify

14 Days Out — Scouting and Connections

  • Visit shelters in person. Learn intake procedures, hours, rules, waitlists
  • Locate food banks, soup kitchens, day centers. Write down addresses and schedules
  • Set up mailing address: USPS General Delivery (free), PO Box ($20–$50/6mo), or shelter
  • Negotiate with landlord (cash for keys, voluntary departure)
  • Tell the people who need to know. Use the scripts in Chapter 3 (p. 27)

7 Days Out — Final Preparations

  • Pack your go-bag (see Chapter 2, p. 15)
  • Forward your mail via USPS
  • Download offline maps of your city on Google Maps
  • Charge all devices. Buy a portable battery bank (10,000 mAh, $15–$25)
  • Clean apartment for move-out. Take photos of condition for deposit disputes
  • Know exactly where you are going on night one

Day 1 — Execute

  • You know where you are sleeping tonight
  • Your go-bag is packed. Documents are on your person
  • Your phone is charged. You have a battery bank
  • You have cash split across body, bank account, and emergency stash

Emergency Contacts to Call TODAY

These numbers work nationwide. Call them now, not when you are in crisis.

  • 211 — National social services hotline. Dial 2-1-1 or visit 211.org. Connects you to local shelters, food, utility assistance. Available 24/7.
  • Local Legal Aid — Free legal help for eviction defense and tenant rights. Find yours at lawhelp.org.
  • Unemployment office — File the day you are laid off. Search “[your state] unemployment file claim” online.
  • SNAP office — Apply at fns.usda.gov/snap or your state’s DSS website. You do not need an address to apply.
  • LIHEAP (utility assistance) — May help pay utility bills or prevent shutoff. Visit liheapch.acf.hhs.gov.
  • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — Call or text 988. Available 24/7.
  • National Domestic Violence Hotline — 1-800-799-7233. Connects you to safe shelter immediately.
  • SAMHSA Helpline — 1-800-662-4357. Free referrals for substance abuse and mental health treatment. 24/7.
  • Veterans Crisis Line — Call 988, press 1. Or text 838255.

♟ The Game: Information Asymmetry

Before you lose housing, you have an information advantage over the systems you will need. You can research shelters, learn eligibility rules, and gather documentation while you still have internet, a mailing address, and cognitive bandwidth. Once you are in crisis, every bureaucratic interaction becomes a blind negotiation where the caseworker knows the rules and you do not. Preposition now: learn what you qualify for, what documentation you need, and who the gatekeepers are.

Mapping Your Resources

Before you can plan, you need to know what you are working with. Make a brutally honest inventory:

  • People who would let you stay for a night or two
  • People who would lend you money (and how much)
  • Cash, savings, sellable assets
  • Skills that translate to immediate income
  • Access to a vehicle
  • Geographic advantages or constraints
  • Benefits you may already qualify for
⚠ Warning

Do not tell everyone you are about to be homeless. Some people will help. Others will distance themselves, judge you, or use the information against you. Be strategic about disclosure.

The Sunk Cost Trap

You have already spent money trying to keep your housing. Rent you could not afford, late fees, utility catch-up payments. Your brain will tell you to keep spending because you have already put so much in. This is the sunk cost trap, and it will drain your last resources if you let it. The money is gone. The question now is what you do with what is left.

Not all homelessness looks the same. Your first strategic decision is choosing which type best fits your situation.

✔ Practical Tip

Write down your plan — even three bullet points. When your cortisol is high, your working memory drops. Externalize your thinking onto paper or your phone’s notes app.

Key Takeaways
  • Know your timeline. Eviction is a process, not an event.
  • Triage financially. Food, phone, meds, transport first. Rent last.
  • Build exit cash. Aim for $1,500–$2,000. Stop paying rent to get there.
  • Gather documents now. ID replacement is catastrophically difficult later.
  • Choose your path. Shelter, vehicle, couch, or outside.
  • Your brain is under load. Write things down.
