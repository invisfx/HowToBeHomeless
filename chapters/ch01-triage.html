Chapter 1

Triage

Assessing your situation, timeline, and options before you lose the roof.

You are about to lose your housing. Maybe you have a week. Maybe you have a month. Maybe it already happened and you are reading this in a parking lot. Whatever the timeline, this chapter is about one thing: making the best possible decisions with the time you have left.

Most people waste the window before homelessness on denial, panic, or both. They spend their last dollars trying to save a situation that is already lost. They burn through favors on emotional calls instead of strategic ones. They lose documents they will desperately need later.

Triage means deciding what matters most when you cannot save everything. In an emergency room, doctors do not treat every patient equally. They treat the most critical cases first. You need to think the same way about your life right now.

This chapter will walk you through the assessment: How much time do you actually have? What money moves should you make right now? Who are your real resources? And which type of homelessness fits your situation best?

None of this is comfortable. But every hour you spend thinking clearly now saves you days of scrambling later. The difference between people who recover from homelessness quickly and those who do not often comes down to what they did in this window — before they lost the roof.

How Much Time Do You Actually Have?

The single most important thing to figure out right now is your actual deadline. Not when you think it will happen. Not when your landlord said it would happen. The legal, enforceable deadline when you must be out.

Most people overestimate how fast eviction works. The legal process has steps, and each step takes time. Understanding these steps does not make eviction okay — it gives you days or weeks you did not know you had.

Eviction Timelines by State

Every state has its own eviction process, but the general sequence is the same everywhere. Here is what to expect and how long each step typically takes.

  • Notice to quit: Typically 3–30 days depending on your state and situation.
  • Court filing: After the notice period, your landlord files in court. This buys you 1–4 weeks.
  • Hearing date: You have the right to appear. Even if you cannot win, showing up often buys time.
  • Writ of possession: The final step. The sheriff gives you 24–72 hours. This is the hard deadline.

Use every day of this process. Do not move out early because it feels inevitable. Legal eviction takes time, and that time is yours. Search your state's legal aid website for specific timelines.

The moment you start fighting the eviction on emotional grounds rather than strategic ones, you have already lost the triage.

▶ Your Brain Right Now: The Scarcity Tax

Research by Mullainathan and Shafir (Harvard/Princeton) found that financial scarcity consumes cognitive bandwidth equivalent to losing 13 IQ points. You are not getting dumber — your brain is allocating resources to the crisis, leaving less for everything else. This is measurable and temporary, but it affects every decision you make right now.

The practical takeaway: Simplify decisions, write things down, do not trust your memory right now. Your brain is rationing under load, not failing.

Financial Triage

Stop paying bills in the order you are used to. You are now in survival mode, and that means spending money in order of what keeps you alive and functional — not what is due first.

  1. Pay for shelter last. Counterintuitive, but rent is the biggest bill and the slowest to enforce. Pay for food, phone, and transportation first.
  2. Keep your phone alive. Your phone is your connection to jobs, services, people, and information.
  3. Stockpile ID documents. Get copies of everything while you still have an address and a printer.
  4. Cancel recurring charges. Subscriptions, memberships, auto-payments. Every dollar counts now.

This is not about being irresponsible. This is about recognizing that your old payment priorities no longer apply. Every dollar you spend now has to earn its place in your survival budget.

♟ The Game: Information Asymmetry

Before you lose housing, you have an information advantage over the systems you will need. You can research shelters, learn eligibility rules, and gather documentation while you still have internet, a mailing address, and cognitive bandwidth. Once you are in crisis, every bureaucratic interaction becomes a blind negotiation where the caseworker knows the rules and you do not.

The caseworker knows the rules; you do not. Preposition now: learn what you qualify for, what documentation you need, and who the gatekeepers are.

Mapping Your Resources

Before you can plan, you need to know what you are working with. Sit down and make a brutally honest inventory of every resource available to you right now.

Resource Inventory

  • People who would let you stay for a night or two
  • People who would lend you money (and how much)
  • Cash, savings, sellable assets
  • Skills that translate to immediate income
  • Access to a vehicle
  • Geographic advantages or constraints
  • Benefits you may already qualify for
⚠ Warning

Do not tell everyone you are about to be homeless. Some people will help. Others will distance themselves, judge you, or use the information against you. Be strategic about disclosure.

The Sunk Cost Trap

You have already spent money trying to keep your housing. Rent you could not afford, late fees, utility catch-up payments. Your brain will tell you to keep spending because you have already put so much in. This is the sunk cost trap, and it will drain your last resources if you let it. The money is gone. The question now is what you do with what is left.

Scenario
Minneapolis, January. Two kids, ages 6 and 9. Eviction in 5 days. $220 in checking.
  • Below zero temps
  • Two minor children
  • 5-day deadline
  • $220 cash
  • No local network

Call 211 immediately. Tell them you have minor children and five days until eviction. Family shelters prioritize children, especially in extreme cold. If 211 cannot place you tonight, ask for the Salvation Army, Catholic Charities, or any faith-based family shelter in the metro area.

The key insight: with children, the decision tree changes completely. Family shelters have different rules, different capacity, and different pathways.

🎒 Gear Note: The Pre-Crisis Go-Bag

If you are reading this chapter, start packing now. The non-negotiables:

  • Photo ID, Social Security card, birth certificate
  • Phone + charger + portable battery
  • Medications (30-day supply if possible)
  • Cash (withdraw what you can)

Decision Tree: What Kind of Homeless?

Not all homelessness looks the same. Your first strategic decision is choosing which type best fits your situation.

Option A: Shelter

Best for: people without a vehicle, in cities with good shelter infrastructure. The Shelter System

Option B: Vehicle

Best for: people with a reliable car or van, employment or income. Vehicle Living

Option C: Couch Surfing

Best for: people with a strong social network, short-term crisis. Couch Surfing

Option D: Outside

Last resort for most. Requires gear, awareness, and tolerance for discomfort. Outside

✔ Practical Tip

Write down your plan — even three bullet points. When your cortisol is high, your working memory drops. Externalize your thinking onto paper or your phone's notes app.

Key Takeaways
  • Know your timeline. Eviction is a process, not an event.
  • Triage financially. Phone, food, transportation first. Rent last.
  • Gather documents now. ID replacement is catastrophically difficult later.
  • Choose your path. Shelter, vehicle, couch, or outside.
  • Your brain is under load. Write things down.
