Chapter 14

Benefits and Services

What you qualify for, how to get it, and why the system makes it so hard.

There is money and assistance available to you right now that you are probably not accessing. Not because you do not qualify, but because the systems that distribute it were designed by people who assumed applicants have an address, a phone, reliable transportation, internet access, and the executive function to navigate a bureaucracy during office hours. You may have none of these things, and the system does not care.

This chapter is a practical guide to getting what you are entitled to. It covers the major federal and state benefit programs, the mailing address problem that blocks access to most of them, how to work with caseworkers effectively, and the specific resources available to veterans. It is not comprehensive — benefit programs vary by state and change frequently — but it gives you the framework and the strategies to navigate whatever programs exist in your area.

The most important thing to understand about benefits is this: the application process is the hardest part. Once you are enrolled, most programs continue with minimal effort. The barrier is getting through the front door, and that barrier is artificially high. It is not designed to keep you out on purpose (usually), but the effect is the same. Knowing how the system works gives you an enormous advantage over people who approach it blind.

Start with 211. Call or text from any phone. It is the national directory of social services and will connect you to local programs you did not know existed.

▶ Your Brain Right Now: Learned Helplessness

Psychologist Martin Seligman discovered that when repeated efforts to improve a situation fail, the brain learns to stop trying — even when new options appear. If you have applied for benefits and been denied, called numbers that went nowhere, or waited months for a callback that never came, your brain may have quietly concluded that trying is pointless. Recognize this for what it is: a neurological adaptation, not the truth. The system is broken in specific ways, and knowing which doors are actually closed vs. which ones your brain has preemptively shut is the difference between stagnation and exit.

What You Likely Qualify For

SNAP (Food Stamps)

If you are homeless, you almost certainly qualify for SNAP. Benefits range from $200 to $300 per month for a single adult. You do not need a fixed address to apply. A shelter address, P.O. box, or even a description of where you stay can work. Apply online, by phone, or in person at your local Department of Social Services. Expedited processing is available if you have less than $100 in liquid assets.

Medicaid

In states that expanded Medicaid under the ACA, any adult earning below 138% of the federal poverty level qualifies. If you are homeless with no income, you qualify. Medicaid covers doctor visits, prescriptions, mental health services, dental (in some states), and emergency care. Apply through your state's health exchange or at any Federally Qualified Health Center.

SSI / SSDI

Supplemental Security Income (SSI) provides monthly payments to disabled individuals with limited income. Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) covers people who have worked enough quarters to qualify. Both require extensive documentation and often take months to process. If you have a disability, start the application immediately — the clock starts when you file, and back payments accrue from the filing date.

General Assistance / General Relief (GA/GR) — Cash for Single Adults

This is the program most people do not know exists. General Assistance (called General Relief in some states) provides monthly cash payments to adults who do not qualify for other programs — no children required, no disability required. It is often the ONLY cash aid available to a single homeless adult.

How Much and Where

  • California: County-funded, varies by county. LA County: ~$221/month. Santa Clara County: $150–$343/month. Contra Costa: $336/month. Apply at your county social services office or BenefitsCal.com.
  • New York: Safety Net Assistance for single adults. Amount varies. Apply at your local HRA office or mybenefits.ny.gov.
  • New Jersey: $185/month (employable) or $277/month (unemployable). Apply at county welfare office.
  • Pennsylvania: GA available in most counties. $205/month typical. Apply at county assistance office.
  • Washington state: Aged, Blind, or Disabled (ABD) program and Housing Essential Needs (HEN). Apply at DSHS.
  • Massachusetts: Emergency Aid to the Elderly, Disabled, and Children (EAEDC). $303.70/month.

Important: About half of U.S. states have eliminated or severely limited GA programs. 15+ states offer nothing. Call 211 and ask: "Is there a General Assistance or General Relief program in my county?" If yes, apply immediately. If no, ask what other cash programs exist.

Asset Limits for GA

GA programs typically have the strictest asset limits of any program: $200–$1,000 in most states. This is lower than SNAP ($2,750) or SSI ($2,000). If you have more cash than the limit, you may need to spend down first. See the "Cash vs. Benefits" section in Chapter 1 (p. 3) for strategy on this.

Free Phone Programs — More Than Just Lifeline

The federal Lifeline program gives you $9.25/month off phone service. But several carriers go further and provide a completely free phone AND free service:

  • Assurance Wireless (assurancewireless.com) — Free smartphone + unlimited talk/text + data (varies by state, typically 10–15GB). T-Mobile network. You qualify if you receive SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, or have income below 135% of poverty level.
  • SafeLink Wireless (safelinkwireless.com) — Free phone + talk/text/data. TracFone network. Same qualification as Assurance.
  • Q Link Wireless (qlinkwireless.com) — Free phone + unlimited talk/text + data. Available in most states.
  • StandUp Wireless (standupwireless.com) — Free phone and service in select states.
  • How to apply: Visit any of these websites or go to lifelinesupport.org. You need proof of benefit enrollment (EBT card, Medicaid card) OR proof of income below 135% of poverty (~$20,000/year for individual). Apply online in 10 minutes. Phone arrives by mail in 5–7 days.

You are allowed ONE Lifeline benefit per household. If you already have one, you cannot get a second. But if you have never applied, there is a free phone waiting for you right now.

The Mailing Address Problem

Almost every benefit program requires a mailing address. Here are the solutions that actually work:

  • Shelter address: Most shelters will receive mail on your behalf. This is the most commonly accepted address for benefit applications.
  • P.O. box: The USPS offers P.O. boxes starting at roughly $20 for six months. Some post offices waive fees for homeless individuals with documentation from a service provider.
  • General Delivery: The USPS will hold mail addressed to you at "General Delivery" at your local post office for up to 30 days. Free, but you must pick it up in person with ID.
  • Service provider address: Many homeless service organizations provide a mailing address as one of their core services.

Working with Caseworkers

A good caseworker can cut months off your timeline to benefits and housing. A bad one or no caseworker at all can leave you stuck indefinitely. Here is how to get one and how to work with them effectively.

  • Get assigned: Contact your local Continuum of Care (CoC) or homeless services authority. They coordinate caseworker assignments.
  • Be organized: Keep all your documents in a waterproof bag or folder. Bring copies to every meeting. Caseworkers process dozens of cases and will prioritize clients who make their job easier.
  • Follow up relentlessly: Do not wait for callbacks. Call them. Show up. Be polite but persistent. Squeaky wheels get greased in this system.
  • Document everything: Write down who you talked to, when, and what they said. This protects you if you need to escalate or appeal a denial.

Veterans

If you are a veteran, you have access to an entirely separate system of services that is generally better funded and more responsive than civilian programs.

  • SSVF (Supportive Services for Veteran Families): Rapid rehousing, case management, financial assistance. The fastest path from street to housing for most veterans.
  • HUD-VASH: Housing vouchers combined with VA case management. Longer wait but permanent housing solution.
  • VA Healthcare: Comprehensive medical and mental health care. Enroll at any VA medical center.
  • National Call Center for Homeless Veterans: 1-877-4AID-VET (1-877-424-3838). 24/7. Start here.

The Complete Benefits Checklist

Here is everything you might qualify for. Check every item on this list. Apply for every program where you meet the criteria. Do it all in the same week. Benefits take time to process and the clocks only start when you file.

  • SNAP (food stamps) — $200-300/month for food. Apply at state DHS office or online.
  • Medicaid — Free healthcare in expansion states. Apply at healthcare.gov or state exchange.
  • TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families) — Cash assistance for families with children. State DHS office.
  • General Assistance (GA) — $100-400/month cash for single adults. Not every state offers it. Ask 211.
  • SSI/SSDI — Monthly payments for disabled individuals. Apply at ssa.gov or any Social Security office.
  • Section 8 — Housing vouchers. Long waitlists (months to years), but get on the list now.
  • LIHEAP — Utility assistance. Helps with heating and cooling bills. Relevant when you find housing.
  • WIC — Nutrition assistance for pregnant women, new mothers, and children under 5.
  • Lifeline / ACP — Free or discounted cell phone service. Apply through your phone carrier or at lifelinesupport.org.
  • Unemployment Insurance — If you were laid off, file immediately at your state workforce website.

How to Apply: The Big Three, Step by Step

SNAP (Food Stamps)

  1. Go to your state's DHS website or walk into a county office. Bring ID and SSN.
  2. Fill out the application. Takes 20 to 30 minutes. State that you are homeless.
  3. Tell them you need expedited processing (if assets under $150). This is not a request — it is your legal right under federal law.
  4. Complete the phone or in-person interview within 7 days.
  5. Receive your EBT card by mail in 7 to 30 days. Use your mailing address solution (see below).

Medicaid

  1. Go to healthcare.gov or your state's health exchange website.
  2. If your state expanded Medicaid (39 states plus DC have), you qualify with no income. If your state did not expand, you may still qualify through other categories.
  3. Complete the application online, by phone (1-800-318-2596), or in person at a Federally Qualified Health Center.
  4. In expansion states, coverage can start immediately. No waiting period for people with no income.

Unemployment Insurance

  1. File within the first week of losing your job. Every week you delay is a week of lost benefits.
  2. Go to your state's workforce or unemployment website. Search "[your state] unemployment insurance."
  3. You need: your SSN, last employer's name and address, dates of employment, reason for separation.
  4. File weekly certifications to continue receiving benefits. Miss a week and your claim can be suspended.
Part V: Money, Work, and Systems Benefits and Services

The Mailing Address Solution

You need mail to receive benefit cards, appointment notices, and legal documents. Without a mailing address, the system cannot reach you and your applications stall. Here is every option, ranked by reliability.

  • USPS General Delivery. Free. Go to any post office and tell them you want to receive mail via General Delivery. Mail addressed to "[Your Name], General Delivery, [City, State, ZIP]" will be held at that post office for up to 30 days. Pick it up with your photo ID. This is the fastest solution and costs nothing.
  • Shelter address. Most shelters receive mail on behalf of clients. This is the most commonly accepted address for benefit applications. Ask at intake which address to use.
  • Day center or service provider address. Many homeless service organizations provide a mailing address as a core service. Some also have message and phone services.
  • PO Box. Starting at approximately $20 for six months. Some post offices waive fees for homeless individuals with a letter from a service provider.
  • Friend or family address. If someone you trust will receive mail for you, use their address. Make sure they know to hold everything and contact you when something arrives.

Whatever address you choose, use the same one on every application. Consistency matters. If your mail goes to three different places, you will miss something important.

Working with Caseworkers

A caseworker can navigate the system for you, connect you to programs you did not know existed, and advocate on your behalf. Here is how to get one and how to make the relationship work.

How to Get Assigned a Caseworker

  • Call 211. Ask for the local Continuum of Care (CoC) lead agency. They coordinate homeless services and caseworker assignments.
  • Go to a shelter. Most shelters provide case management as part of their services. You may need to stay there to qualify, or they may offer it to walk-ins.
  • Visit a homeless day center. Many day centers have on-site case managers who can take new clients.
  • Contact your county DHS. Ask about homeless services case management. Some counties assign caseworkers directly.

How to Make It Work

  • Bring everything to every meeting. ID, SSN card, benefit letters, appointment notices, all in your waterproof folder. Make copies of everything.
  • Show up on time. Caseworkers manage dozens of cases. They prioritize clients who show up ready.
  • Follow up. Do not wait for callbacks. Call them. Go to their office. Be polite but persistent. The squeaky wheel gets greased in this system.
  • Write everything down. Who you talked to, when, what they said, what they promised. This protects you if you need to escalate or appeal.
  • Ask them what else you qualify for. A good caseworker knows programs you have never heard of. Ask the direct question: "Is there anything else I should be applying for?"

Veterans: Your Separate, Better System

If you served in the military, you have access to an entirely separate system of services that is generally better funded and more responsive than civilian programs. Use it. That is what it exists for.

Start Here

Call 1-877-4AID-VET (1-877-424-3838). This is the National Call Center for Homeless Veterans. Available 24/7. They will connect you to local VA services, coordinate housing, and start the intake process. One phone call.

VA Healthcare

Enroll at any VA medical center or online at va.gov/health-care/apply. If you are a combat veteran, there is no income requirement. If you are a non-combat veteran, income-based eligibility applies, but homelessness essentially guarantees qualification. VA healthcare covers medical, dental (in some programs), mental health, and substance abuse treatment.

HUD-VASH

HUD-Veterans Affairs Supportive Housing combines a Section 8 housing voucher with VA case management. This is the gold standard for veteran homelessness: permanent housing with support services. Wait times vary by location (weeks to months), but the program is specifically reserved for homeless veterans. Get on the list immediately.

SSVF (Supportive Services for Veteran Families)

Rapid rehousing grants that cover security deposits, first month's rent, and short-term rental assistance. Also provides case management, financial planning, and legal assistance. This is the fastest path from street to housing for most veterans. Contact your local SSVF grantee through 211 or the call center above.

Other Veteran Resources

  • Stand Down events. Multi-day events in many cities providing free meals, clothing, medical care, legal help, and benefit enrollment for veterans. Usually held annually. Search "Stand Down [your city]."
  • Veterans Treatment Courts. If you have legal issues, these courts handle veteran cases with understanding of service-related issues like PTSD and TBI.

How to Appeal a Benefits Denial

Many initial applications are denied. This is normal. It does not mean you do not qualify. The system denies by default and approves on persistence. Here is the process.

  1. Read the denial letter carefully. It tells you the specific reason for denial and the deadline to appeal. Mark the deadline in your phone immediately.
  2. File the appeal before the deadline. Most programs give you 30 to 90 days. File it even if you are not sure you will win. You lose nothing by appealing.
  3. Get help. Contact Legal Aid (find yours at lawhelp.org) or ask your caseworker for help with the appeal. Legal Aid attorneys win appeals at much higher rates than individuals filing alone.
  4. Bring new documentation. If the denial was for insufficient evidence, provide more. A letter from a shelter, a doctor's note, proof of homelessness — anything that supports your case.
  5. Show up to the hearing. If an appeal hearing is scheduled, attend it. Dress neatly. Bring all your documents. Be calm and factual. Many appeals are won simply because the applicant showed up and the original reviewer did not.

Navigating the Bureaucracy

The benefit system is not designed to be navigated by people in crisis. It requires patience, persistence, and organizational skills that homelessness actively degrades. Here are strategies that help.

  • Apply for everything at once. Do not wait to finish one application before starting another. Start all the clocks simultaneously.
  • Never miss an appointment. Missed appointments can restart your entire application process. Set multiple alarms.
  • Appeal every denial. Many initial denials are reversed on appeal.
  • Ask for a supervisor. If a frontline worker says you do not qualify, politely ask to speak with their supervisor. Frontline workers sometimes apply rules incorrectly.
  • Use legal aid. Free legal services exist in every state. They can help with benefit appeals, housing disputes, and ID replacement. Find yours at lawhelp.org.
✔ Practical Tip

Keep a "benefits binder" — a waterproof folder or gallon ziplock bag with copies of every document you need: ID, Social Security card, birth certificate, benefit letters, appointment notices. Losing these documents can set you back months. Guard this folder like cash.

Key Takeaways
  • Call 211 first. It is the directory for every local benefit and service.
  • Apply for everything on the checklist. SNAP, Medicaid, unemployment, GA, Lifeline phone, Section 8. Start all clocks at once.
  • USPS General Delivery is free. Any post office will hold mail for you for 30 days.
  • Get a caseworker. They know programs you have never heard of. Call 211 or visit a shelter.
  • Appeal every denial. Legal Aid at lawhelp.org wins appeals at much higher rates.
  • Veterans: call 1-877-424-3838. Separate, better-funded system exists for you.
  • Learned helplessness is real. Past failures do not mean current applications will fail.

Tax Filing When Homeless

You still have to file taxes. Homelessness does not exempt you. And if you lost your job mid-year, you almost certainly overpaid through payroll withholding. That means the IRS owes you money. File and get it back.

The Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

If you earned any money this year — even a few thousand dollars — you may qualify for the EITC. This is free money from the federal government. Not a loan. Not a deduction. A direct payment. The amount depends on your income and number of kids: $600 for single adults with no children, up to $7,430 for families with three or more kids. You must file a tax return to claim it. If you earned anything at all, file.

How to File Without an Address

The IRS does not require a permanent home address. You have options.

  • Shelter address. Use the address of the shelter where you stay or receive mail. The IRS accepts it.
  • USPS General Delivery. Put your local post office's address with "General Delivery" as your street address. Pick up any IRS correspondence with your photo ID.
  • A friend's address. Any address where you can receive mail works on a tax return. It does not have to be where you live.

Free Tax Preparation: VITA

Volunteer Income Tax Assistance (VITA) provides free tax prep for people earning under $67,000. Trained volunteers do your return at no cost. Find a VITA site at irs.gov/vita or call 211. Many libraries, community centers, and shelters host VITA clinics from January through April. Bring your W-2, photo ID, and Social Security card.

Getting Your Refund

Choose direct deposit. Send your refund to a Chime account, Cash App, or any prepaid debit card with a routing number. Do not choose a paper check. You may not have a mailbox when it arrives, and a lost refund check takes 6 to 8 weeks to reissue. Direct deposit hits in 10 to 21 days.

IRS Free File

If your income was under $84,000, you can file your federal return for free at irs.gov/freefile. Several partnered software companies offer guided filing at no cost. You need your W-2, your Social Security number, and about 30 minutes.

Your W-2

Your former employer must send your W-2 by January 31. If you changed addresses and did not update it with HR, call your old employer and ask them to mail it to your current mailing address. If they refuse or you cannot reach them, file IRS Form 4852 (Substitute for Form W-2) using your last pay stub to estimate your earnings. The IRS also has your W-2 data — you can request a Wage and Income Transcript at irs.gov or by calling 1-800-908-9946.

COBRA vs. Medicaid

You lost your job. Your employer-sponsored health insurance ended. You have two paths. One costs hundreds of dollars a month. The other is free. Here is how to decide.

COBRA: What It Is

COBRA is your legal right to continue your employer's health insurance for up to 18 months after losing your job. Same plan, same doctors, same coverage. The catch: you pay the full premium yourself. Your employer was covering 70–80% of the cost. Now you pay 100%. For an individual, that is $400 to $700 per month. For a family, $1,000 to $2,000 per month. Most homeless people cannot afford this. You have 60 days from your coverage end date to elect COBRA, and it is retroactive to the day your employer coverage stopped.

Medicaid: What It Is

Medicaid is free health insurance for low-income adults. In the 40 states that expanded Medicaid under the ACA, zero income means automatic qualification. No premiums. No copays for most services. No deductibles. Apply at healthcare.gov, at your local DHS office, or by calling 1-800-318-2596. In expansion states, coverage can start immediately.

The Strategic Move

Skip COBRA. Apply for Medicaid immediately. Save $400 to $700 per month. That money is better spent on food, transportation, and saving for housing. Medicaid covers doctor visits, prescriptions, mental health, and emergency care. For most homeless people, Medicaid provides everything COBRA does at zero cost.

The Exception

If you are in the middle of active treatment — chemotherapy, surgical follow-up, specialist care for a serious condition — and your doctors do not accept Medicaid, COBRA keeps your existing network. It may be worth paying for one or two months to finish treatment with your current providers. After treatment is complete, drop COBRA and switch to Medicaid. Do not pay $600 per month for insurance you do not need.

Special Enrollment Period

Job loss triggers a 60-day Special Enrollment Period for ACA marketplace plans at healthcare.gov. If you earn some income but too much for Medicaid, marketplace plans with subsidies may cost $0 to $50 per month depending on your income. Apply within 60 days of losing your employer coverage.

Retroactive Coverage

Some states offer retroactive Medicaid that covers medical expenses from up to 3 months before your application date. If you have unpaid medical bills from the gap between losing your job and applying, Medicaid may cover them. Apply immediately. Every day you wait is a day of potential coverage you lose.

Benefits Application Checklist

Apply Immediately (Week 1)

  • SNAP (food stamps): state DHS website or office, request EXPEDITED if under $150 cash
  • Medicaid: healthcare.gov or 1-800-318-2596, free in expansion states
  • Unemployment: state workforce website, file day of layoff

Apply in Week 2

  • Lifeline phone program: lifelinesupport.org (free/discounted phone)
  • LIHEAP utility assistance: call 211 (if you still have utility bills)
  • Local emergency assistance: call 211, ask about rent/deposit help

If Applicable

  • WIC: if pregnant or have children under 5
  • TANF: if you have minor children
  • SSI/SSDI: if you have a disability (apply at ssa.gov or local SSA office)
  • Veterans: call 1-877-4AID-VET for VA homeless programs
  • Section 8 / housing waitlist: contact local housing authority
  • Coordinated entry: call 211 or visit shelter for VI-SPDAT assessment

Documents to Bring

  • Photo ID (or explain you don’t have one — they can’t deny you)
  • Social Security number (memorized is fine if no card)
  • Proof of income or no income (pay stubs, termination letter, or self-declaration)
  • Proof of residency or homelessness (shelter letter, self-declaration)
  • Bank statements if available
