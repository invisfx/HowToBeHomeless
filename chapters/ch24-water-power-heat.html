Water, Power, and Heat

The three resources housed people take for granted that you now need to produce yourself.

In a house, you turn a tap and water comes out. You plug into a wall and electricity flows. You adjust a thermostat and the temperature changes. On the street, you produce all three of these resources yourself, or you go without. This chapter is the operations manual for water acquisition and purification, portable power generation, and heat management — the three pillars of physical sustainability outside the built environment.

Each of these resources follows the same strategic pattern: know where to find it, know how to store it, know how to produce it yourself when the easy sources dry up. The person who can reliably access clean water, keep a phone charged, and stay warm through a cold night has solved the three problems that most commonly drive people off the street and into shelters they would rather avoid or emergency rooms they cannot afford.

Some of what follows is simple and immediately applicable. Some of it is more advanced and only relevant if you are in a long-term outdoor situation or living in a vehicle. Read what applies to your circumstances and bookmark the rest — your situation may change.

Water

Clean water is the most fundamental survival resource. You can go weeks without food. You can go three days without water — less in heat. And contaminated water can make you sicker than no water at all. Understanding how to find, store, and purify water is essential knowledge.

Water Filtration

Three approaches, each with trade-offs:

  • Sawyer Squeeze ($25): Filters bacteria and protozoa. Attaches to standard water bottles. Filters up to 100,000 gallons before replacement. The best balance of cost, weight, and effectiveness for most situations.
  • LifeStraw ($15): Personal filter you drink through directly. Lighter and cheaper than the Sawyer, but less versatile — you cannot filter water into a container for later use. Best as a backup or ultralight option.
  • Chemical treatment: Iodine tablets ($8 for 50) or household bleach (2 drops per liter, wait 30 minutes). Lighter than any filter, effective against bacteria and viruses, but leaves a taste and does not remove sediment. Bleach must be unscented, regular strength (8.25% sodium hypochlorite).

Finding Water

Exterior spigots on commercial buildings, churches, and parks are the most reliable free water sources in urban environments. Time of day matters: early morning and late evening minimize the chance of confrontation. Gas stations and fast food restaurants have bathroom sinks. Libraries have water fountains. Many cities have public drinking fountains in parks, though many are shut off in winter.

For bulk water storage, collapsible water jugs (available at camping stores for $5–10) hold 2–5 gallons and fold flat when empty. In a pinch, any clean container works: repurposed juice jugs, soda bottles, even heavy-duty ziplock bags.

Purification by Boiling

Boiling is the most reliable purification method when you have the means. Bring water to a rolling boil for a minimum of one minute. At elevations above 6,500 feet, boil for three minutes. Boiling kills bacteria, viruses, and protozoa. It does not remove chemical contaminants or sediment — if the water is cloudy, filter it through a cloth first.

Signs of Contaminated Water

Do not drink water that has unusual color, odor, or surface film. Stagnant water near industrial sites, agricultural runoff areas, or downstream from mining operations may contain chemicals that filtration and boiling cannot remove. When in doubt, find another source.

Power

Solar Panels

Portable solar panels in the 10–20W range are a game-changer for anyone spending significant time outdoors. A 20W panel can charge a phone in 2–3 hours of direct sunlight and can keep a battery pack topped off indefinitely. Position the panel perpendicular to the sun and adjust it throughout the day for maximum output. Overcast days produce 10–25% of full-sun output — still useful, but plan accordingly.

Generators

Generators are only practical for vehicle dwellers or established camps. They produce significant noise, which attracts attention you probably do not want. Propane generators are safer and less smelly than gasoline models. Never run a generator inside an enclosed space — carbon monoxide kills without warning. Consider the legal and social implications: many jurisdictions prohibit generators in public spaces, and the noise can get you reported and relocated.

Battery Banks and Vehicle Systems

Size your battery bank to your needs. A 10,000 mAh pack handles a phone. A 20,000 mAh pack handles a phone and a headlamp for a week. Charge your highest-priority device first. For vehicle dwellers: running accessories off your starter battery risks stranding you. A battery isolator ($30–$80) separates your house battery from your starter battery, letting you run lights, fans, and chargers without risking your ability to start the engine.

Heat

The Layering System

Cold management is not about one warm garment — it is about a system. Three layers, each with a specific job:

  • Base layer (wicking): Synthetic or wool, worn against skin. Pulls moisture away from your body. Cotton is the enemy — it absorbs moisture and holds it against your skin, accelerating heat loss.
  • Mid layer (insulation): Fleece, down, or synthetic fill. Traps body heat in dead air space. This is where most of your warmth comes from.
  • Outer layer (shell): Wind and waterproof. Blocks wind chill and keeps rain off your insulating layers. A wet mid layer loses up to 90% of its insulating value.

Parabolic Reflectors

Parabolic reflectors focus sunlight to a point, generating enough concentrated heat to boil water, cook food, and sterilize tools. Commercial solar cookers are available, but you can build one from a discarded satellite dish lined with reflective tape or aluminum foil, or from a car windshield sunshade shaped into a cone. In direct sun, a well-made reflector can reach temperatures above 350°F at the focal point.

Fresnel Lens

A page-size Fresnel lens costs about $3 and fits flat in your bag. In direct sunlight, it can start a fire in seconds and boil a small container of water in minutes. It is one of the highest-utility, lowest-weight, lowest-cost items you can carry. Available at office supply stores, educational supply catalogs, or online.

Fire

Fire is the original survival tool, but on the street it comes with legal and safety constraints that vary dramatically by environment. In urban areas, open fires are usually illegal and will draw police attention. In rural or wooded areas, fire restrictions may be seasonal. Know your local rules before lighting anything.

When fire is an option: build it small, contain it in a fire ring or metal container, keep water nearby for extinguishing, and never leave it unattended. A Dakota fire hole (a small pit with an air channel) produces less visible flame and smoke than a surface fire, which matters when discretion is important.

Insulation Hacks

Cardboard is one of the most underrated insulation materials available. It has an R-value of 3–4 per inch, which means a few layers of flattened cardboard under your sleeping bag provide meaningful insulation from ground cold. Ground cold is the primary heat thief when sleeping outdoors — you lose more heat downward into the ground than you lose upward into the air.

  • Cardboard: Layer under your sleeping bag and pad. Free, widely available, effective. Replace when it gets wet.
  • Newspaper: Stuffed inside clothing, it provides surprisingly effective insulation. Crumple it rather than layering it flat — the air pockets are what insulate.
  • Mylar emergency blankets: These $1–$2 sheets reflect up to 90% of radiant body heat. Wrap one around your sleeping bag or tape one to the underside of your tarp as a radiant barrier. They are noisy and fragile but extraordinarily effective for their weight and cost.
  • Chemical hand warmers vs. reusable hand warmers: Disposable chemical warmers ($1 each, 8–10 hours of heat) are effective but expensive over time. Reusable sodium acetate warmers ($5–$10, reactivated by boiling) pay for themselves in a few weeks of cold weather.
Key Takeaways
  • Water: Sawyer Squeeze is the best all-around filter. Boil for 1 minute minimum. Do not drink discolored or odorous water.
  • Power: A 10–20W solar panel and a battery bank give you energy independence. Charge opportunistically.
  • Heat: Layer (wicking, insulating, shell). Insulate from the ground with cardboard. Cotton kills.
  • Fresnel lens: $3, weighs nothing, starts fires and boils water in direct sun.
  • Cardboard is your friend. R-value of 3–4 per inch. Free. Everywhere. Use it.
