Vehicle Living

Your car is the best shelter you can have. Here is how to make it work.

If you have a car, you have a massive advantage over most homeless people. Your vehicle is a lockable, private, mobile shelter. You can sleep in it, store your belongings in it, and drive it to wherever the resources are. Do not underestimate how much this changes your situation.

Vehicle living is not easy. You will deal with parking enforcement, condensation, temperature extremes, isolation, and the constant anxiety of being discovered. But compared to sleeping outside or in a crowded shelter, a car gives you control over your environment that nothing else can match.

The goal of this chapter is to help you set up your vehicle for sustainable living. Not camping for a weekend — living. That means sleeping, hygiene, food, storage, and stealth. It also means keeping the vehicle itself running, because the day your car breaks down is the day you lose your shelter.

People live in vehicles for weeks, months, and sometimes years. Some hold down jobs the entire time. Some save enough money to get back into housing. The ones who do it successfully treat their vehicle like a home and maintain strict routines around sleep, hygiene, and parking.

This chapter covers setup, parking strategy, legal issues, daily routines, and what to do when things go wrong. Read it all. The details matter — the difference between getting a ticket and sleeping undisturbed is often a single bad decision about where to park.

Setup: Making Your Vehicle Livable

Your first priority is turning your vehicle from a car into a sleeping space. This does not require money or tools. It requires clearing everything out, configuring a flat sleeping surface, and making the interior invisible from outside.

Sleeping Configuration

You need a flat surface long enough to lie down. How you get that depends on your vehicle. Here are the basics for each type.

  • Sedan: Fold back seat down, sleep diagonally. A foam pad cut to fit makes the difference between tolerable and unbearable.
  • SUV/Hatchback: Remove or fold all rear seats. You have a platform. Use it.
  • Van: The gold standard. Enough room for a real sleeping setup, storage, and some semblance of living space.

Storage and Organization

Everything must have a place. Clutter in a vehicle announces to anyone looking through the window that someone lives there.

Light Discipline

After dark, any light inside your vehicle is a beacon. Use a red headlamp, keep your phone brightness at minimum, and never use overhead dome lights. Window shades — reflective sun shades for the windshield, dark fabric for side windows — are essential.

Climate Management

Temperature is your biggest comfort challenge. A car is a metal box that gets extremely hot in summer and extremely cold in winter. In summer, crack windows for airflow but use mesh screens to keep insects out. In winter, your body heat and a good sleeping bag are your primary tools.

Where to Park

Where you park is the most important decision you make every night. The wrong spot gets you a knock on the window at 2 AM, a ticket, or a tow. The right spot lets you sleep undisturbed until morning.

  • Walmart parking lots: Some allow overnight parking. Call the specific store first.
  • Hospital parking garages: Visitors come and go at all hours. You blend in.
  • Residential streets: Rotate nightly. Never park in front of the same house twice in a row.
  • Rest stops: Many have posted time limits (2–4 hours). Know the rules before you settle in.
  • Church parking lots: Ask permission. Many churches are willing to help.

Rotation Strategy

Never park in the same spot two nights in a row. Keep a mental or written list of 5–7 spots and cycle through them. Predictability gets you noticed.

Scenario
Portland, November. Living in a 2009 Civic. It has been raining for 11 days.
  • Relentless wet cold
  • Condensation inside vehicle
  • Engine running burns gas and draws attention
  • Need to maintain employment
  • No gym membership yet

Covers condensation management, staying warm without running the engine, keeping employment while car-dwelling, and the psychological toll of relentless wet cold.

The key insight: condensation is your biggest enemy. Crack a window — even a quarter inch — or moisture from your breathing will soak everything by morning. A small silica gel container or a sock filled with cat litter absorbs interior moisture. Wipe surfaces before sleeping.

Legal Considerations

Vehicle dwelling exists in a legal gray area in most of the country. Some cities are tolerant. Others will ticket, tow, and even impound your vehicle. Know your local laws before you settle into a routine.

  • Overnight parking laws: Vary by city. Some ban sleeping in vehicles outright; others restrict it to certain areas.
  • Vehicle dwelling ordinances: Some cities have specific laws against "habitation" in a vehicle. Know your local code.
  • Registration and insurance: Keep both current. An expired tag is probable cause for a stop, and a stop leads to questions.

Hygiene, Cooking, and Daily Routine

Living in a car does not mean living like a car. You still need to shower, eat, and maintain a schedule. The challenge is doing all of this without a fixed address.

  • Showers: Gym membership ($10/month at Planet Fitness), truck stops ($12–15 per shower), community centers.
  • Cooking: A small propane stove outside the vehicle (never inside). Cold meals when cooking is not practical.
  • Routine: Build one and keep it. Wake at the same time, park in the same sequence, maintain structure.

Maintaining the Vehicle

Your vehicle is your shelter. When it breaks, you are outside. Preventive maintenance is not optional — it is survival infrastructure.

When the Car Breaks

The line between car living and car failure is the most dangerous transition in vehicle homelessness. Have a contingency plan before you need one.

🎒 Gear Note: Vehicle Living Essentials
  • Foam sleeping pad cut to fit your vehicle
  • Reflective windshield shade + dark fabric for side windows
  • Red headlamp (preserves night vision, low visibility from outside)
  • Silica gel packets or cat litter in a sock for moisture
  • Portable phone charger (your phone is your lifeline)
  • Basic tool kit: jumper cables, tire iron, duct tape
Key Takeaways
  • Setup matters. A well-organized vehicle is the difference between sustainable living and misery.
  • Light discipline. No interior lights after dark. Window covers are non-negotiable.
  • Rotate parking spots. 5–7 locations, cycled regularly. Predictability is your enemy.
  • Manage condensation. Crack a window, use moisture absorbers, wipe surfaces before sleep.
  • Maintain the vehicle. It is your shelter. Treat it accordingly.
