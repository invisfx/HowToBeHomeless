Prioritizing the Exit

Rapid rehousing, Housing First, waitlists, and saving money while you have almost none.

Everything in this book up to now has been about surviving. This chapter is about leaving. The distinction matters because survival mode and exit mode require different thinking, different priorities, and sometimes different sacrifices. Survival optimizes for tonight. Exit optimizes for six months from now. Your brain, shaped by weeks or months of immediate threat management, will resist this shift. That resistance is normal. It is also the thing you need to overcome.

The exit from homelessness is rarely a single dramatic moment. It is a series of small, unglamorous steps: filling out applications, showing up to appointments, saving eleven dollars when you want to spend it, and staying on a waitlist that feels like it leads nowhere. The people who exit are not always the ones with the most resources. They are often the ones who figured out which doors were actually open and walked through them before the doors closed again.

This chapter maps those doors. Some of them are well-known. Others are buried in bureaucratic language that most people never decode. All of them require you to be ready when your number comes up, because the window between "your name is next" and "we gave it to someone else" can be measured in hours.

The hardest truth about exiting homelessness is that the system was not designed with you in mind. It was designed by people who have never experienced what you are experiencing, funded by people who would prefer the problem to be invisible, and administered by people who are overwhelmed and underpaid. None of that is your fault. All of it is your problem. Understanding how the system actually works, rather than how it claims to work, is the single most valuable piece of knowledge in this chapter.

What Doors Open First

Not all housing pathways move at the same speed. Understanding which programs have the shortest timelines and which ones match your situation can save you months of waiting in the wrong line.

Rapid Rehousing

Rapid rehousing programs are designed to get you into permanent housing quickly and provide short-term rental assistance, typically three to twelve months. They are the fastest pathway for people who are recently homeless, have some income or income potential, and do not have severe disabilities or chronic health conditions. The philosophy is simple: get someone housed first, then address everything else.

These programs usually cover first month's rent and security deposit, provide limited ongoing rental subsidy, connect you with a case manager, and expect you to take over full rent within the assistance period. To access them, contact your local Continuum of Care, dial 211, or ask at any shelter intake.

Transitional Programs

Transitional housing bridges the gap between shelter and permanent housing. Programs typically last six to twenty-four months and combine housing with services like job training, substance use treatment, or mental health support. They are more structured than rapid rehousing but less permanent. Some require sobriety. Some require participation in programming. Read the rules before you commit.

Subsidized Housing

Section 8 vouchers and public housing are the long game. Waitlists in major cities can stretch three to ten years. In smaller cities and rural areas, the wait may be months. The key is getting on the list as early as possible, because your place in line starts the day you apply, not the day you become homeless.

Housing First Programs

Housing First is both a philosophy and a program model. The core idea is radical in its simplicity: give people housing without preconditions, then address the other issues. No sobriety requirement. No treatment compliance. No "housing readiness" assessment. Just a key and a door.

The research supporting Housing First is overwhelming. Programs in Houston, Salt Lake City, and across Canada have demonstrated that providing permanent housing first reduces emergency room visits, hospitalizations, incarceration, and overall public spending. More importantly, retention rates hover around eighty-five percent, meaning the vast majority of people placed in Housing First programs remain housed.

To find Housing First programs in your area, start with your local Continuum of Care, contact the National Alliance to End Homelessness, or ask a caseworker specifically about "permanent supportive housing" or "Housing First" options.

▶ Your Brain Right Now: The Waitlist and Learned Helplessness

Waitlists are psychologically corrosive. You apply, you wait, nothing happens. Your brain learns that action does not produce results. Psychologists call this learned helplessness, and it is one of the most dangerous psychological traps in homelessness. The antidote is not blind optimism. It is strategic action: apply to multiple programs simultaneously, follow up regularly, and keep records of every application and contact. Action itself, even when the results are delayed, preserves your sense of agency.

The practical takeaway: Apply everywhere. Follow up monthly. Keep a written log of every application, every call, every appointment. The log itself is evidence that you are still in the game.

The Waitlist Reality

Waitlists are not first-come, first-served. Most use a vulnerability scoring system called the VI-SPDAT (Vulnerability Index — Service Prioritization Decision Assistance Tool). Higher scores indicate higher vulnerability, and higher vulnerability gets housed first. This means that being "more stable" can paradoxically push you further down the list.

Understanding the scoring criteria can help you advocate for yourself. Factors include length of time homeless, number of emergency room visits, chronic health conditions, mental health diagnoses, and substance use history. If you have conditions that qualify, make sure they are documented and communicated to your assessor.

Housing Programs Explained

There are five major housing program types. They work differently, move at different speeds, and serve different populations. Most people apply to the wrong ones or apply to only one. Apply to all of them.

Rapid Rehousing (RRH)

Short-term rent subsidy, typically three to twelve months. You find the apartment. The program pays part of the rent while you stabilize income. This is the fastest pathway for people who lost housing recently and have some earning potential. The program usually covers first month, security deposit, and a declining rent subsidy. You are expected to take over full rent by the end. Contact your local Continuum of Care or dial 211 to find RRH in your area.

Permanent Supportive Housing (PSH)

Long-term housing with case management built in. Designed for people with chronic homelessness, disabilities, or serious mental health conditions. No time limit. A case manager checks in regularly, helps with benefits, medical appointments, and daily living. This is the gold standard for people with complex needs, but slots are limited and prioritized by vulnerability score.

Section 8 / Housing Choice Voucher

The voucher covers roughly 70% of rent. You pay 30% of your income. The waitlists are the problem. Average wait time nationally is two to three years. In cities like New York, Los Angeles, and Chicago, wait times stretch to seven to ten years. Some housing authorities close their waitlists entirely for years at a time. Get on every open list now. Your position starts the day you apply. Check HUD.gov for local housing authority contacts.

SSVF (Supportive Services for Veteran Families)

Veterans only. This is the fastest housing program in the country. It provides rapid rehousing, deposit assistance, case management, and employment help specifically for veterans and their families. If you are a veteran, call the VA homeless hotline at 1-877-4AID-VET (1-877-424-3838). Do not wait. This program moves in days, not months.

Housing First

No preconditions. No sobriety requirement. No treatment compliance. No "housing readiness" tests. You get a key and a door. Then services come to you. The research is overwhelming: Housing First has 85% retention rates. Houston housed 30,000 people this way. Salt Lake City cut chronic homelessness by 91% with this model. Ask for it by name. If your caseworker does not know about it, ask their supervisor.

The Waitlist Reality

Waitlists are not first-come, first-served. Most Continuums of Care use a vulnerability assessment called the VI-SPDAT. Higher scores mean higher vulnerability. Higher vulnerability gets housed first. This means being more functional can push you further down the list. It is frustrating. It is also the system you are working within.

Average Wait Times by Program

  • SSVF (veterans): Days to weeks. Fastest in the system.
  • Rapid Rehousing: Two weeks to three months, depending on local funding.
  • Permanent Supportive Housing: Three months to two years. Prioritized by vulnerability score.
  • Section 8 vouchers: Two to ten years. Some lists are closed entirely.
  • Public housing: One to five years. Shorter in rural areas, longer in cities.

How to Work the Waitlists

  • Get on multiple lists simultaneously. There is no rule against it. Apply to every open waitlist in your area and in nearby counties. Some people apply in three or four jurisdictions.
  • Check your position regularly. Call monthly. Ask: what is my position? Has anything changed? Some lists purge people who do not respond to contact attempts. Stay active.
  • Update your information. If your situation worsens — new medical diagnosis, disability, hospitalization, longer duration of homelessness — tell your assessor. Your vulnerability score can be reassessed. A higher score moves you up.
  • What bumps you up: documented disability, chronic health condition, mental health diagnosis, veteran status, family with children, domestic violence survivor status, longer duration of homelessness, frequent emergency room visits.
  • Keep records of everything. Every application date, every phone call, every contact name. If your name gets lost in the system, your records are your proof.
✔ Practical Tip

When you call to check your waitlist position, write down the date, the name of the person you spoke with, and what they told you. If a program later claims you did not respond or were not on the list, this log is your evidence. Keep it in your phone or in a small notebook.

Saving Money While Homeless

This is the hardest financial task you will ever face. Every dollar you earn is immediately claimed by a dozen urgent needs. Food, phone, laundry, hygiene, storage, transportation. The idea of saving seems absurd when you cannot meet today's needs. And yet, saving is what separates the people who exit when an opportunity appears from the people who watch it pass because they cannot cover a security deposit.

The target is $1,000. That covers a security deposit and part of first month's rent in most markets outside the coasts. It sounds impossible. It is not. Five dollars a day is $150 a month. In six months, that is $900. Seven months, $1,050. The math works if you are ruthless about it.

Step 1: Open a No-Fee Bank Account

Chime, Current, or Varo. No minimum balance. No monthly fees. No overdraft fees. Apply online with a phone. Direct deposit from a job or gig work goes straight in. This is not optional. Cash in your pocket gets spent. Cash in an account you do not look at accumulates.

Step 2: Automate Even $5

If you have any income, set up an auto-transfer of $5 per day or $35 per week into a savings account. Treat it like a bill. It is not optional spending money. It is your exit ticket. You will feel the pinch. That is the point. The pinch is temporary. Being unable to cover a deposit is not.

Step 3: Track Every Dollar

Use the notes app on your phone. Every morning, write down what you have. Every night, write down what you spent. Do this for one week and you will find $20 to $40 in spending you did not realize was happening. Convenience store food costs three to five times what grocery food costs. A $2 coffee every day is $60 a month. A pack of cigarettes a day is $200 to $350 a month depending on your state.

Step 4: Avoid the Spending Traps

  • Convenience store food. A gas station sandwich costs $6. A loaf of bread and peanut butter cost $5 and make ten sandwiches. Do the math every time.
  • Cigarettes. At $10 a pack, a pack-a-day habit costs $300 a month. That is your security deposit in three months. If you cannot quit, cut to half a pack.
  • Lottery tickets. The expected return on a $2 lottery ticket is about 50 cents. You are paying $1.50 for a fantasy. Put the $2 in your savings account instead.
  • Energy drinks. At $3 each, two a day is $180 a month. Make coffee at a day center or shelter for free.

The Math of Getting Housed

Here is what it actually costs to move into an apartment in 2026. These numbers are national averages. Your city may be higher or lower. Look up your local market on Zillow or Apartments.com.

The Baseline Cost

  • First month's rent: $800–$1,500 (national median for a one-bedroom is about $1,100)
  • Last month's rent: $800–$1,500 (not always required; ask)
  • Security deposit: Usually one month's rent, sometimes half
  • Application fee: $25–$75 per application (non-refundable)
  • Total move-in for a $1,000/month apartment: $2,000–$3,000
  • Total move-in for a $1,500/month apartment: $3,000–$4,500

The Income Requirement

Most landlords require gross income of 2.5 to 3 times the monthly rent. For a $1,000/month apartment, that means you need to show $2,500 to $3,000 per month in income. At $15/hour full-time, your gross is about $2,600. At $18/hour, about $3,120. Part-time work usually does not clear this bar alone. A co-signer or roommate can help.

What Assistance Programs Cover

  • Rapid rehousing: First month + deposit + declining rent subsidy (3–12 months)
  • Salvation Army: Grants up to $1,500 for deposit/first month
  • Catholic Charities: Emergency housing funds (varies by diocese)
  • Community Action Agencies: Deposit assistance, utility deposits, furniture vouchers
  • SSVF (veterans): Full move-in costs including deposit, first month, and furnishing

The gap: Assistance programs usually cover the upfront costs. What they do not cover is the ongoing rent after the subsidy ends, and the furnishing beyond basics. Your savings cover that gap. Every dollar you save while homeless is a dollar of runway once you are housed.

Key Takeaways
  • Five housing program types exist. Rapid Rehousing, PSH, Section 8, SSVF, Housing First. Apply to all that fit.
  • Waitlists are scored, not first-come. Document your vulnerabilities. Get reassessed if your situation changes.
  • Get on every open waitlist now. Multiple jurisdictions. Check monthly. Keep records.
  • Save $5/day. Open a no-fee bank account. Automate transfers. Track spending. Avoid convenience store traps.
  • Move-in costs $2,000–$4,500. Assistance programs cover much of this. Your savings cover the gap.
  • Target $1,000 in savings. It is the difference between being ready when your name comes up and watching the opportunity pass.
