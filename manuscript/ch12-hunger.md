# Chapter 12: Hunger {#ch12}

*What happens when you stop eating, how to manage it, and how not to die when you start again.*

Hunger is not an emergency. It feels like one, and your brain will treat it like one, but a healthy adult with access to water can survive thirty to forty days without food.

You are not going to die tonight because you missed dinner. You are not going to die tomorrow because you skipped every meal. Internalize that. It is the difference between rational decisions and panicked ones that cost you resources you cannot replace.

### Hunger on the Street Is Different

In a house, hunger is an inconvenience you resolve by opening the refrigerator. On the street, hunger is a compounding problem:

- It degrades your thinking
- It makes you irritable and impulsive
- It saps your energy for the tasks that get you off the street
- When food does finally appear, eating too much too fast after a prolonged period without can put you in the emergency room or kill you outright

That last point is not hyperbole. It has a clinical name — **refeeding syndrome** — and it has killed concentration camp survivors, hunger strikers, and homeless people who got their first real meal in a week.

## The Biology of Not Eating

Your body runs on glucose. When you eat, your digestive system breaks food down into glucose, which fuels your brain, muscles, and organs.

When you stop eating, your body does not shut down. It shifts fuel sources in a predictable sequence. Famine is not new to the human body. Your ancestors survived regular periods without food, and the biological machinery for handling it is still operational inside you.

### The Timeline

**12 hours — glucose drops, hunger peaks.**
Blood glucose drops. You get irritable. Concentration wavers. You may get a headache. Your stomach growls because ghrelin — the hunger hormone — is firing on schedule.

Your body is running through available glucose and tapping into glycogen (glucose stored in liver and muscles). You have roughly 1,600 to 2,000 calories worth of glycogen — enough for another 12 to 24 hours depending on activity level. Uncomfortable, but not dangerous.

**24 hours — glycogen depleted, transition begins.**
Your body begins **ketosis** — switching from glucose to ketone bodies produced by breaking down fat. This transition is why the first full day without food is often the hardest. Your body is between fuel sources.

Symptoms at this stage:

- Weakness, dizziness, brain fog
- Mood crash, zero patience
- Nausea on an empty stomach
- Breath may smell metallic or fruity (acetone byproduct of ketosis — normal)

**48 hours — full ketosis, hunger often decreases.**
Your body is now running on fat stores, converting them into ketone bodies. The counterintuitive part: hunger often decreases. The ghrelin signaling pattern shifts. Your body stops sending the acute hunger signals that made the first day miserable.

You may still think about food constantly, but the physical gnawing sensation often subsides. Many people report mental clarity during this stage — an evolutionary adaptation. When our ancestors were two days into a famine, they needed sharper thinking to find food, not foggy despair.

**72 hours and beyond — adapted fasting state.**

- Metabolic rate drops 10-15% to conserve energy
- Body prioritizes vital functions, reduces expenditure elsewhere
- Muscle protein conserved more aggressively than most people realize
- Your body preferentially burns fat first and switches to significant muscle catabolism only when fat stores are severely depleted (takes weeks, not days)

### The Calorie Math

A functioning adult needs roughly 1,200 to 1,500 calories per day at minimum for basic body functions — breathing, circulation, digestion, temperature regulation. Below that threshold, your body makes up the difference from stored reserves.

The 30-40 day figure is for an average-weight person. More body fat means significantly more runway.

### Calculate Your Own Runway

- **1 pound of body fat = 3,500 calories of stored energy**
- **Your body burns roughly 1,500-2,000 calories per day at rest** (your basal metabolic rate)
- Divide your stored calories by your daily burn rate

Examples:

- **20 lbs of excess fat:** 70,000 stored calories / 1,500 per day = **~47 days**
- **30 lbs of excess fat:** 105,000 stored calories / 1,500 per day = **~70 days**
- **50 lbs of excess fat:** 175,000 stored calories / 1,500 per day = **~117 days**

These numbers assume water intake. Without water, none of this matters — dehydration kills in 3-5 days regardless of fat stores.

**You have time.** Far more time than your panicking brain tells you. Do the math with your own body. Starvation is not the emergency you think it is. Dehydration is. Cold is. Violence is. Missing meals for a few days is something your body was built to handle.

### What Prolonged Fasting Does to You

None of this means going without food is good for you. Prolonged fasting:

- Degrades your immune system
- Impairs wound healing
- Weakens bones and teeth
- Causes organ damage over extended periods

Hunger is survivable. Knowing the timeline helps you make rational decisions about when to spend resources on food, when to wait, and when the situation has become medically dangerous.

**The first forty-eight hours are the worst. After that, it gets easier.** Knowing this in advance does not eliminate the suffering, but it gives you a framework to endure it.

## Managing Hunger with No Food

When you cannot eat, you manage. You do not fix hunger without food — you reduce its impact on your ability to function. Every trick in this section buys time, reduces discomfort, and keeps your decision-making intact until food becomes available.

### If You Have Some Food: Eat Protein, Not Carbs

When food is scarce, what you eat matters more than how much. Carbohydrates — bread, rice, crackers, candy, chips — spike your blood sugar fast. Insulin surges to bring it down. Blood sugar crashes below where it started. You feel hungrier than before you ate.

Protein breaks this cycle. It digests slowly, keeps blood sugar stable for hours, and triggers stronger satiety hormones than carbs do. A can of tuna keeps you full for 4-6 hours. A bag of chips keeps you full for 45 minutes then makes you hungrier.

If you can only eat once today, make it protein:

- **Peanut butter** — protein + fat, keeps you full the longest
- **Canned tuna or chicken** — pure protein, $1-2 per can
- **Eggs** — cheap, protein-dense, available at every gas station (hard-boiled)
- **Beef jerky** — expensive ($8-15) but calorie-for-calorie keeps hunger away longest
- **Nuts** — trail mix, peanuts, almonds. Fat + protein combo

Avoid spending your last dollars on:

- Bread, rice, or ramen alone (blood sugar spike → crash → worse hunger)
- Candy bars or pastries (worst possible choice — massive insulin spike, hungry again in an hour)
- Soda or juice (liquid sugar, zero satiety)
- Chips (high salt tricks you into thinking you're full, but empty calories and fast crash)

One spoonful of peanut butter will carry you further than a whole bag of chips. When you are managing a long stretch without food, protein is your best weapon.

### Water First. Always Water First.

Your brain is bad at distinguishing between hunger and thirst. Dehydration amplifies every symptom of hunger — headache, irritability, fatigue, dizziness.

Drink 8 to 16 ounces of water as soon as you feel hunger building. In roughly half of cases, this alone will reduce the immediate urgency.

- **Cold water** is slightly more effective than warm for suppressing appetite (your body burns a small amount of energy warming it)
- Any temperature works — the goal is volume in the stomach
- A full stomach sends stretch signals to the brain that partially override hunger signals

### Salt Water Is Better

Dissolve a small pinch of salt — less than a quarter teaspoon — in a glass of water. The sodium:

- Helps your body retain water longer
- Prevents electrolyte depletion from drinking large volumes of plain water on an empty stomach
- Triggers a mild satiety response

**Where to get free salt:** Every McDonald's, Burger King, Wendy's, and Taco Bell has salt packets at the condiment station. Take a handful. They cost nothing, weigh nothing, and are among the most useful survival items you can carry. Keep 10-15 packets in your bag at all times.

### Hot Drinks Trick Your Stomach

Hot water, coffee, and tea work better than cold water. The warmth triggers a mild digestive response your brain interprets as "something is being processed."

Free coffee sources:

- Most shelters offer it
- Many churches have it available
- Gas stations will sometimes give a cup to someone who asks

The caffeine provides a temporary energy boost and appetite suppression. Even plain hot water from a gas station coffee machine's water spigot helps. Slowly drinking something warm provides psychological comfort that cold water does not.

### Sleep Through It

Every hour you are asleep is an hour you are not suffering through hunger and not burning extra calories from activity. If you have a safe enough spot and a warm enough setup, and food is not available until tomorrow — go to sleep.

You will wake up deeper into ketosis, past the worst of the ghrelin spikes, and you will have conserved energy. Sleep is free calories saved.

### Chew Gum

A pack of gum costs a dollar and lasts for days. The physical act of chewing tricks your digestive system into a preparatory state that partially satisfies the mechanical craving for food.

**Mint-flavored gum** is better than fruit-flavored for appetite suppression — the menthol creates a mild analgesic effect that dampens hunger signaling.

### Brush Your Teeth

If you have a toothbrush and toothpaste (and you should — see Chapter 13), brush your teeth when hunger becomes intense. The mint suppresses appetite for 30 to 60 minutes through the same menthol mechanism as gum, and the clean-mouth sensation creates a psychological disinclination to eat. Small trick. Works reliably.

### Tighten Your Belt

Old military trick. A snug belt or waistband pressing against your abdomen sends mild pressure signals to the same stretch receptors that fire when your stomach is full. Does not fool your body entirely, but reduces the intensity of hunger pangs.

Cinch your belt one notch tighter than comfortable when hunger peaks.

### Breathe Through the Waves

Hunger is not constant. It comes in waves driven by ghrelin release — typically every 20-30 minutes, each wave lasting 5-10 minutes at peak intensity before subsiding.

**Box breathing** when a wave hits:

1. Inhale for 4 seconds
2. Hold for 4 seconds
3. Exhale for 4 seconds
4. Hold for 4 seconds
5. Repeat for 2-3 minutes

This activates the parasympathetic nervous system, reduces cortisol, and blunts the acute edge of the hunger spike. You are not eliminating hunger. You are riding out the wave until it passes. It will pass.

### Avoid Food Smells

Harder than it sounds on the street, but make the effort. Walking past a bakery or burger joint when you are 24 hours into a fast is not character-building. It is self-sabotage.

- Food smells trigger ghrelin release and can restart the hunger cycle even after it has begun to subside
- If your usual route takes you past restaurants, change your route
- If you are near a food court in a mall, move to a different part of the building
- Do not test your willpower unnecessarily — willpower is a finite resource, and hunger depletes it faster than almost anything else

### Foraging — With Extreme Caution

Foraging is possible in most environments but comes with real risks. These common plants are edible:

- **Dandelion greens** — the common lawn weed with jagged leaves and yellow flowers. Edible raw. Mildly nutritious. Grows in virtually every park, yard, and roadside strip in America.
- **Clover** — both the leaves and the small white or pink flowers. Slightly sweet.
- **Plantain weed** — not the banana relative, but the low-growing broadleaf plant found in sidewalk cracks and lawns. Edible with mild anti-inflammatory properties.
- **Wood sorrel** — looks like small clover with heart-shaped leaves. Sour lemon-like taste. Provides some vitamin C.

> **CRITICAL WARNING:** Misidentification kills. Do not eat any plant you cannot identify with absolute certainty.
>
> - Poison hemlock looks similar to wild carrot
> - Water hemlock is one of the most toxic plants in North America — can kill within hours
> - Nightshade berries look appetizing
> - Do NOT eat mushrooms unless you are a trained mycologist
> - Do NOT eat berries unless you can identify the species with certainty
>
> A handful of wrongly identified berries can put you in the emergency room or the morgue. That is considerably worse than being hungry for another 12 hours.

### Stay Busy

Boredom amplifies hunger the same way it amplifies pain. Your brain, with nothing else to focus on, fixates on the hunger signal and turns up the volume.

Occupy your prefrontal cortex:

- Walk
- Organize your gear
- Read something
- Do push-ups
- Plan your next steps

Idle time is when hunger becomes unbearable. Occupied time is when it fades into the background.

## Weight Loss

You will lose weight on the street. Expect it. The pattern below helps you distinguish between expected weight loss and dangerous weight loss.

### The Pattern

**First week of significant caloric restriction or fasting:** 1-2 pounds per day. Most of this is water weight. Glycogen is stored with water molecules attached — as you burn glycogen, that water is released and excreted.

Do not be alarmed by rapid weight loss in the first week. Do not be encouraged by it either. It is water. It comes back when you resume eating.

**After the first week:** roughly half a pound to one pound per day, depending on starting size, activity level, and how much you are eating. This is now primarily fat loss with some muscle catabolism mixed in.

**Over a month** of the kind of inconsistent, inadequate eating that characterizes homelessness, losing 20-30 pounds is common.

### The Danger Threshold

**Watch for 10% of your body weight lost in a month.**

- A 180-pound person losing 18 pounds in a month is approaching the danger zone
- A 140-pound person losing 14 pounds in a month is in it

Beyond this threshold, your body is stripping resources from systems it considers non-essential — immune response, bone density, dental health, cognitive function — to keep your heart and lungs running. You are surviving, but you are degrading.

### What Breaks Down

- **Immune system** drops after 2-3 weeks of significant caloric restriction. Every cold, cut, and infection that a housed and fed person shakes off in days becomes a multi-week ordeal. A simple cold becomes bronchitis. A small cut gets infected.
- **Teeth and gums** weaken from malnutrition. Acid reflux from an empty stomach erodes tooth enamel. Losing teeth on the street is common and devastating — it affects eating, job interviews, and self-image. Brush your teeth. Use fluoride toothpaste. Protect your teeth like the survival tools they are.
- **Joints** hurt as weight loss reduces padding. Your knees, hips, and ankles bear the impact of walking all day on concrete, and without proper nutrition, cartilage and synovial fluid degrade. Worse if you are sleeping on hard surfaces.
- **Skin** may become loose if you lose 30+ pounds quickly. Cosmetic, not medical, but it affects how your clothes fit and how you feel about your body.

### The Social Impact

The visible changes happen faster than you expect.

- Friends and family who see you after a month may be shocked
- Co-workers from a previous life may not recognize you
- Some people will express concern; others will avoid you
- Some will assume drugs
- The weight loss becomes another stigma layered on top of homelessness, compounding isolation

### Get Clothes That Fit

Get your clothes from thrift stores, not from punching extra holes in your belt. Clothes that fit properly make you look employed, which matters for:

- Job interviews
- Interactions with police
- Your own psychological state

**Goodwill, Salvation Army, and church clothing closets** provide free or near-free clothing. A pair of pants that fits you at your current weight is worth more than expensive pants cinched with a belt that makes you look like you are wearing a sack. As you lose weight, rotate your wardrobe through thrift stores. Trade in what no longer fits for what does.

## Diarrhea and Digestive Issues

Nobody wants to read this section. Everybody on the street needs it. Digestive problems during periods of hunger and inconsistent eating are common, and they create a dangerous spiral that can land you in the emergency room.

### Bile Acid Diarrhea

The most common and least understood digestive issue during periods of not eating. Your liver continuously produces bile to aid in fat digestion. When you eat, the bile is used and reabsorbed. When you do not eat, the bile accumulates in your small intestine and eventually floods into your large intestine, where it acts as a laxative.

Urgent, watery diarrhea that is often bright yellow or greenish — the color of bile. Hits without warning. Can hit multiple times per day during the first few days of not eating.

Your liver doing its job in the absence of food. Not an infection. Not food poisoning. Resolves when you resume eating.

### Ketosis Transition Diarrhea

Occurs in the first 2-3 days as your body shifts from glucose to fat metabolism. The metabolic shift alters your gut flora and intestinal chemistry. Some people get constipation. Others get diarrhea. Both are normal and self-limiting — resolves within 2-4 days as your gut adapts.

### Refeeding Diarrhea

Happens when you eat again after a period without food. Your digestive system has downregulated — reduced enzyme production and slowed intestinal movement because there was nothing to process. When food suddenly arrives, your gut is not ready.

Cramping, bloating, diarrhea. This is why eating slowly and in small amounts after a fast matters — not just for refeeding syndrome risk, but for basic digestive function.

### The Dehydration Spiral

This is the real danger:

1. Diarrhea causes fluid loss
2. Fluid loss causes dehydration
3. Dehydration causes weakness and confusion
4. Weakness makes it harder to get to a water source
5. Confusion impairs your judgment about how serious the situation is
6. Each cycle makes the next one worse

A person who started with hunger and developed bile acid diarrhea can end up severely dehydrated within 24-48 hours, especially in hot weather or if already mildly dehydrated when the diarrhea started.

### Treatment

**The BRAT diet** — bananas, rice, applesauce, toast — is the standard recovery protocol. Bland, binding, easy to digest.

- A single banana and a cup of rice will do more for your gut than a full meal
- If you cannot get BRAT foods, any bland starchy food helps — plain bread, crackers, plain pasta

**Imodium (loperamide)** is available over the counter for roughly $4 for a generic box at Walmart. It slows intestinal motility.

- **Dosage:** 2 mg (one pill) at onset, then 1 mg after each loose stool, up to 8 mg per day
- **Do not exceed this dose.** Loperamide in large doses is dangerous and can cause cardiac events
- Carry some if you can afford it

### When to Go to the ER

Go immediately if you experience:

- **Blood in your stool** — red or black and tarry
- **Diarrhea lasting more than 3 days** without improvement
- **Signs of severe dehydration:**
  - No urine output for 8+ hours
  - Dark brown urine
  - Rapid heartbeat at rest
  - Sunken eyes
  - Inability to stand without dizziness
  - Dry mouth with no saliva production

Do not wait. Severe dehydration kills faster than hunger.

### The Embarrassment Factor

Finding a bathroom when you are homeless is already difficult. Finding one urgently and repeatedly with diarrhea is a logistical nightmare.

- Public restrooms are scarce in many cities
- Fast food restaurants increasingly require a purchase
- Libraries have limited hours
- The fear of soiling yourself in public is real

Do not let embarrassment kill you. Use the bathroom resources outlined in Chapter 13. If you cannot find a bathroom, find a private outdoor location and clean up as well as you can. Dignity matters, but survival matters more.

## Refeeding Syndrome — This Can Kill You

> **WARNING:** This section describes a medical condition that can be fatal. If you have not eaten for more than two days and you experience chest pain, difficulty breathing, confusion, swelling in your extremities, or seizures after eating, call 911 immediately. Tell the dispatcher "I think I have refeeding syndrome." This phrase will get you treated faster than trying to explain your situation from scratch.

### What It Is

Refeeding syndrome is what happens when a starved body receives food too quickly. Not a stomach problem. A metabolic crisis that affects your heart, lungs, and brain. It can kill within hours of onset.

### The Mechanism

When you fast for an extended period, your body depletes its intracellular stores of electrolytes — primarily phosphate, potassium, and magnesium. These electrolytes are critical for heart rhythm, breathing muscles, and brain function. Your body compensates by adjusting to a lower baseline. You feel terrible, but your heart keeps beating because your body has calibrated to the depleted state.

When you suddenly eat — especially carbohydrate-heavy food — your body releases a surge of insulin. Insulin drives glucose into cells, but it also drives phosphate, potassium, and magnesium from your bloodstream into your cells.

Your total body stores are already depleted. The shift causes a precipitous drop in blood levels of all three:

- **Your heart** depends on potassium to maintain its rhythm
- **Your breathing muscles** depend on phosphate for energy production
- **Your brain** depends on all three to function

When their blood levels crash, things go wrong fast.

### Symptoms

- Heart palpitations or irregular heartbeat
- Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
- Confusion or disorientation
- Swelling in hands, feet, or legs (edema from fluid shifts)
- Muscle weakness that may be severe
- In extreme cases: seizures and cardiac arrest

### Who Is at Highest Risk

The highest risk period is after 5+ days without food. Refeeding syndrome has been documented after as few as 2-3 days of fasting in people who were already malnourished or had depleted electrolyte stores.

Higher risk groups:

- Chronic alcoholics
- People with anorexia
- People who have been eating poorly for weeks before a complete fast

### This Is Not Theoretical

Allied forces liberated concentration camps and gave survivors full meals. Thousands died in the days following — not from starvation, but from the food that was supposed to save them.

The same mechanism kills hunger strikers who break their fasts too quickly, and homeless people who eat a large meal at a shelter after days without food.

### Prevention

Prevention is straightforward but requires discipline at a moment when your body is screaming at you to eat everything in sight.

**If you have not eaten for 2 or more days:**

1. Start with **half a sandwich.** Not a whole sandwich. Half.
2. Eat it slowly over 10-15 minutes
3. **Stop. Wait 2 hours.**
4. If you feel fine — no chest pain, no palpitations, no breathing difficulty, no swelling, no confusion — eat the other half
5. Wait another 2 hours
6. Eat another small portion
7. Continue this pattern: small portions every 2-3 hours, gradually increasing over 4 days

### What to Eat When Breaking a Fast

- **Start with** protein and complex carbs — peanut butter on bread, cheese and crackers, rice and beans, canned tuna
- **Avoid simple sugars** — no candy bars, no soda, no energy drinks, no fruit juice
- Simple sugars cause the largest insulin spike, which drives the most severe electrolyte shift
- **A Snickers bar after 5 days of fasting is more dangerous than a turkey sandwich**
- Protein is safest because it triggers the lowest insulin response

### Get Electrolytes In First

If possible, get electrolytes into your body before you eat:

- A glass of salt water (quarter teaspoon of salt in a liter of water) provides sodium
- A banana for potassium
- A cheap electrolyte drink

The electrolytes will not fully prevent refeeding syndrome, but they partially buffer the shift by raising your blood levels before the insulin-driven crash.

### The 4-Day Refeeding Schedule

Clinical standard:

- **Day 1:** 500-800 calories in small portions spread across the day
- **Day 2:** 800-1,200 calories
- **Day 3:** 1,200-1,500 calories
- **Day 4+:** Normal eating

**If at any point** you feel your heart racing, have difficulty breathing, become confused, or experience swelling — stop eating and get to an emergency room.

### The Hardest Part

After days without food, when food finally appears, every instinct demands that you eat as much as possible as fast as possible. Override that instinct.

Eating slowly after a fast is not about willpower. It is about staying alive.

## Sleep and Hunger

Hunger and sleep feed a vicious cycle on the street.

### The 3 a.m. Blood Sugar Crash

When blood glucose drops, your body releases cortisol. Low blood sugar meant food was scarce for your ancestors, and cortisol triggered alertness to drive hunting or foraging.

For you, lying in a sleeping bag at 2 a.m., it means you wake up with a pounding heart and a racing mind and cannot get back to sleep. Common among people who are not eating enough.

### The Fix: Eat Fat or Protein Before Sleep

If you have any food at all, eat something with fat or protein before you try to sleep:

- A spoonful of peanut butter
- A handful of nuts
- A piece of cheese
- A glass of milk

Fat and protein digest slowly, providing stable blood glucose over 4-6 hours. Carbohydrates spike and crash within 2-3 hours. This does not eliminate the overnight crash, but it pushes it later, giving you more consecutive hours of sleep.

### The Sleep Debt Spiral

Sleep debt compounds with hunger:

- **Day 1** of poor sleep from hunger — tired and irritable but functional
- **Day 3** — decision-making is impaired. Research shows 3 days of significant sleep deprivation degrades cognitive function to the equivalent of being legally drunk
- **Day 5** — hallucinations become possible

The combination of hunger, sleep deprivation, and stress creates a neurochemical environment where your brain begins generating sensory input that is not there.

### Micro-Naps as Countermeasure

A 10-20 minute nap — true sleep, not just closing your eyes — restores a surprising amount of cognitive function. Even brief sleep allows your brain to flush adenosine (the chemical that builds up during waking hours and makes you feel tired).

If hunger keeps waking you at night, get 2-3 micro-naps during the day:

- A bench in a park
- A chair in a library
- A seat on a bus

Set an alarm on your phone if you have one. The naps will not fully compensate for lost nighttime sleep, but they will keep your cognitive function above the threshold where bad decisions start happening.

## DIY Electrolyte Management

Electrolytes are minerals in your blood that carry electrical charges. They regulate your heartbeat, muscle contractions, nerve signaling, and fluid balance.

The three that matter most during hunger: **sodium, potassium, and magnesium.** When you are not eating, your body depletes these because it is not getting them from food, and because fasting increases urinary excretion of all three.

### Salt Water — Simplest and Cheapest

One quarter teaspoon of table salt in one liter of water provides sodium chloride — the most acutely important electrolyte for preventing dehydration and maintaining blood pressure. Costs nothing if you use salt packets from fast food restaurants.

### WHO Oral Rehydration Solution

One of the most important medical discoveries of the twentieth century. Has saved tens of millions of lives worldwide.

**The formula:**

- 1/4 teaspoon salt
- 6 teaspoons sugar
- 1 liter water

The glucose-sodium co-transport mechanism allows your intestines to absorb water far more efficiently than plain water or salt water alone. If you are dehydrated and nauseous and cannot keep plain water down, this solution may stay down and absorb.

Mix it in any clean container. Tastes mildly salty and slightly sweet, like flat sports drink. Works better than any commercial electrolyte product you can buy. Costs nearly nothing. **Memorize this recipe.**

### Signs You Need Electrolytes

- **Muscle cramps** (especially calves, feet, hands) — sodium or potassium depletion
- **Muscle twitching** (involuntary contractions in eyelids, fingers, legs) — magnesium depletion
- **Dizziness when standing up** (orthostatic hypotension) — sodium depletion dropping blood pressure
- **Heart flutter or palpitations** — potassium or magnesium depletion. Take this seriously.
- **Tingling or numbness** (fingers, toes, around mouth) — calcium or magnesium depletion

### Free Electrolyte Sources

**Salt packets** from fast food restaurants are free survival gear. Ten packets weigh almost nothing, fit in any pocket, and contain enough sodium to make several liters of electrolyte solution. Collect them every time you pass through a fast food restaurant. Sugar packets are equally available and equally free. Together, they give you the raw materials for WHO oral rehydration solution anywhere you can find clean water.

**Potassium sources:**

- **Bananas** — cheapest accessible source at roughly $0.25 each. A single banana provides about 400 mg of potassium (roughly 10% of daily requirement)
- **Potatoes** — potassium-rich and cheap, but require cooking
- **Coconut water** — $1-2 at most grocery stores, naturally high in potassium. If you are showing signs of potassium depletion (cramps, palpitations, muscle weakness) and have a dollar or two, it is a good investment

### Do Not Overdo It

More is not better with electrolytes. Excess sodium causes its own problems — high blood pressure, swelling, nausea.

- Stick to the quarter teaspoon per liter ratio for salt water
- Follow the WHO formula precisely if making rehydration solution
- Do not add extra salt thinking it will help more — the solution works because of the specific ratio of glucose to sodium, and altering it reduces its effectiveness

## The Psychology of Hunger

Hunger rewrites your brain. It impairs judgment, erodes identity, and drives bad decisions faster than the physical symptoms do.

### "Hanger" Is Neuroscience, Not a Character Flaw

When blood glucose drops, your prefrontal cortex — the part responsible for impulse control, planning, empathy, and rational decision-making — loses its primary fuel source. It functions less effectively. More primitive brain structures (amygdala, limbic system) gain proportional influence over your behavior.

You become more reactive, more irritable, more prone to anger, and less capable of the long-term thinking that gets you off the street. Brain imaging studies show reduced prefrontal cortex activity in glucose-depleted subjects. **You are stupider when you are hungry.**

### What Prolonged Hunger Does to the Mind

The Minnesota Starvation Experiment (University of Minnesota, 1944-1945) remains the definitive study. Thirty-six conscientious objectors volunteered for a controlled semi-starvation diet. Within weeks:

- Food became the only thing they could think about
- They lost interest in sex, socializing, politics, their beliefs, their relationships — everything except food
- They developed obsessive fantasies about eating
- Some developed ritualistic behaviors around meals — cutting food into tiny pieces, spending hours eating a single portion
- Several experienced depression severe enough to require intervention
- One participant, in a state of mental breakdown, cut off three of his own fingers with an axe

That experiment involved controlled conditions, monitoring, and guaranteed eventual refeeding. On the street, you have none of those supports. The psychological effects are amplified by uncertainty — you do not know when you will eat next. Uncertainty is more stressful than known adversity because your brain cannot plan around it.

### The Shame of Forced Hunger

Intermittent fasting is trendy. Choosing to skip meals feels empowering. Being unable to eat feels degrading. The difference is agency.

Shame drives isolation. Isolation makes it harder to access the social resources — shelters, meal programs, mutual aid — that would solve the hunger. People ashamed of their hunger hide it. Hiding it prevents them from getting help.

### Scarcity Mindset

Develops quickly. Persists long after the scarcity is resolved. When you have experienced real hunger, your relationship with food changes:

- You hoard food when it is available — stuffing your pockets at a soup kitchen, hiding food in your bag even when you are not hungry
- You eat too fast when food appears because your body has learned that food might disappear
- You binge when given access to unlimited food because your survival brain overrides your rational brain

These are not character flaws. They are adaptive responses to real deprivation, and they are difficult to unlearn.

### Willpower Drain

Your brain uses glucose to exercise self-control. When glucose is low, your capacity for self-control drops. Resisting the urge to eat uses the same neurological resources as:

- Resisting the urge to yell at someone
- Making a careful decision about where to sleep
- Maintaining the motivation to fill out a job application

When hunger is consuming your willpower, you have less available for everything else. You do not just miss a meal — you also make a worse decision about something unrelated, because your decision-making fuel tank is empty.

### Food as Social Currency

On the street, food functions as social currency:

- **Sharing food** builds alliances
- **Withholding food** creates enemies
- **Knowing where the free meals are** is information with trade value
- **Having a stash of snacks** makes you more socially connected

Hunger is not just physical deprivation. It is social marginalization within an already marginalized community.

### Calm the Panic with Knowledge

The fear of dying from hunger is common among people experiencing it for the first time, and it is almost always disproportionate to the actual risk. A healthy adult with water access is weeks away from starvation, not hours.

But the fear drives panic-based decisions:

- Spending your last dollars on food when you needed them for a bus pass to a job interview
- Eating something unsafe because anything is better than nothing
- Accepting dangerous help from someone offering food with strings attached

**You are not dying. You are uncomfortable. Those are different things.** You have time to make a good decision.

## Key Takeaways

- **You will not die tonight.** A healthy adult with water can survive 30-40 days without food. Make decisions from knowledge, not panic.
- **The first 48 hours are the worst.** After that, your body shifts to burning fat, and the acute hunger sensation often decreases.
- **Water is your first response.** Drink 8-16 ounces before deciding you are truly hungry. Add a pinch of salt if you have it.
- **Collect salt and sugar packets** from fast food restaurants. Free, weigh nothing, and can make electrolyte solutions that prevent medical emergencies.
- **Memorize the WHO rehydration formula.** 1/4 teaspoon salt + 6 teaspoons sugar + 1 liter water.
- **Refeeding syndrome can kill you.** After 2+ days without food, start with half a sandwich. Wait 2 hours. Eat small portions every 2-3 hours. Increase gradually over 4 days. Avoid sugary foods when breaking a fast.
- **911 trigger phrase:** If you experience chest pain, difficulty breathing, confusion, swelling, or seizures after eating following a fast, call 911 and say "refeeding syndrome."
- **Eat fat or protein before sleep** to prevent the 3 a.m. cortisol spike that wakes you from low blood sugar.
- **Hunger impairs your prefrontal cortex.** You are worse at making decisions when hungry. Acknowledge this and defer important decisions until after you have eaten.
- **Diarrhea during fasting is normal** (bile acid accumulation) but can cause a dehydration spiral. Carry generic Imodium if you can. Go to the ER if you see blood, go more than 3 days without improvement, or show signs of severe dehydration.
- **Get clothes that fit your current weight** from thrift stores. Looking put-together matters for job interviews and police interactions.
- **Scarcity mindset** — hoarding, binge eating, eating too fast — is a normal adaptive response to deprivation, not a character flaw. Recognize it when it happens.
