# Chapter 12: Hunger {#ch12}

*What happens when you stop eating, how to manage it, and how not to die when you start again.*

Hunger is not an emergency. It feels like one, and your brain will treat it like one, but a healthy adult with access to water can survive thirty to forty days without food. You are not going to die tonight because you missed dinner. You are not going to die tomorrow because you skipped every meal. Understanding this fact — really internalizing it — is the difference between making rational decisions and making panicked ones that cost you resources you cannot afford to waste.

This chapter exists because Chapter 11 covers how to find and afford food. This chapter covers what happens when you cannot. When the soup kitchen closed before you got there. When you spent your last dollar on a bus pass because getting to a job interview mattered more than eating today. When you simply have nothing and there is nothing to be done about it until tomorrow.

Hunger on the street is different from hunger in a house. In a house, hunger is an inconvenience that you resolve by opening the refrigerator. On the street, hunger is a compounding problem. It degrades your thinking. It makes you irritable and impulsive. It saps your energy for the tasks that get you off the street. And when food does finally appear, eating too much too fast after a prolonged period without can put you in the emergency room or kill you outright. That last part is not hyperbole. It has a clinical name — refeeding syndrome — and it has killed concentration camp survivors, hunger strikers, and homeless people who got their first real meal in a week.

So this chapter is about biology, management, and survival. What your body actually does when you stop eating, how to get through the worst of it, and how to start eating again without hurting yourself.

## The Biology of Not Eating

Your body is an engine that runs on glucose. When you eat, your digestive system breaks food down into glucose, which enters your bloodstream and fuels your brain, muscles, and organs. When you stop eating, your body does not simply shut down. It shifts fuel sources in a predictable sequence that humans have been adapted to handle for hundreds of thousands of years. Famine is not new to the human body. Your ancestors survived regular periods without food, and the biological machinery for handling it is still fully operational inside you.

Here is what happens on a timeline, roughly, when you stop eating.

At twelve hours without food, your blood glucose drops noticeably. This is the point where most people feel what they call "hungry." You get irritable. Your concentration wavers. You may get a headache. Your stomach growls because ghrelin — the hunger hormone — is firing on its normal schedule, telling your brain that it is time to eat. This is the stage where most people in normal life reach for a snack. It is uncomfortable but not dangerous in any medical sense. Your body is running through its immediately available glucose and beginning to tap into glycogen, which is glucose stored in your liver and muscles. You have roughly 1,600 to 2,000 calories worth of glycogen stored, enough to get you through the next twelve to twenty-four hours depending on your activity level.

At twenty-four hours, your glycogen stores are largely depleted. This is the transition point. Your body begins the process of ketosis — switching from glucose as its primary fuel to ketone bodies produced by breaking down fat. This transition is why the first full day without food is often the hardest. Your body is between fuel sources. You may feel weak, dizzy, and foggy. Your mood drops. Your patience disappears. You may feel nauseous even though there is nothing in your stomach. This is also the point where your breath may start to smell different — slightly metallic or fruity — which is the acetone byproduct of early ketosis. This is normal. It means your body is doing what it is supposed to do.

At forty-eight hours, you are in full ketosis. Your body is now running primarily on fat stores, converting them into ketone bodies that fuel your brain and organs. Here is the counterintuitive part: hunger often decreases at this stage. Not because your body does not need food, but because the ghrelin signaling pattern shifts. Your body stops sending the acute hunger signals that make the first day so miserable. You may still think about food constantly, but the physical sensation of gnawing, painful hunger often subsides. Many people report a sense of mental clarity during this stage, which is likely an evolutionary adaptation — when our ancestors were two days into a famine, they needed sharper thinking to find food, not dull foggy despair. Your body obliges.

At seventy-two hours and beyond, your body is adapted to its fasting state. Metabolic rate drops slightly — roughly ten to fifteen percent — to conserve energy. Your body is prioritizing vital functions and reducing expenditure elsewhere. Muscle protein is being conserved more aggressively than most people realize. The old myth that your body immediately eats its own muscle when you stop eating is largely wrong. Your body preferentially burns fat first and switches to significant muscle catabolism only when fat stores are severely depleted. For most people, that takes weeks, not days.

The calorie math matters here. A functioning adult needs roughly 1,200 to 1,500 calories per day at minimum to maintain basic body functions — breathing, circulation, digestion, temperature regulation. Below that threshold, you are in a caloric deficit, and your body is making up the difference from stored reserves. The average adult male carries enough fat stores to provide energy for thirty to forty days. The average adult female, owing to naturally higher body fat percentage, can often go somewhat longer. These are rough numbers and vary enormously by individual, but the point is clear: you have time. More time than your panicking brain tells you.

None of this means going without food is good for you. It is not. Prolonged fasting degrades your immune system, impairs wound healing, weakens your bones and teeth, and causes organ damage over extended periods. The point is not that hunger is fine. The point is that hunger is survivable, and knowing the timeline of what your body does helps you make rational decisions about when to spend resources on food, when to wait, and when the situation has become genuinely medically dangerous.

The first forty-eight hours are the worst. After that, it gets easier. Knowing this in advance does not eliminate the suffering, but it gives you a framework to endure it.

## Managing Hunger with No Food

When you cannot eat, you manage. You do not fix hunger without food; you reduce its impact on your ability to function. Every trick in this section is about buying time, reducing discomfort, and keeping your decision-making capacity intact until food becomes available.

Water first. Always water first. Your brain is notoriously bad at distinguishing between hunger and thirst, and dehydration amplifies every symptom of hunger — headache, irritability, fatigue, dizziness. Drink eight to sixteen ounces of water as soon as you feel hunger building. In roughly half of cases, this alone will reduce the immediate urgency. Cold water is slightly more effective than warm for suppressing appetite in the short term because your body expends a small amount of energy warming it, but any temperature water works. The goal is volume in the stomach. A full stomach sends stretch signals to the brain that partially override hunger signals.

Salt water is better than plain water for hunger management. Dissolve a small pinch of salt — less than a quarter teaspoon — in a glass of water. The sodium helps your body retain the water longer, prevents the electrolyte depletion that comes with drinking large volumes of plain water on an empty stomach, and the slight saltiness triggers a mild satiety response. Where do you get salt with no money? Fast food restaurants. Every McDonald's, Burger King, Wendy's, and Taco Bell has salt packets freely available near the condiment station. Take a handful. They cost nothing, weigh nothing, and are among the most useful survival items you can carry. Keep ten or fifteen packets in your bag at all times.

Hot water, coffee, and tea trick your stomach more effectively than cold water. The warmth triggers a mild digestive response that your brain interprets as "something is being processed." If you can get free coffee — most shelters offer it, many churches have it available, and gas stations will sometimes give a cup to someone who asks — drink it. The caffeine provides a temporary energy boost and appetite suppression. Tea works similarly. Even plain hot water from a gas station's coffee machine water spigot helps. The act of slowly drinking something warm provides a psychological comfort that cold water does not.

Sleep through it. This sounds obvious, but it is a legitimate strategy. Every hour you are asleep is an hour you are not suffering through hunger and not burning extra calories from activity. If you have the ability to sleep — a safe enough spot, a warm enough setup — and food is not going to be available until tomorrow, go to sleep. You will wake up deeper into ketosis, past the worst of the ghrelin spikes, and you will have conserved energy. Sleep is free calories saved.

Chew gum. A pack of gum costs a dollar at most stores and lasts for days. The physical act of chewing tricks your digestive system into a preparatory state that partially satisfies the mechanical craving for food. Mint-flavored gum is better than fruit-flavored for appetite suppression because the menthol creates a mild analgesic effect in your mouth and throat that dampens hunger signaling. You are not eating. Your body knows you are not eating. But the chewing provides a partial override of the physical urge.

Brush your teeth. If you have a toothbrush and toothpaste — and you should, as covered in Chapter 13 — brush your teeth when hunger becomes intense. The mint in toothpaste suppresses appetite for thirty to sixty minutes through the same menthol mechanism as gum, and the clean-mouth sensation creates a psychological disinclination to eat that your body interprets as reduced hunger. It is a small trick, but it works reliably.

Tighten your belt. This is an old military trick that works because it is mechanical, not chemical. A snug belt or waistband pressing against your abdomen sends mild pressure signals to the same stretch receptors that fire when your stomach is full. It does not fool your body entirely, but it reduces the intensity of hunger pangs. Soldiers and sailors have used this technique for centuries during periods of rationing. Cinch your belt one notch tighter than comfortable when hunger peaks.

Breathe through the waves. Hunger is not constant. It comes in waves driven by ghrelin release, typically every twenty to thirty minutes, each wave lasting five to ten minutes at peak intensity before subsiding. When a wave hits, box breathing helps: inhale for four seconds, hold for four seconds, exhale for four seconds, hold for four seconds. Repeat for two to three minutes. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system, reduces cortisol, and blunts the acute edge of the hunger spike. You are not eliminating hunger. You are riding out the wave until it passes, because it will pass.

Avoid food smells. This is harder than it sounds when you are on the street, but make the effort. Walking past a bakery or a burger joint when you are twenty-four hours into a fast is not a character-building exercise. It is self-sabotage. Food smells trigger ghrelin release and can restart the hunger cycle even after it has begun to subside. If your usual route takes you past restaurants, change your route. If you are near a food court in a mall where you are killing time, move to a different part of the building. Do not test your willpower unnecessarily. Willpower is a finite resource, and hunger depletes it faster than almost anything else.

Foraging is possible in most environments, but it comes with real risks. Dandelion greens — the common lawn weed with jagged leaves and yellow flowers — are edible raw and mildly nutritious. They grow in virtually every park, yard, and roadside strip in America. Clover — both the leaves and the small white or pink flowers — is edible and slightly sweet. Plantain weed, not the banana relative but the low-growing broadleaf plant found in sidewalk cracks and lawns everywhere, is edible and has mild anti-inflammatory properties. Wood sorrel, which looks like small clover with heart-shaped leaves and has a sour lemon-like taste, provides some vitamin C.

Here is the critical warning about foraging: misidentification kills. Do not eat any plant you cannot identify with absolute certainty. Poison hemlock looks similar to wild carrot. Water hemlock is one of the most toxic plants in North America and can kill within hours. Nightshade berries look appetizing. If you are going to forage, learn the specific plants listed above and eat only those. Do not experiment. Do not eat mushrooms unless you are a trained mycologist. Do not eat berries unless you can identify the species with certainty. A handful of wrongly identified berries can put you in the emergency room or the morgue, which is considerably worse than being hungry for another twelve hours.

Stay busy. Boredom amplifies hunger the same way it amplifies pain. Your brain, with nothing else to focus on, fixates on the hunger signal and turns up the volume. Walk. Organize your gear. Read something. Do push-ups. Plan your next steps. Anything that occupies your prefrontal cortex and gives it something to process other than the hunger signal reduces the subjective experience of hunger. Idle time is when hunger becomes unbearable. Occupied time is when it fades into the background.

## Weight Loss

You will lose weight on the street. Nearly everyone does. Understanding the pattern helps you distinguish between expected weight loss and dangerous weight loss.

In the first week of significant caloric restriction or fasting, you will lose one to two pounds per day. Most of this is water weight. Glycogen is stored with water molecules attached, and as you burn through glycogen, that water is released and excreted. This is why the scale drops fast initially and why the first five to ten pounds feel dramatic but are not primarily fat loss. Do not be alarmed by rapid weight loss in the first week. Do not be encouraged by it either. It is water, and it will come back when you resume eating normally.

After the first week, the rate slows to roughly half a pound to one pound per day, depending on your starting size, activity level, and how much you are eating. This is now primarily fat loss with some muscle catabolism mixed in. Over a month of significant caloric restriction — not total fasting, but the kind of inconsistent, inadequate eating that characterizes homelessness — losing twenty to thirty pounds is common. For people who were overweight to begin with, this may not be immediately dangerous. For people who were already lean, this rate of loss puts you into medically concerning territory within weeks.

The threshold to watch is ten percent of your body weight in a month. A 180-pound person losing 18 pounds in a month is approaching the danger zone. A 140-pound person losing 14 pounds in a month is in it. Beyond this threshold, your body is stripping resources from systems it considers non-essential — your immune response, your bone density, your dental health, your cognitive function — to keep your heart and lungs running. You are surviving, but you are degrading.

The visible changes happen faster than you expect, and they trigger reactions from people who knew you before. Friends and family who see you after a month may be shocked. Co-workers from a previous life may not recognize you. Some people will express concern. Others will avoid you because your appearance makes them uncomfortable. Some will assume drugs. The weight loss itself becomes a social stigma layered on top of the homelessness stigma, creating a compounding isolation effect.

Your immune system drops measurably after two to three weeks of significant caloric restriction. Every cold, every cut, every infection that a housed and fed person shakes off in a few days becomes a multi-week ordeal. A simple cold becomes bronchitis. A small cut gets infected. Your body does not have the spare resources to mount a full immune response while simultaneously trying to keep your organs functioning on inadequate fuel. This is why nutrition matters more on the street than it ever did in a house. Every calorie you get should be high quality when possible.

Dental impact is real and underappreciated. Malnutrition weakens your teeth and gums. The acidic environment of an empty stomach produces acid reflux that erodes tooth enamel. If you are vomiting from hunger or bile, the acid accelerates this. Losing teeth on the street is common and devastating because dental problems affect your ability to eat, your appearance during job interviews, and your self-image. Brush your teeth. Use fluoride toothpaste. Protect your teeth like the survival tools they are.

Joint pain develops as you lose weight because the padding around your joints decreases and the lubricating effect of adequate nutrition diminishes. Your knees, hips, and ankles bear the impact of walking all day on concrete, and without proper nutrition, the cartilage and synovial fluid that cushion them degrade. This is especially pronounced if you are sleeping on hard surfaces.

Loose skin becomes an issue for people who lose significant weight quickly. If you lose thirty or more pounds in a month or two, the skin that stretched to accommodate your previous size does not snap back immediately. It may not snap back at all without time and adequate nutrition. This is a cosmetic issue, not a medical one, but it affects how your clothes fit and how you feel about your body at a time when your self-image is already taking hits from every direction.

Get your clothes from thrift stores, not from punching extra holes in your belt. Clothes that fit properly make you look employed, which matters for job interviews, for interactions with police, and for your own psychological state. Goodwill, Salvation Army, and church clothing closets provide free or near-free clothing. A pair of pants that fits you at your current weight is worth more than a pair of expensive pants cinched with a belt that makes you look like you are wearing a sack. As you lose weight, rotate your wardrobe through thrift stores. Trade in what no longer fits for what does.

## Diarrhea and Digestive Issues

This is the section nobody wants to read, and everybody on the street needs. Digestive problems during periods of hunger and inconsistent eating are nearly universal, and they create a dangerous spiral that can land you in the emergency room if you do not manage them.

Bile acid diarrhea is the most common and least understood digestive issue during periods of not eating. Your liver continuously produces bile to aid in fat digestion. When you eat, the bile is used and reabsorbed. When you do not eat, the bile has nowhere to go. It accumulates in your small intestine and eventually floods into your large intestine, where it acts as a laxative. The result is urgent, watery diarrhea that is often bright yellow or greenish — the color of bile. It hits without warning, and it can hit multiple times a day during the first few days of not eating. This is your liver doing its job in the absence of food to process. It is not an infection. It is not food poisoning. It is a mechanical consequence of fasting, and it resolves when you resume eating.

Ketosis transition diarrhea occurs in the first two to three days as your body shifts from glucose to fat metabolism. The metabolic shift alters your gut flora and the chemical environment of your intestines. Some people get constipation during this transition. Others get diarrhea. Both are normal and self-limiting, resolving within two to four days as your gut adapts to the new metabolic state.

Refeeding diarrhea happens when you eat again after a period without food. Your digestive system has downregulated — it has reduced the production of digestive enzymes and slowed the movement of the intestinal lining because there was nothing to process. When food suddenly arrives, your gut is not ready for it. The result is cramping, bloating, and diarrhea as your system tries to process food with a machinery that has partially shut down. This is why eating slowly and in small amounts after a fast matters. Not just for refeeding syndrome risk, which is covered below, but for basic digestive function.

The dehydration spiral is the real danger. Diarrhea causes fluid loss. Fluid loss causes dehydration. Dehydration causes weakness and confusion. Weakness makes it harder to get to a water source. Confusion impairs your judgment about how serious the situation is. Each iteration of this cycle makes the next one worse. A person who started with hunger and developed bile acid diarrhea can end up severely dehydrated within twenty-four to forty-eight hours, especially in hot weather or if they were already mildly dehydrated when the diarrhea started.

The BRAT diet — bananas, rice, applesauce, toast — is the standard recovery protocol for diarrhea, and it works because these foods are bland, binding, and easy to digest. If you have access to any of these, eat them in small amounts. A single banana and a cup of rice will do more for your gut than a full meal. If you have no access to these specific foods, any bland starchy food will help — plain bread, crackers, plain pasta.

Imodium (loperamide) is available over the counter for roughly four dollars for a generic box at Walmart. It slows intestinal motility, which is a clinical way of saying it makes the diarrhea stop. Carry some if you can afford it. Two milligrams (one pill or capsule) at the onset of diarrhea, then one milligram after each loose stool, up to eight milligrams in a day. Do not exceed this dose. Loperamide in large doses is dangerous and can cause cardiac events.

Know when to go to the emergency room. Blood in your stool — red or black and tarry — is an ER visit. Diarrhea lasting more than three days without improvement is an ER visit. Signs of severe dehydration — no urine output for eight or more hours, dark brown urine, rapid heartbeat at rest, sunken eyes, inability to stand without dizziness, dry mouth with no saliva production — are an ER visit. Do not wait. Severe dehydration kills, and it kills faster than hunger.

The embarrassment factor deserves mention because it is a genuine barrier to getting help. Finding a bathroom when you are homeless is already difficult. Finding a bathroom urgently and repeatedly when you have diarrhea is a logistical nightmare. Public restrooms are scarce in many cities. Fast food restaurants increasingly require a purchase. Libraries have limited hours. The fear of soiling yourself in public is real and can lead people to restrict their fluid intake, which worsens dehydration, which worsens everything. Do not let embarrassment kill you. Use the bathroom resources outlined in Chapter 13. If you cannot find a bathroom, find a private outdoor location and clean up as well as you can. Dignity matters, but survival matters more.

## Refeeding Syndrome — This Can Kill You

> **WARNING:** This section describes a medical condition that can be fatal. If you have not eaten for more than two days and you experience chest pain, difficulty breathing, confusion, swelling in your extremities, or seizures after eating, call 911 immediately. Tell the dispatcher "I think I have refeeding syndrome." This phrase will get you treated faster than trying to explain your situation from scratch.

Refeeding syndrome is what happens when a starved body receives food too quickly. It is not a stomach problem. It is a metabolic crisis that affects your heart, lungs, and brain, and it can kill within hours of onset.

Here is the mechanism. When you fast for an extended period, your body depletes its intracellular stores of electrolytes — primarily phosphate, potassium, and magnesium. These electrolytes are critical for heart rhythm, breathing muscles, and brain function. Your body compensates for their reduced levels by adjusting to a lower baseline. You feel terrible, but your heart keeps beating and your lungs keep working because your body has calibrated to the depleted state.

When you suddenly eat, especially carbohydrate-heavy food, your body releases a surge of insulin to process the incoming glucose. Insulin drives glucose into cells, but it also drives phosphate, potassium, and magnesium from your bloodstream into your cells. Since your total body stores of these electrolytes are already depleted from fasting, this shift causes a precipitous drop in blood levels of all three. Your heart depends on potassium to maintain its rhythm. Your breathing muscles depend on phosphate for energy production. Your brain depends on all three to function. When their blood levels crash, things go wrong fast.

The symptoms of refeeding syndrome include heart palpitations or irregular heartbeat, difficulty breathing or shortness of breath, confusion or disorientation, swelling in the hands, feet, or legs (edema from fluid shifts), muscle weakness that may be severe, and in extreme cases, seizures and cardiac arrest.

The highest risk period is after five or more days without food, but refeeding syndrome has been documented after as few as two to three days of fasting in people who were already malnourished or had depleted electrolyte stores. Chronic alcoholics, people with anorexia, and people who have been eating poorly for weeks before a complete fast are at elevated risk even with shorter fasting periods.

This is not theoretical. This is how concentration camp survivors died after liberation. Allied forces, not understanding the biology, gave liberated prisoners full meals. Thousands died in the days following liberation — not from their starvation, but from the food that was supposed to save them. The same mechanism kills hunger strikers who break their fasts too quickly, and it kills homeless people who eat a large meal at a shelter after days without food.

Prevention is straightforward but requires discipline at a moment when your body is screaming at you to eat everything in sight.

If you have not eaten for two or more days, start with half a sandwich. Not a whole sandwich. Half. Eat it slowly over ten to fifteen minutes. Then stop. Wait two hours. If you feel fine — no chest pain, no palpitations, no breathing difficulty, no swelling, no confusion — eat the other half. Wait another two hours. Eat another small portion. Continue this pattern: small portions every two to three hours, gradually increasing the size over four days until you are back to normal meals.

Start with simple carbohydrates and protein. Bread with peanut butter. A few crackers with cheese. Rice with beans. Avoid sugar-heavy foods and drinks — no candy bars, no soda, no energy drinks. The sugar causes a larger insulin spike, which drives a more severe electrolyte shift. A Snickers bar after five days of fasting is more dangerous than a turkey sandwich.

If possible, get electrolytes into your body before you eat. A glass of salt water — a quarter teaspoon of salt in a liter of water — provides sodium and helps set the stage. If you can get a banana for potassium or a cheap electrolyte drink, even better. The electrolytes will not fully prevent refeeding syndrome, but they partially buffer the shift by raising your blood levels before the insulin-driven crash.

The four-day refeeding schedule is the clinical standard. Day one: 500 to 800 calories in small portions spread across the day. Day two: 800 to 1,200 calories. Day three: 1,200 to 1,500 calories. Day four and beyond: normal eating. If at any point during this progression you feel your heart racing, have difficulty breathing, become confused, or experience swelling, stop eating and get to an emergency room.

The hardest part of managing refeeding is the psychological component. After days without food, when food finally appears, every instinct in your body demands that you eat as much as possible as fast as possible. Overriding that instinct requires understanding why you must, and it requires remembering this section. Eating slowly after a fast is not about willpower or self-control in the way those terms are normally used. It is about staying alive.

## Sleep and Hunger

Hunger and sleep are intertwined in ways that create a vicious cycle on the street. Understanding the biology helps you manage both.

When your blood glucose drops, your body releases cortisol — the stress hormone. This is an evolutionary adaptation. For your ancestors, low blood sugar meant food was scarce, and cortisol triggered a heightened alertness state that drove them to hunt or forage. For you, lying in a sleeping bag at two in the morning, it means you wake up with a pounding heart and a racing mind and cannot get back to sleep. This is the 3 a.m. blood sugar crash, and it is nearly universal among people who are not eating enough.

If you have any food at all, eat something with fat or protein before you try to sleep. A spoonful of peanut butter, a handful of nuts, a piece of cheese, even a glass of milk if you can get it. Fat and protein digest slowly, providing a more stable blood glucose level over four to six hours, compared to carbohydrates, which spike and crash within two to three hours. This does not eliminate the overnight crash, but it pushes it later into the night, giving you more consecutive hours of sleep before the cortisol response wakes you.

The sleep debt compounds with hunger in a way that is genuinely dangerous. Day one of poor sleep from hunger, you are tired and irritable but functional. Day three, your decision-making is measurably impaired. Research shows that three days of significant sleep deprivation degrades cognitive function to the equivalent of being legally drunk. Your judgment about risk, about whom to trust, about how to spend your limited resources — all of it becomes unreliable. By day five, hallucinations become possible. Not common, but possible. The combination of hunger, sleep deprivation, and stress creates a neurochemical environment where your brain begins generating sensory input that is not there.

Micro-naps are your countermeasure. A ten to twenty minute nap — true sleep, not just closing your eyes — restores a surprising amount of cognitive function. The mechanism is not fully understood, but even brief periods of sleep allow your brain to flush adenosine, the chemical that builds up during waking hours and makes you feel progressively more tired. If you cannot get a full night's sleep because hunger keeps waking you, get two or three micro-naps during the day. A bench in a park, a chair in a library, a seat on a bus — anywhere you can safely close your eyes for fifteen minutes. Set an alarm on your phone if you have one. The naps will not fully compensate for lost nighttime sleep, but they will keep your cognitive function above the threshold where bad decisions start happening.

## DIY Electrolyte Management

Electrolytes are minerals in your blood that carry electrical charges. They regulate your heartbeat, your muscle contractions, your nerve signaling, and your fluid balance. The three that matter most during periods of hunger are sodium, potassium, and magnesium. When you are not eating, your body depletes these because it is not getting them from food, and because fasting increases urinary excretion of all three.

Salt water is the simplest and cheapest electrolyte solution. One quarter teaspoon of table salt dissolved in one liter (roughly one quart) of water provides sodium chloride, which is the most acutely important electrolyte for preventing dehydration and maintaining blood pressure. This costs nothing if you use salt packets from fast food restaurants, which you should be collecting and carrying at all times.

The World Health Organization oral rehydration solution is one of the most important medical discoveries of the twentieth century. It has saved tens of millions of lives worldwide, primarily children with cholera and diarrheal disease. The formula is simple: one quarter teaspoon of salt plus six teaspoons of sugar in one liter of water. The glucose-sodium co-transport mechanism allows your intestines to absorb water far more efficiently than plain water or salt water alone. If you are dehydrated and nauseous and cannot keep plain water down, this solution may stay down and absorb. Mix it in any clean container. It tastes mildly salty and slightly sweet, like flat sports drink. It works better than any commercial electrolyte product you can buy, and it costs nearly nothing.

The signs that you need electrolytes are specific and recognizable. Muscle cramps, especially in your calves, feet, or hands, indicate sodium or potassium depletion. Muscle twitching — involuntary small contractions in your eyelids, fingers, or legs — suggests magnesium depletion. Dizziness when standing up (orthostatic hypotension) indicates your blood pressure is dropping from sodium depletion. Heart flutter or palpitations — a sensation that your heart is skipping beats or beating irregularly — can indicate potassium or magnesium depletion and should be taken seriously. Tingling or numbness in your fingers, toes, or around your mouth can indicate calcium or magnesium depletion.

Salt packets from fast food restaurants are free survival gear. Ten packets of salt from McDonald's weigh almost nothing, fit in any pocket, and contain enough sodium to make several liters of electrolyte solution. Collect them every time you pass through a fast food restaurant. Sugar packets are equally available and equally free. Together, they give you the raw materials for WHO oral rehydration solution anywhere you can find clean water.

For potassium, bananas are the cheapest accessible source at roughly twenty-five cents each. A single banana provides about 400 milligrams of potassium, which is roughly ten percent of your daily requirement. Potatoes are also potassium-rich and cheap, though they require cooking. Coconut water, available for one to two dollars at most grocery stores, is naturally high in potassium and makes an effective electrolyte drink. If you have a dollar or two to spare and you are showing signs of potassium depletion — cramps, palpitations, muscle weakness — coconut water is a good investment.

Do not overdo it. More is not better with electrolytes, and excess sodium can cause its own problems — high blood pressure, swelling, nausea. Stick to the quarter teaspoon per liter ratio for salt water. If you are making WHO rehydration solution, follow the formula precisely. Do not add extra salt thinking it will help more. The solution works because of the specific ratio of glucose to sodium, and altering it reduces its effectiveness.

## The Psychology of Hunger

Hunger is not just a physical sensation. It is a cognitive state that rewrites your priorities, impairs your judgment, and erodes your sense of self. Understanding the psychology is as important as understanding the biology because the psychological effects of hunger drive more bad decisions than the physical effects.

Hanger — the irritability and anger that accompany hunger — is not a personality flaw or a lack of self-control. It is neuroscience. When blood glucose drops, your prefrontal cortex — the part of your brain responsible for impulse control, planning, empathy, and rational decision-making — loses its primary fuel source. It begins to function less effectively, and the more primitive brain structures — the amygdala, the limbic system — gain proportional influence over your behavior. You become more reactive, more irritable, more prone to anger, and less capable of the long-term thinking that gets you off the street. This is not a metaphor. Brain imaging studies show reduced prefrontal cortex activity in glucose-depleted subjects. You are measurably stupider when you are hungry.

The Minnesota Starvation Experiment, conducted at the University of Minnesota in 1944-1945, remains the definitive study of what prolonged hunger does to the human mind. Thirty-six conscientious objectors volunteered for a controlled semi-starvation diet. Within weeks, food became the only thing they could think about. They lost interest in sex, socializing, politics, their beliefs, their relationships — everything except food. They reported obsessive fantasies about eating. Some developed ritualistic behaviors around their meager meals — cutting food into tiny pieces, mixing foods in specific combinations, spending hours eating a single portion. Several experienced depression severe enough to require intervention. One participant, in a state of mental breakdown, cut off three of his own fingers with an axe.

That experiment involved controlled conditions, monitoring, and guaranteed eventual refeeding. On the street, you have none of those supports. The psychological effects are amplified by the uncertainty — you do not know when you will eat next, which is worse than knowing you will not eat. Uncertainty is more stressful than known adversity because your brain cannot plan around it.

The shame of forced hunger is distinct from the experience of choosing not to eat. Intermittent fasting is trendy. Choosing to skip meals feels empowering. Being unable to eat feels degrading. The distinction is entirely about agency, and it matters because shame drives isolation, and isolation makes it harder to access the social resources — shelters, meal programs, mutual aid — that would solve the hunger. People who are ashamed of their hunger hide it, and hiding it prevents them from getting help.

Scarcity mindset develops quickly and persists long after the scarcity is resolved. When you have experienced real hunger, your relationship with food changes at a fundamental level. You begin hoarding food when it is available — stuffing your pockets at a soup kitchen, hiding food in your bag even when you are not hungry. You eat too fast when food appears because your body has learned that food might disappear. You binge when given access to unlimited food because your survival brain overrides your rational brain's understanding that the all-you-can-eat buffet at the shelter will be there again tomorrow. These are not character flaws. They are adaptive responses to real deprivation, and they are extraordinarily difficult to unlearn.

Willpower drain is a measurable phenomenon. Your brain uses glucose to exercise self-control, and when glucose is low, your capacity for self-control drops. Resisting the urge to eat when you are hungry uses the same neurological resources as resisting the urge to yell at someone, or making a careful decision about where to sleep, or maintaining the motivation to fill out a job application. When hunger is consuming your willpower, you have less of it available for everything else. This is why hunger causes downstream failures — you do not just miss a meal, you also make a worse decision about something apparently unrelated, because your decision-making fuel tank is empty.

Food functions as social currency on the street in ways that housed people do not typically experience. Sharing food builds alliances. Withholding food creates enemies. Knowing where the free meals are is information that has trade value. Being the person who has a stash of snacks makes you more socially connected than being the person who has nothing. This social dimension of food means that hunger is not just physical deprivation — it is social marginalization within an already marginalized community.

The fear of dying from hunger is nearly universal among people experiencing it for the first time, and it is nearly always disproportionate to the actual risk. As established in the biology section, a healthy adult with water access is weeks away from starvation, not hours. But the fear is real, and it drives panic-based decisions — spending your last dollars on food when you needed them for a bus pass to a job interview, eating something unsafe because anything is better than nothing, accepting dangerous help from someone offering food with strings attached. Calm the panic with knowledge. You are not dying. You are uncomfortable. Those are different things. You have time to make a good decision.

## Calorie-Dense Foods Ranked by Cost

When you do have money for food, spend it on the highest calorie-per-dollar options. This is not about eating well in the nutritional sense — that is covered in Chapter 11. This is about raw survival calories when your budget is approaching zero.

Peanut butter is the single best survival food available in an American grocery store. A standard jar costs roughly three dollars and contains approximately 2,600 calories. That is 190 calories per two-tablespoon serving, with fat, protein, and carbohydrates in a ratio that provides sustained energy. It does not require refrigeration, does not require cooking, and can be eaten directly from the jar with a finger if necessary. One jar and a loaf of bread (another dollar, roughly 1,200 calories) gives you roughly 3,800 calories for four dollars — close to two full days of survival-level nutrition.

Dry rice provides roughly 200 calories per uncooked cup at a cost of approximately three dollars for a five-pound bag, which contains roughly thirty-six cups. That works out to roughly 7,200 calories for three dollars. The limitation is that rice requires cooking, which means you need a heat source and a pot. If you have a camp stove or access to a kitchen, rice is the cheapest calorie source available. If you do not, it is useless.

Dried beans provide roughly 350 calories per uncooked cup. A two-pound bag costs about two dollars and contains roughly five cups. That is 1,750 calories for two dollars. Like rice, beans require cooking and soak time (ideally overnight, or boil for two minutes and let sit for one hour). Combined with rice, beans create a complete protein — all the amino acids your body needs.

Oatmeal provides roughly 150 calories per packet in the instant variety, or about 150 calories per half-cup of bulk oats. A box of ten instant oatmeal packets costs roughly three dollars (1,500 calories). Bulk oats are cheaper — about two dollars for a canister that contains roughly 2,400 calories. Instant oatmeal can be prepared with just hot water from a gas station or shelter, making it one of the few calorie-dense foods that requires no cooking equipment.

Ramen noodles provide approximately 380 calories per packet at a cost of roughly twenty-five cents. That is one of the highest calorie-per-dollar ratios in any grocery store. The nutritional profile is poor — mostly refined carbohydrates, sodium, and a small amount of fat — but when the question is survival calories, ramen delivers. A single packet can be eaten dry as a crunchy snack or prepared with hot water. Ten packets cost two-fifty and provide 3,800 calories.

Bananas cost roughly twenty-five cents each and provide about 100 calories. They are one of the cheapest sources of potassium available and require no preparation. They spoil within a few days, so buy only what you will eat in the next day or two.

Eggs provide roughly 70 calories each and currently cost about twenty-five cents per egg depending on your location and the current market. They require cooking to be safe, though the risk from raw eggs is often overstated. If you have a camp stove or access to a microwave (libraries, some gas stations), eggs provide the cheapest per-calorie source of complete protein available. Hard-boiled eggs keep for about a week without refrigeration in cool weather.

## Key Takeaways

- A healthy adult with water can survive thirty to forty days without food. You are not going to die tonight. Make decisions from knowledge, not panic.
- The first forty-eight hours without food are the worst. After that, your body shifts to burning fat, and the acute hunger sensation often decreases.
- Water is your first response to hunger. Drink eight to sixteen ounces before deciding you are truly hungry. Add a pinch of salt if you have it.
- Collect salt and sugar packets from fast food restaurants. They are free, weigh nothing, and can make electrolyte solutions that prevent medical emergencies.
- The WHO oral rehydration solution — one quarter teaspoon salt plus six teaspoons sugar in one liter of water — is one of the most effective medical interventions in human history. Memorize the recipe.
- Refeeding syndrome can kill you. After two or more days without food, start with half a sandwich. Wait two hours. Eat small portions every two to three hours. Increase gradually over four days. Avoid sugary foods when breaking a fast.
- If you experience chest pain, difficulty breathing, confusion, swelling, or seizures after eating following a period of fasting, call 911 and say "refeeding syndrome."
- Eat fat or protein before sleep to prevent the 3 a.m. cortisol spike that wakes you from low blood sugar.
- Peanut butter is the best survival food in America. Three dollars for roughly 2,600 calories that require no refrigeration and no cooking.
- Hunger impairs your prefrontal cortex. You are measurably worse at making decisions when you are hungry. Acknowledge this and defer important decisions until after you have eaten.
- Diarrhea during fasting is normal (bile acid accumulation) but can cause a dehydration spiral. Carry generic Imodium if you can. Go to the ER if you see blood, go more than three days without improvement, or show signs of severe dehydration.
- Get clothes that fit your current weight from thrift stores. Looking put-together matters for job interviews and police interactions regardless of your actual situation.
- Scarcity mindset — hoarding, binge eating, eating too fast — is a normal adaptive response to deprivation, not a character flaw. Recognize it when it happens.
