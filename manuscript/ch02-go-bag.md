# Chapter 2: The Go-Bag {#ch02}

*What to take, what to leave, and what to get before you lose access.*

Everything you own is about to be reduced to what you can carry on your back. That is a hard sentence to read, but the sooner you accept it, the better your decisions will be. The go-bag is not a camping trip packing list. It is the difference between recovering from homelessness in weeks versus months.

The biggest mistake people make is packing too much. You grab sentimental items, extra clothes, things that feel important in the moment. Then you are hauling two overstuffed bags through a bus station at 11 PM, exhausted, with a broken zipper. Pack for function, not comfort.

The second biggest mistake is packing too late. If you are reading this chapter and you still have housing, start assembling your go-bag today. Not tomorrow. Today. Put it in a closet where it is ready to grab.

This chapter is organized by priority. Priority 1 is what you grab if the house is on fire. Priority 11 is what you add if you have a day to prepare. Everything is cross-referenced to the chapter that covers it in depth. One backpack. One duffel at most. If you cannot carry it for a mile without stopping, you have packed too much.

## Priority 1: Documents

*Grab these first. Everything else can be replaced. These cannot — or at least, replacing them without an address takes weeks to months and creates a bureaucratic nightmare that stalls your entire recovery.*

### The Non-Negotiable List

- **Photo ID** (driver’s license or state ID)
- **Social Security card** (or at minimum, your number memorized)
- **Birth certificate** (or a certified copy)
- **Medical records** and prescription documentation
- **Court documents** (custody orders, restraining orders, probation paperwork)
- **Proof of income** or last pay stubs
- **Insurance cards** (health, auto)
- **Tax returns** from last 2 years (needed for benefits)
- **Lease or rental agreement** (proves previous housing)
- **DD-214** (veterans — unlocks VA benefits)

Put originals in a waterproof bag. Zip-lock freezer bags work fine. Keep this bag **on your body** at all times — not in your backpack. A neck pouch or inside jacket pocket is ideal.

### If You Do Not Have ID

Replacing ID without an address is one of the hardest logistics problems in homelessness. Do this NOW while you still have a mailing address:

1. **Birth certificate:** Request from vital records in your state of birth. Cost: $10–$30. Processing: 2–8 weeks. Find your state’s office at [cdc.gov/nchs/w2w/index.htm](https://cdc.gov/nchs/w2w/index.htm).
2. **State ID:** Visit your DMV with birth certificate + Social Security card. Cost: $5–$35. Some states offer free IDs for homeless individuals — ask.
3. **Social Security card:** Free at any SSA office or online at [ssa.gov/myaccount](https://ssa.gov/myaccount). Need photo ID to request one. Limit: 3/year, 10 lifetime.
4. **DD-214 (veterans):** Request at [archives.gov/veterans](https://archives.gov/veterans) or call **314-801-0800**. Free.

### Digitize Everything

Photograph every document with your phone — front and back. Photograph your driver's license (front and back), Social Security card, birth certificate, any professional certifications, your resume, and your vehicle registration and insurance cards. Also photograph court documents, prescriptions, and anything else from the list above. Email them to yourself (Gmail, Outlook — free). Upload to Google Drive or iCloud. Write your email password on paper and keep it in your document bag. If your phone dies, you can access everything from a library computer.

**Also digitize:**

- **Your resume.** Save it as a PDF in your email and Google Drive. You can print copies at any library (often free, sometimes $0.10/page). Update it before you lose access to a computer.
- **A professional photo of yourself.** Clean clothes, neutral background, head and shoulders. This works for job applications, benefit forms, ID replacements, and shelter intake. Take it now while you still have a mirror and a clean shirt.
- **Photos of your belongings.** If you are putting items in storage or leaving them with someone, photograph everything. This documents what you own for insurance claims, disputes, or police reports if items are stolen or lost.
- **Your apartment condition.** Photograph every room, every wall, every appliance before you leave. This protects your security deposit and defends against damage claims.
- **Important phone numbers.** Screenshot your contacts list. If your phone dies or is stolen, you lose every number. Write the 10 most critical numbers on paper and keep it in your document bag.

> **✔ Practical Tip**
> Take a photo of yourself in clean, presentable clothes and save it digitally. Some benefit applications, shelter intakes, and ID replacements require a photo. Having one ready saves time.

*Documents are covered in detail in this chapter only. No cross-reference needed — this is the definitive section.*

## Priority 2: Phone + Power

*Your phone is not a luxury. It is your connection to jobs, benefits, shelter, maps, and people. Without it, you are cut off from every system designed to help you.*

### What to Pack

- **Your phone** — charged to 100% before you leave
- **Charging cable** (bring 2 — one breaks, one is backup)
- **Portable battery bank:** 10,000 mAh ($15–$25 at Walmart) gives 2–3 full charges. 20,000 mAh ($25–$40) gives 4–5. This is one of the most important purchases you will make.
- **Wall charger** (the brick that plugs into outlets)

### Free Phone Service

- **Lifeline program:** $9.25/month federal subsidy. Qualify with SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, or income below 135% of poverty. Apply at [lifelinesupport.org](https://lifelinesupport.org).
- **Assurance Wireless:** Free phone + free service (unlimited talk/text). Enroll at [assurancewireless.com](https://assurancewireless.com).
- **SafeLink Wireless:** Same deal, different carrier. [safelinkwireless.com](https://safelinkwireless.com).
- **Cheap prepaid:** Mint Mobile $15/month, Tello $5/month, Visible $25/month unlimited.

### Essential Apps to Install Before You Leave

- **Google Maps** — download offline maps of your city now
- **findhelp.org** — search by zip for food, shelter, health, jobs
- **Gmail or Outlook** — required for job applications and benefits
- **Your bank’s app** — check balance, find ATMs
- **Indeed** — job searching from your phone
- **Notes app** — track services, schedules, your plan
- **Camera** — document everything (apartment condition, receipts, forms)

### Battery Conservation

Turn off Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, and location when not in use. Screen brightness to minimum. Close background apps. Enable low-power mode permanently. Delete social media apps (use the browser versions — they drain less battery).

### Free Charging Spots

Libraries, coffee shops ($1 coffee buys you an outlet), day centers, hospital waiting rooms, airport terminals, some parks and bus stations have USB outlets.

## Priority 3: Medications + First Aid

*Running out of insulin, psych meds, or blood pressure medication on the street is a medical emergency. Get ahead of this now.*

### Medications

- **Get a 90-day supply** of every medication you take. Call your doctor today and explain your situation. Most will write a 90-day prescription.
- **GoodRx:** Free app/website. Cuts prescription costs 50–80%. Show the coupon at any pharmacy. No insurance needed.
- **Walmart $4 generics:** Over 100 common medications for $4 (30-day) or $10 (90-day). Includes metformin, lisinopril, amoxicillin, and many psych meds.
- **Patient Assistance Programs:** Drug manufacturers offer free medications to people without insurance. Search [needymeds.org](https://needymeds.org) by medication name.
- **FQHCs (Federally Qualified Health Centers):** Sliding-scale clinics that cannot turn you away. Many have on-site pharmacies with 340B drug pricing (drastically discounted). Find one at [findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov](https://findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov).

### First Aid Kit

You will get blisters, cuts, headaches, and stomach problems. Minor injuries become serious infections without basic treatment. Build this for under $20 at a dollar store:

- Adhesive bandages, assorted sizes ($1)
- Antibiotic ointment — Neosporin or generic ($3)
- Ibuprofen — 100-count bottle ($4). Pain, inflammation, fever.
- Antidiarrheal — Imodium generic ($4). Food poisoning happens.
- Alcohol wipes or hand sanitizer ($1)
- Moleskin ($4 at any pharmacy). Apply at the first sign of a hot spot on your feet. Do not wait for a blister to form.
- Sunscreen SPF 30+ ($3). Sunburn is a real problem when you are outside all day.
- Lip balm with SPF ($1)
- Tweezers ($1) — splinters, ticks
- Ace bandage ($2) — sprains, wrapping injuries
- Electrolyte packets ($3–$5 for 10). Pedialyte, Liquid IV, or generic. Dehydration causes confusion, dizziness, and fainting — especially in summer or if you are vomiting. Mix one packet into your water bottle daily in hot weather.

> **⚠ Hydration Warning**
> Dehydration impairs your thinking before you feel thirsty. By the time you notice, your decision-making is already compromised. Drink water every hour. In heat above 90°F, you need 8+ bottles per day. Signs of dehydration: dark urine, headache, dizziness, confusion. If someone is confused and has not been drinking water, get them fluids immediately. Electrolytes matter — water alone is not enough if you are sweating heavily. Salt packets from fast food restaurants are free and work in a pinch.

## Priority 4: Cash + Financial Tools

*Cash keeps you alive. How you carry it determines whether you keep it.*

### How to Carry Cash

- **Do not carry it all on you.** Theft is the #1 daily risk on the street. Split your money into at least 3 locations.
- **On your body:** $50–$100 for daily needs. Front pocket, money belt, or sock. Never back pocket or outer bag pocket.
- **Bank account:** Keep the bulk here. Chime, Cash App, or any no-fee account. Access via debit card. Harder to steal than physical cash.
- **Emergency stash:** $100–$200 hidden separately. Taped inside your sleeping bag, sewn into a jacket lining, or in a sealed bag deep in your pack. Last-resort money.

### Banking Without an Address

- **Chime:** No minimum balance, no fees, no credit check. Online-only bank. Accepts direct deposit. Get the debit card shipped to a shelter or friend’s address.
- **Cash App:** Free account, free debit card (Cash Card). Accepts direct deposit. Card ships in 1–2 weeks.
- **Walmart MoneyCard:** Prepaid debit. Buy in-store for $1. Load cash at any register.
- **Walmart check cashing:** $4 flat fee for checks up to $1,000. No bank account needed.

## Priority 5: Clothing System

> **⚠ Cotton Kills**
> When cotton gets wet from rain, sweat, or condensation, it loses all insulating ability and pulls heat from your body. This is the #1 cause of hypothermia in homeless deaths. Wear wool or synthetic fabrics (polyester, nylon, fleece). Thrift stores sell wool sweaters for $3–$8. This is not a suggestion — it is a survival rule.

### Base Layer (Next to Skin)

Polyester or merino wool long-sleeve shirt and long underwear. Wicks sweat away from your body. Walmart sells synthetic base layers for $8–$12. Thrift stores have them for $2–$5.

### Mid Layer (Insulation)

Fleece jacket or wool sweater. Traps body heat. A fleece pullover from Goodwill costs $4–$8. Military surplus wool sweaters run $10–$15 and last decades.

### Outer Layer (Wind and Rain)

Waterproof shell jacket. Does not need to be expensive — a $10 rain jacket from Walmart works. What matters is that wind and water cannot reach your mid layer. Keep a $1 emergency poncho from the dollar store as backup.

### Feet

**Wool socks.** Not cotton. Wool stays warm when wet, dries faster, resists odor. Carry 3 pairs minimum. Rotate daily. Kirkland (Costco) merino wool socks cost $3/pair. Darn Tough brand lasts years but costs $20/pair. Wet feet lead to trench foot, blisters, and fungal infections that can disable you.

**Boots.** Waterproof, broken-in, ankle-supporting. You will walk miles daily. Do not pack new boots — break them in before you leave. Goodwill occasionally has hiking boots for $8–$15. Military surplus stores sell used combat boots for $20–$40 that outlast anything from Walmart.

### Head and Hands

You lose significant heat through your head. Wool or fleece beanie: $2–$5 at a thrift store. Thin glove liners ($3–$5) let you use your phone. In cold climates, add insulated gloves or mittens over the liners.

### What to Pack

- 2 complete base layer sets (top + bottom)
- 1 mid layer (fleece or wool)
- 1 waterproof outer shell
- 3 pairs wool socks
- 3 pairs underwear (synthetic, not cotton)
- 1 beanie
- 1 pair glove liners
- Broken-in waterproof boots (wear these, do not pack them)

## Priority 6: Sleeping Gear

*You will sleep outside, in a car, or in a shelter. Each situation is different, but the fundamentals are the same: insulate from the ground, insulate from the air, stay dry.*

### Sleeping Bag

Get the lowest temperature rating you can afford. A **20°F bag** covers 3 seasons in most of the US. Walmart sells the Ozark Trail 20°F mummy bag for $25–$35. Military surplus sleeping bags (rated to 0°F or −20°F) cost $40–$80 at surplus stores and are nearly indestructible. Mummy bags are warmer than rectangular bags because they trap less dead air around your body.

### Ground Insulation

The ground steals heat 25 times faster than air. **Never sleep directly on concrete, dirt, or metal.** A closed-cell foam pad ($8–$15 at Walmart) is the minimum. Even flattened cardboard under your bag adds critical insulation. Two layers of cardboard equal roughly one foam pad in insulation value. In cold weather, use both.

### Tarp

An 8x10 tarp ($8–$12 at Walmart or Harbor Freight) is one of the most versatile items you can carry: ground cloth, rain shelter, wind block, privacy screen, gear cover. Get grommeted edges. Learn 3 configurations:

- **A-frame:** Ridge line between two trees. Both sides hang down. Sheds rain.
- **Lean-to:** One edge tied high, opposite staked to ground. Blocks wind from one direction.
- **Ground cloth:** Flat under your sleeping bag. Blocks ground moisture.

50 feet of paracord ($4–$6) ties it to trees, poles, or fences. Learn 3 knots:

- **Taut-line hitch:** Adjustable tension. Slide it up or down the line to tighten or loosen. Perfect for guy lines on a tarp.
- **Bowline:** Secure loop that does not slip. Use it to tie off to a tree or post. Will not bind under load.
- **Trucker's hitch:** The most important knot for getting a line drum-tight. It gives you a 3:1 mechanical advantage — multiplies your pulling strength by three. This is what gets your tarp tight enough to shed rain instead of pooling water. Also essential for clotheslines and lashing gear to a pack.

Learn all three at [animatedknots.com](https://www.animatedknots.com) — free step-by-step animations. Download the **Knot IQ** app for offline 3D knot guides you can reference without WiFi. Search YouTube for "trucker's hitch tarp setup" for video walkthroughs. Practice each knot 10 times before you need it.

### Emergency Mylar Blankets

$1 at the dollar store. Reflects 90% of body heat back to you. Loud and not durable, but weighs nothing. Wrap one around your sleeping bag on cold nights for a significant temperature boost. Keep 2–3 in your bag at all times. They also work as a rain poncho, ground cloth, or signal mirror in an emergency.

## Priority 7: Water + Food

### Water

- **Water bottle:** 32 oz minimum, durable. A Nalgene ($10) lasts forever. A stainless steel bottle ($8–$15) can also boil water over a fire. Fill at every opportunity — libraries, gas stations, parks, fast food restaurants, fire station spigots, church spigots.
- **Dehydration impairs thinking before you feel thirsty.** Drink water every hour. In heat above 90°F, you need 8+ bottles per day.
- **Water purification:** If you are drinking from streams, fountains, or unknown sources, Sawyer Squeeze filter ($35–$45) filters 100,000 gallons. Water purification tablets ($7 for 50) as backup. When in doubt, boil for 1 minute.

### Emergency Food (Keep 2–3 Days in Your Bag)

- **Peanut butter** — 190 cal/serving, $2. Protein, fat, shelf-stable. Eat with a spoon or on crackers.
- **Granola bars** — $3–$5 for a box of 6–8. Compact, calorie-dense.
- **Trail mix / mixed nuts** — $3–$5. High calorie, high protein.
- **Beef jerky** — $8–$15 per bag. Expensive but protein-dense, salt, shelf-stable for months. Buy in bulk at Costco or Walmart for better value, or make your own with a car dashboard on a hot day (see [Chapter 24](#ch24)).
- **Crackers** — Individually wrapped, $2–$3. Pair with peanut butter.
- **Canned goods:** Tuna, chicken, beans, ravioli. $1–$2 each. Heavy but calorie-dense. Carry a P-38 can opener (see Tools).
- **Instant oatmeal packets** — $3 for 10. Add hot water from any gas station coffee dispenser.

Rotate these. Do not let them expire in your bag. When you eat one, replace it.

## Priority 8: Tools + Gear

*Every item here earns its weight. If it does not get used weekly, it does not belong in your bag.*

- **Flashlight or headlamp ($8–$15):** A headlamp frees your hands for setting up camp, reading, walking, or searching your bag at night. Carry extra batteries. Headlamp is dramatically more useful than a handheld flashlight.
- **Knife or multitool ($5–$30):** A basic folding knife ($5–$15) or Leatherman-style multitool ($15–$30). Used daily: food prep, cutting cord, opening cans, repairs. Keep in your pocket, not your bag. Note: some shelters prohibit knives. Leave it in your car or hidden stash before intake.
- **Duct tape:** Wrap 10–15 feet around a pencil or water bottle. Repairs tarps, boots, bags, sleeping pads, and clothing. Weighs nothing.
- **Zip ties (10–20 assorted):** Secure tarp grommets, repair zippers, attach gear to your pack, improvise locks.
- **Heavy-duty trash bags (55-gallon, 3–5):** Emergency poncho (cut hole for head). Pack liner to keep everything dry. Ground cloth. Gear cover. Weigh nothing and solve problems daily.
- **P-38 can opener ($1):** Military-issue folding can opener. Weighs less than a quarter. Opens any can without a pull tab. Clip to your keychain. Available at surplus stores or online.
- **Small padlock ($5–$8):** Combination lock (no key to lose). For shelter lockers, storage units, securing your bag to a fence.
- **Carabiner clips, 2–3 ($1–$2 each):** Attach gear to your pack, hang a water bottle, clip your bag to a bed frame. Cheap aluminum from dollar stores.
- **Paracord, 50 feet ($4–$6):** Tarp lines, clotheslines, gear lashing, boot lace replacement. 550-lb test. Cut to length as needed.
- **Sewing needle + dental floss ($2 total):** Dental floss is stronger than thread. Repairs sleeping bags, jackets, bags, and clothing. A heavy-duty needle and 20 feet of floss weigh nothing and save gear that would otherwise be trashed.

## Priority 9: Self-Defense

*You are more vulnerable on the street. Predators target people who look isolated, distracted, or unable to call for help. These items are legal, non-lethal, and designed to create distance — not escalate a fight.*

### What to Carry

- **Tactical flashlight ($10–$20):** 500+ lumens blinds an attacker for 3–5 seconds in the dark. Completely legal everywhere. Also functions as a striking tool if absolutely necessary. Keep it in your pocket or clipped to your belt, not buried in your bag. This is your #1 self-defense tool because it does not look like a weapon.
- **Personal alarm ($5–$10):** Keychain-sized device that emits 120+ decibels when you pull the pin. Louder than a car horn. Attackers do not want attention. Clip to your bag strap where you can reach it instantly.
- **Whistle ($1):** Louder than screaming and requires less energy. Three short blasts is the universal distress signal. Clip to your bag strap or wear around your neck.
- **Pepper spray ($8–$15):** Legal in all 50 states but restrictions vary (size limits in some states, age requirements, banned in some shelters). Effective range: 8–12 feet. One burst to the face gives you 15–30 seconds to run. Check your state’s laws before carrying. Never use indoors or into the wind.
- **Walking stick / trekking pole ($10–$25):** Looks innocent. Extends your reach. Provides balance on rough terrain. Aluminum trekking poles from Walmart are lightweight and collapse to fit alongside your pack.

### What NOT to Carry

- **Knives as weapons:** A utility knife is a tool. A knife carried for self-defense changes the legal equation if you use it. Intent matters in court. Carrying a large fixed-blade knife signals to police that you are a threat, not a victim.
- **Anything illegal:** Brass knuckles, switchblades (in most states), blackjacks, tasers (varies by state). Getting arrested for a weapons charge while homeless creates a cascade: warrant, criminal record, disqualification from shelters, harder to get housing. Not worth it.
- **Firearms:** Legal in many situations, but homeless + firearm = police escalation. You cannot safely store a firearm without housing. Most shelters prohibit them. The risk/reward calculation does not favor carrying one.

### The Real Self-Defense

Situational awareness beats every weapon. Head up. Earbuds out. Know your exits. Travel in well-lit areas. Sleep near other people. Trust your gut — if something feels wrong, leave. De-escalation saves lives. Compliance during a robbery saves lives. Your wallet is not worth a stabbing.

## Priority 10: Hygiene

*Looking and smelling clean keeps you employable, lets you use public spaces without being asked to leave, and preserves your dignity. A $12–$15 kit from Dollar Tree or Walmart covers the basics.*

### The $12–$15 Hygiene Kit

Dollar Tree or Walmart — either works. Dollar Tree is $1.25 per item across the board. Walmart's Great Value brand costs a little more per item but sometimes better quality. Mix and match based on what is near you.

- Toothbrush — $1.25 (Dollar Tree) or $2 (Walmart)
- Toothpaste — $1.25 (Dollar Tree) or $2 (Walmart)
- Deodorant — $1.25 (Dollar Tree small stick) or $3–$4 (Walmart Great Value full size)
- Bar soap in a travel case — $1.25 (Dollar Tree) or $1.50 (Walmart)
- Razor + shaving cream — $1.25 each (Dollar Tree disposable razors work)
- Comb or brush — $1.25
- Wet wipes, 80-pack — $1.25 (Dollar Tree) or $2–$3 (Walmart)
- Hand sanitizer — $1.25 (Dollar Tree) or $1.50 (Walmart)
- Small mirror — $1.25
- Nail clippers — $1.25

### Where to Shower

- **Planet Fitness:** Black Card membership ($25/month) gives access to any location nationwide. Showers, lockers, charging outlets. This is infrastructure, not a luxury.
- **YMCA Open Doors:** Income-based membership. Some locations offer free day passes. Ask at the front desk.
- **Truck stops:** Pilot, Flying J, Love’s. Showers cost $12–$15 but are private and clean. Some truckers give away unused shower credits — ask politely at the fuel desk.
- **Shelters and day centers:** Most offer showers during service hours.
- **Hospital visitor restrooms:** Large hospitals have single-occupancy restrooms. Not technically a shower, but a sink, soap, paper towels, and a locked door. Enough for a wipe-down.

### Laundry

Maintain 2 outfits and rotate. Wear one, wash the other. Laundromat: $2–$4 per load. Sink washing with Dr. Bronner’s soap works for underwear, socks, and base layers — wring out, hang to dry overnight.

## Priority 11: The Dignity Layer

*Survival gear gets you through tonight. Dignity gear gets you out of homelessness. You need both.*

### What to Pack

- **One set of clean, presentable clothes:** Collared shirt or clean top, dark pants or slacks, clean shoes. For job interviews, benefit appointments, shelter intake, court appearances. Keep these separate from your daily wear in a compression sack or clean garbage bag inside your pack. Do not wear them unless you are going somewhere that matters.
- **Basic grooming:** Comb, razor, small mirror. Already in your hygiene kit. Before any appointment, use a restroom to clean up: wash face, comb hair, change into your clean clothes, apply deodorant.
- **Copies of your resume:** If you are job searching, print 5–10 copies while you have access to a printer. Keep them in a folder inside your pack, flat and unwrinkled. Libraries offer free printing in many locations.
- **One personal item:** A book. A journal. A family photo. Something small and lightweight that is yours — not survival, but sanity. Homelessness strips your identity. Keeping one thing that connects you to who you are matters more than you think right now.
- **Notebook and pen:** Write down service provider names, phone numbers, appointment times, your plan. When your cortisol is high, your working memory drops. Externalize your thinking onto paper. Writing things down also saves phone battery — every time you unlock your phone to check a note, you drain power you might need for an emergency call. Your phone is your lifeline. Use paper for everything you can.

> **▶ Why Dignity Matters**
> Research consistently shows that people who maintain personal appearance and daily routines exit homelessness faster. This is not about vanity. Looking presentable opens doors: employers hire you, landlords approve you, service providers take you seriously, and strangers treat you like a person. The moment you stop caring about how you look is the moment the street starts winning.

> **⚠ The Weight Rule: 20–30 Pounds Maximum**
> Military soldiers carry 60–100 lbs, but they are trained for it and have unit support. You are carrying your bag alone, all day, with no break. Over 30 lbs, you get shoulder and back pain within hours, blisters on your feet within days, and you start abandoning gear within a week. Weigh your packed bag on a bathroom scale before you leave. If it is over 30 lbs, take something out.

## The Bag Itself

- **Backpack:** 40–65 liter capacity. Internal frame if possible (distributes weight to hips). Goodwill and thrift stores sell hiking packs for $8–$20. Military surplus MOLLE packs ($20–$40) are bombproof.
- **Backpack rain cover ($5–$10):** This does three things. It keeps your gear dry in rain. It keeps your pack clean so it lasts longer. And most importantly, it conceals your pack — a generic rain cover hides the overstuffed hiking bag that signals homelessness. A plain black or grey cover over any backpack makes it look like a commuter bag. Buy one that fits your pack size or use a large trash bag cinched with paracord as a free alternative.
- **Avoid duffel bags and suitcases.** You need your hands free. A rolling suitcase screams “I just lost my housing” and breaks on rough ground. A backpack distributes weight evenly and leaves your hands free for opening doors, carrying food, and defending yourself.
- **Second bag (optional):** A small daypack or drawstring bag for daily essentials (phone, wallet, water, snacks). Leave your main pack locked at a shelter or hidden at camp. Carry only what you need for the day.

## Where to Store What You Cannot Carry

- **Trusted friend or family:** Free. Get agreement via text. Best option if available.
- **Self-storage unit:** 5x5 (closet): $30–$60/month. 5x10 (half garage): $50–$100/month. Many chains offer first month at $1. Only worth it for items that cost more than 3 months of rent on the unit.
- **Church or nonprofit programs:** Some offer free temporary storage. Call **211** and ask about storage assistance.
- **Your car trunk:** Free but limited space. Keep valuables hidden and locked.

## What to Sell Before You Leave

Start 2–3 weeks early. Desperate last-day sales get 50% less.

- **Holds value:** Power tools (50–70%), electronics (40–60%), musical instruments (40–60%), jewelry (gold/silver). Sell on Facebook Marketplace, OfferUp, Craigslist.
- **Does not hold value:** Furniture (give away), clothing (donate), books (donate), kitchen items (donate).
- **Pawn shops:** 20–40% of value. Fast cash, no waiting for a buyer. Bring ID.

## Storage Unit Survival Guide

If you have a storage unit, understand what happens when you stop paying. Most facilities auction your belongings after 30–60 days of missed payments. The clock starts the day your payment is late.

### The Notification Trap

Facilities must notify you by certified letter before selling your stuff. They send it to the address on file. If you moved and did not update your address, you will not get the notice. Update your address with every facility every time it changes. Use a shelter, PO Box, or General Delivery.

### Before You Miss a Payment

Call the facility before the due date. Many managers grant 2–4 weeks grace. Some negotiate reduced rates or let you downsize to a smaller unit. A tenant who communicates is a tenant they want to keep. A tenant who goes silent gets auctioned.

### If You Are Going to Lose the Unit

Go immediately. Pull out everything irreplaceable: documents, photos, sentimental items. Do this before the lock gets cut. Once the facility puts their lock on your door, everything inside becomes their property.

### The Auto-Pay Trap

If your card expires, gets declined, or gets cancelled, auto-pay fails silently. Late fees accumulate. The lien process starts. Check your storage account monthly. Verify the payment went through.

## City Go-Bag vs. Rural Go-Bag

The master checklist above covers the universal essentials. But where you are changes what you need most. A city has shelters, transit, and services within walking distance. Rural areas have none of that — you are on your own.

### City Adjustments

- **Lighter pack.** Shelters provide beds, day centers provide showers, food banks provide meals. You do not need to carry a full camping setup if services are available. A sleeping bag and foam pad are still smart (shelters fill up), but you can skip the heavy tarp and paracord.
- **Transit money.** Budget $30–$75/month for a bus pass. Walking everywhere burns calories you cannot afford and time you need for appointments.
- **Gym membership.** Planet Fitness Black Card ($25/month) is infrastructure: showers, lockers, charging outlets, climate control. This replaces your need to carry extensive hygiene gear.
- **Padlock is essential.** Shelter lockers are first-come-first-served and you share space with strangers. A combination padlock protects your belongings.
- **Blend in.** Urban survival is partly about not being noticed. A clean backpack and presentable clothes keep security guards, police, and the public from targeting you. Avoid military surplus gear that screams “homeless.”
- **Stealth sleeping gear.** A dark-colored sleeping bag and a compact bivvy sack are less visible than a bright blue tarp if you are sleeping in doorways, parking garages, or stairwells.
- **Earplugs + eye mask ($2).** Cities are loud and bright at night. Shelters are louder. You need sleep. These weigh nothing and dramatically improve rest quality.

### Rural Adjustments

- **Heavier on survival gear.** No shelters, no day centers, no soup kitchens within walking distance. You need to be self-sufficient for days at a time. Full tarp setup, paracord, and sleeping bag rated for the coldest local temperature are non-negotiable.
- **Water purification is critical.** No public fountains, no gas station restrooms every few blocks. Carry a Sawyer Squeeze filter ($35–$45) and water purification tablets ($7) as backup. Know where natural water sources are — streams, springs, rivers. Never drink untreated water from standing sources (ponds, puddles).
- **Fire-starting supplies.** A Bic lighter ($1), waterproof matches ($3), and a magnesium fire starter ($5) as backup. Fire means warmth, cooked food, water purification, and morale. Learn to build a fire before you need one. Practice in your backyard if you still have one.
- **More food.** Carry 5–7 days of food instead of 2–3. No food banks, no soup kitchens, no dumpsters behind restaurants. Calorie-dense and lightweight: rice, dried beans, oatmeal, peanut butter, trail mix, jerky, ramen packets. A small pot ($5–$10) for boiling water and cooking.
- **Navigation.** Cell signal may not exist. Download offline maps before you leave service areas. Carry a compass ($5–$10) and a paper map of your region. Know which direction town is from your camp at all times.
- **Warmer sleeping bag.** No warming centers, no 24-hour businesses, no hospital lobbies. If your bag is not warm enough, you have no backup. Budget for a 0°F or −20°F bag. Military surplus is the best value here ($40–$80).
- **Axe or hatchet ($15–$25).** Processing firewood, clearing brush for camp, splitting kindling. A small hatchet from Walmart or Harbor Freight works. Keep it sharp — dull axes are dangerous.
- **Fishing line + hooks ($3).** Supplemental food source near rivers and lakes. A small kit (50 feet of line, 10 hooks, a few split-shot weights) weighs almost nothing. Check local fishing regulations — many states allow fishing without a license if you are homeless or below the poverty line, but this varies.
- **Signaling.** If you are injured in a remote area, you need to be found. A signal mirror (or the back of a Mylar blanket), a whistle, and a brightly colored bandana can be seen and heard from far away.

### Suburban Adjustments

- **Vehicle is king.** Suburbs are designed for cars, not pedestrians. Without a car, distances between services are brutal. If you have a vehicle, see [Ch7: Vehicle Living](#ch07).
- **Libraries are your base.** Suburban libraries have WiFi, outlets, restrooms, and climate control. They open early and close late. Use them as your daytime headquarters.
- **Church and faith networks.** Suburbs have fewer government services but more churches. Many run food pantries, clothing closets, and informal shelter programs that do not show up on **211**. Walk in and ask.

## Master Packing Checklist

### Priority 1–4: Grab First

- Photo ID, Social Security card, birth certificate
- Medical records, prescriptions, court documents
- Phone + 2 charging cables + wall charger
- Portable battery bank (10,000+ mAh)
- All medications (90-day supply)
- First aid kit
- Electrolyte packets
- Cash (split: body, bank, emergency stash)
- Debit card (Chime / Cash App)

### Priority 5–7: Core Survival

- 2 base layer sets (synthetic or wool)
- 1 fleece or wool mid layer
- 1 waterproof shell jacket
- 3 pairs wool socks
- 3 pairs underwear (synthetic)
- Beanie + glove liners
- Waterproof boots (worn, not packed)
- Sleeping bag (20°F or lower)
- Foam sleeping pad
- Tarp (8x10) + 50ft paracord
- 2–3 Mylar emergency blankets
- 32oz water bottle
- 2–3 days emergency food

### Priority 8–11: Tools, Safety, Dignity

- Headlamp + extra batteries
- Knife or multitool
- Duct tape (wrapped around pencil)
- Zip ties (10–20)
- Heavy-duty trash bags (3–5)
- P-38 can opener
- Padlock (combination)
- Carabiners (2–3)
- Tactical flashlight
- Personal alarm
- Whistle
- Pepper spray (check local laws)
- Hygiene kit ($12–$15, Dollar Tree or Walmart)
- 1 set interview clothes (in compression sack)
- Resume copies (5–10)
- Notebook + pen
- 1 personal item

**Key Takeaways**

- **Documents first.** ID replacement is the hardest logistics problem in homelessness. Secure originals and digital copies before you leave.
- **Phone is survival.** Apply for free service. Buy a battery bank. Download offline maps. Install essential apps.
- **Cotton kills.** Wool and synthetic only. This is the #1 clothing rule.
- **Insulate from the ground.** The ground steals heat 25x faster than air. Never sleep on bare concrete.
- **Split your cash.** Body, bank, emergency stash. Never carry everything in one place.
- **20–30 lbs maximum.** If you cannot carry it for a mile, take something out.
- **Dignity gear gets you out.** One clean outfit and basic grooming open doors that survival gear alone cannot.
