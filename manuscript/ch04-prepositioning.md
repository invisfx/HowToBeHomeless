# Chapter 4: Prepositioning {#ch04}

*Scouting resources before you need them desperately.*

Prepositioning is a military term. It means moving supplies and resources into position before you need them. Soldiers do not wait until they are under fire to figure out where the ammunition is. You should not wait until you are sleeping outside to figure out where the shelters are.

If you still have housing — even a week of it — you have something most homeless people would kill for: time, internet access, a mailing address, and the mental bandwidth to think clearly. Use all of it.

Every phone call you make now, every application you file, every relationship you establish saves you days of scrambling once you are in crisis. The system is not designed to be navigated under stress. It is a maze of phone trees, eligibility requirements, and office hours that assumes you have a stable life.

This chapter is about scouting. You are going to locate every resource you might need, establish contact with the people who control access to those resources, and apply for every benefit you qualify for while your current address still works.

Think of it as building a map before you enter unfamiliar territory. The people who do this step recover faster. The people who skip it spend their first weeks homeless just figuring out what exists.

## Locating Shelters, Food Banks, Clinics, and Day Centers

Your first job is to build an inventory of every service available in your area. Write this down — on paper, in your phone, wherever you will not lose it. You need names, addresses, phone numbers, and hours of operation.

Do this research now, while you have internet access, a quiet place to sit, and enough cognitive bandwidth to process information. Once you are in crisis, you will not have the patience or the battery life to search.

- **Call 211** (or visit [211.org](https://211.org)) — the national clearinghouse for local social services
- **Search “homeless services” + your city** — identify emergency shelters, transitional housing, day centers
- **Map the food infrastructure:** food banks, soup kitchens, church meals, community fridges
- **Locate free or sliding-scale clinics** — Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) cannot turn you away
- **Find day centers** — places you can be inside during the day with access to showers, mail, case management

Write all of this down in one place. A single sheet of paper or a note on your phone with every service, its address, its hours, and its phone number. This list becomes your operating manual.

## Establishing Relationships with Caseworkers

A caseworker is someone in the social services system whose job is to connect people to resources. They work at shelters, day centers, nonprofits, and government agencies. Getting connected to one before you lose housing is one of the most valuable things you can do.

Call **211** and ask to speak with someone about your situation. Visit a local day center or homeless services organization and request an intake appointment. Be honest about your timeline. Having a caseworker who already knows your name and situation before you become homeless changes the entire trajectory of your crisis.

## Applying for Benefits While You Still Have an Address

Your current address is an asset. Use it before you lose it.

### Benefits Application Timeline

Every one of these programs has a processing time. That is why you apply now, not when you are already on the street.

- **SNAP (food stamps):** Apply online in most states. Normal processing: 30 days. **Expedited processing: 7 days** if you have less than $150 in liquid assets and less than $100 in monthly gross income. Tell them you need expedited. The average benefit is $194/month for an individual.
- **Medicaid:** Apply through your state’s marketplace or [healthcare.gov](https://healthcare.gov). In the 40 Medicaid expansion states, single adults under 138% of the federal poverty level qualify. Approval can be same-day in some states. Having active coverage before crisis is critical — ER visits without insurance generate bills that follow you.
- **Unemployment insurance:** File the day you are laid off. Not tomorrow. Not next week. The day of. Benefits start from your filing date, not your layoff date. Most states have online filing. Weekly benefit amounts vary ($200–$600/week) and last 12–26 weeks depending on state.
- **General Assistance (GA):** Small cash aid ($100–$400/month) available in some states and counties. Requirements vary widely. Not available everywhere. Call **211** to check.
- **Lifeline phone program:** Free or discounted phone service if you qualify for any federal assistance. Apply at [lifelinesupport.org](https://lifelinesupport.org).
- **LIHEAP (utility assistance):** Can help pay utility bills or prevent shutoff. May keep the lights on a few more weeks. Apply through **211** or [liheapch.acf.hhs.gov](https://liheapch.acf.hhs.gov).

### How to Apply for SNAP Without an Address

You do not need a permanent address to get food stamps. This is a common misconception that keeps people from applying. Here is how:

- On the application, you can list a shelter address, a friend’s address (with their permission), or write “homeless — no fixed address.”
- For the interview (required in most states), provide a phone number where they can reach you. A cell phone works.
- You can use General Delivery at your local post office to receive the EBT card.
- If you have less than $150 in liquid assets and less than $100 in monthly income, you qualify for 7-day expedited processing. State this clearly on your application.

## How to Find Every Resource in Your Area

There are more programs than you think. The problem is nobody tells you about them. Here is how to find everything available.

### [211.org](https://211.org) Walkthrough

1. Go to [211.org](https://211.org) or dial 2-1-1 on your phone.
2. Enter your zip code.
3. Search by category: Housing, Food, Health Care, Utility Assistance, Mental Health, Employment.
4. Each result shows the organization name, address, phone number, hours, and eligibility requirements.
5. Call each one. Do not just read the website. Call and ask: “I am about to lose my housing. What services do you offer and how do I qualify?”

### [findhelp.org](https://findhelp.org) (Formerly Aunt Bertha)

1. Go to [findhelp.org](https://findhelp.org).
2. Enter your zip code.
3. Browse categories: Food, Housing, Goods, Transit, Health, Money, Care, Education, Work, Legal.
4. Results include eligibility details, application links, and contact information.
5. This site is updated more frequently than most government databases. Use it.

### Other Resources to Check

- **Local United Way:** [unitedway.org](https://unitedway.org) — funds many of the programs **211** connects you to.
- **Salvation Army:** Emergency assistance, food, shelter, utility help. Walk in or call your local office.
- **Catholic Charities:** Available to anyone regardless of religion. Emergency assistance, food, housing help.
- **St. Vincent de Paul:** Emergency rent/utility assistance, food pantries, clothing.
- **Local community action agencies:** Search “community action agency [your county].” They administer LIHEAP, weatherization, and emergency assistance.

## Establishing a Mailing Address

You need an address. Benefits mail, job offers, court documents, and medical correspondence all need somewhere to go. Here are your options.

- **USPS General Delivery:** Free. Go to your local post office and ask to set up General Delivery. Mail is addressed to your name, General Delivery, [City, State, ZIP]. You pick it up at the counter with ID. Not all post offices participate — call first.
- **PO Box:** $20–$50 for 6 months at USPS. Provides a stable address. Some benefit applications do not accept PO Boxes — use the street address format instead (e.g., “123 Main St #456”).
- **UPS Store mailbox:** $15–$30/month. Gives you a real street address, not a PO Box number. More expensive but accepted everywhere.
- **Shelter address:** Many shelters allow you to receive mail there. Ask during intake.
- **Day center address:** Some day centers provide mail service. Ask.
- **Trusted friend or family:** Use their address with permission. Set up USPS Informed Delivery (free) to get email scans of incoming mail so you do not have to visit daily.

## Who to Tell and Exactly What to Say

Disclosure is a strategic decision. Who you tell and how you frame it determines whether people help you, pity you, or disappear. Here are scripts for each conversation.

- **Tell:** People with spare rooms, practical skills, or professional connections to social services
- **Tell carefully:** Close family who might help but might also judge or try to control the situation
- **Do not tell:** Acquaintances, coworkers (unless necessary), anyone who gossips, anyone who would use it against you

### Script: Asking a Friend to Stay

“I need to be straightforward with you. I’m losing my apartment on [date]. I have a plan to get back on my feet, but I need a place to crash for [specific timeframe — one week, two weeks]. I’ll stay out of your way, keep the space clean, and I’m happy to [cook, help with chores, pay what I can]. Would that be possible?”

### Script: Telling Family

“I want you to know what’s going on with me so you hear it from me first. I lost my job [or: I can’t make rent], and I’m going to lose my apartment on [date]. I have a plan — I’ve applied for [benefits, shelter, etc.]. I’m not asking you for money. I wanted you to know so you’re not blindsided.”

If you are asking for help, be specific: “What would help me most right now is [a place to store three boxes / a ride to the shelter / help with my phone bill for one month].” Specific requests get better results than vague pleas.

### Script: Telling Your Employer (If You Still Have a Job)

“I’m going through a housing transition and I want to make sure it doesn’t affect my work. I may need to update my address on file in the next few weeks. Is there anything I need to do on my end for HR?”

Do not overshare with coworkers. HR may have resources: Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs) often include emergency financial assistance, counseling, and referrals.

### Script: Telling Your Landlord (Negotiating)

“I want to discuss options for my lease. I’m unable to continue paying rent as of [date]. I’d like to propose [a cash-for-keys arrangement / a voluntary departure by a specific date / a payment plan]. I want to leave the unit in good condition and avoid a formal eviction for both our sakes. Can we put something in writing?”

> **♟ The Game: Information Asymmetry**
> Before you lose housing, you have an information advantage over the systems you will need. You can research shelters, learn eligibility rules, and preposition resources while you still have internet, a mailing address, and cognitive bandwidth. Once you are in crisis, every bureaucratic interaction becomes a blind negotiation. The caseworker knows the rules; you do not. Preposition now: learn what you qualify for, what documentation you need, and who the gatekeepers are. This is the last moment where the information asymmetry favors you.

## If You Have Kids

If you have children, your planning becomes more urgent and more complicated. But you also have access to resources that single adults do not. Family homelessness is treated differently by the system — usually with higher priority.

### McKinney-Vento Act: Your Children’s Rights

Federal law protects homeless children’s education. This is not optional for schools — it is the law.

- Your children have the right to **stay enrolled in their current school**, even if you move out of the district or to a different city.
- The school **must provide transportation** to and from the school of origin, at no cost to you.
- Your children can **enroll immediately** in a new school even without the records that are normally required (immunization records, proof of residency, birth certificate, school records). The school must enroll them and request records afterward.
- Every school district has a **McKinney-Vento liaison**. This is the person whose job it is to help you. Call the school district office and ask for the homeless liaison by name. They can connect you to free meals, school supplies, tutoring, and other family services.
- Children qualify as “homeless” under McKinney-Vento if they are living in a shelter, motel, car, campground, doubled up with another family due to economic hardship, or in substandard housing.

### CPS and Custody Fears

This is the fear that keeps parents from seeking help. Let’s address it directly.

- **Being homeless is NOT child neglect.** Courts and child protective services cannot remove your children solely because you do not have housing.
- What CPS investigates is whether children are being fed, supervised, safe, and attending school. If you are doing all of those things, homelessness alone is not grounds for removal.
- Document everything. Keep records of where your children sleep, what they eat, their school attendance. If anyone ever questions your parenting, you want a paper trail.
- If you are in a custody dispute, homelessness can be used against you. Get legal help immediately. Contact your local Legal Aid office — find one at [lawhelp.org](https://lawhelp.org).

### Family Shelters

- Family shelters prioritize families with children, especially young children.
- They typically offer longer stays than single-adult shelters (30–90 days vs. overnight).
- Many provide case management, childcare, and employment assistance.
- Intake is often different — call ahead rather than walk in. Many require a referral from **211** or a social worker.
- If no family shelter has space, many cities have hotel/motel voucher programs for families. Ask **211** about emergency family housing vouchers.

> **⚠ Warning**
> Never leave your children unsupervised in a car, tent, or public space while you handle logistics. Take them with you. If you need childcare, ask the McKinney-Vento liaison, shelter staff, or **211** about emergency childcare options. Leaving children alone, even briefly, is what triggers CPS involvement.

## If You Have Pets

Your pet is family. The system does not see it that way. Most shelters will not let your dog or cat through the door. That means you need a plan for your animal before you need one, or you will be forced to choose between shelter and your pet at the worst possible moment.

- **Pet-friendly shelters:** They exist but are rare. Call ahead. Some require vaccination records. The ASPCA maintains a list of pet-friendly shelters at [aspca.org](https://aspca.org).
- **Temporary foster programs:** Organizations like PAWS (Pets Are Wonderful Support), Safe Place for Pets, and local rescues offer emergency foster care while you stabilize. Your pet is not surrendered — it is fostered until you are housed again.
- **Friends or family:** Ask someone to keep your pet temporarily. Be specific about the timeframe. Get agreement in writing (text is fine).
- **Veterinary care:** Many areas have free or low-cost vet clinics for people experiencing homelessness. Call your local Humane Society for referrals.
- **Pet food assistance:** Many food banks also distribute pet food. Ask specifically. Some programs (like Pet Food Pantry) deliver pet food to people in need.
- **Never surrender your pet to a kill shelter.** If you must temporarily give up your animal, use a no-kill rescue or a foster network. Once surrendered to a kill shelter, you may not get your pet back.

If you are living in your car with a pet, keep water available at all times, never leave your pet in a hot car, and check local leash laws. Some cities have “pet rest areas” in parks where you can let your animal exercise.

> **✔ Practical Tip**
> Keep your pet’s vaccination records with your own documents. Some shelters, foster programs, and housing applications require them. If you do not have records, call your vet and ask for copies before you lose your phone number or address.

## The Dry Run

Soldiers do not deploy to a combat zone without field exercises. Preppers do not wait for an actual emergency to test their bug-out bag. Backpackers do not attempt a hundred-mile thru-hike without a shakedown trip. And you should not spend your first night sleeping rough without ever having practiced it.

A dry run is exactly what it sounds like: you simulate the experience before it counts. You sleep outside, live from your bag, and discover what works and what does not — while you still have the option of going back inside to fix it.

The value of a dry run is not just logistical. It is psychological. The first night sleeping outside is terrifying because everything is unknown. The sounds, the cold, the vulnerability, the discomfort — your brain treats all of it as a threat because it has no frame of reference. A dry run gives your brain that reference. It converts the unknown into the known, and the known is always less frightening than the unknown.

One night in your backyard will teach you more than reading this entire book.

### Progressive Difficulty

Do not start by sleeping under a highway overpass downtown. Build up to it. Each step introduces new variables while keeping the stakes manageable.

1. **Your backyard.** This is the safest possible version. You are twenty feet from your back door. If it rains, if you get cold, if something goes wrong, you walk inside. But commit to the exercise. Set up as if you cannot go inside. Use only the gear you would actually have.
2. **A local campground.** Now you are away from home but in a controlled environment with other people, bathrooms, and a defined space. Pay the $15 to $30 for a site. This is where you test your sleep system, your cooking setup, and your ability to be comfortable without a bed.
3. **An urban park.** Now you are closer to the real environment. Check local ordinances first — some parks allow overnight camping, most do not. Choose a park where you can arrive late and leave early. This is where you learn what urban nighttime sounds like and how it feels to sleep in a space you do not control.
4. **Downtown or near a shelter.** This is the full simulation. You are sleeping where homeless people actually sleep. You learn the rhythms, the noise, the foot traffic, the police patterns, and the reality of spending a night outside in an urban environment.

You do not need to do all four. Even step one — one night in your backyard — gives you information you cannot get any other way.

### What to Test

Your dry run is a systems check. You are testing every piece of gear and every assumption you have about what you need. Here is what to evaluate:

- **Sleeping bag warmth rating.** Is your bag warm enough? Most people underestimate how cold it gets at ground level. If you are cold at 3 AM in your backyard in October, you will be dangerously cold on a concrete sidewalk in December. Your bag’s temperature rating is a survival rating, not a comfort rating — you need a bag rated 10 to 15 degrees below the lowest temperature you expect.
- **Tarp or shelter setup.** Can you set it up in the dark? In the rain? In wind? How long does it take? A tarp that takes 20 minutes to rig in daylight with no pressure will take 40 minutes in the dark when you are tired and it is already raining. Practice until you can do it in under 10 minutes by feel.
- **Cooking system.** If you have a camp stove or alcohol burner, test it. Can you boil water? Heat a can of soup? Do it without drawing attention? How much fuel do you use per meal? Do you have a way to carry fuel safely?
- **Water purification.** Test your filter, your purification tablets, or whatever system you plan to use. Know how long tablets take (usually 30 minutes to 4 hours depending on the type). Know how many liters your filter handles before it needs replacing.
- **Pack weight.** Carry your full bag for a mile. Then two miles. Then five. If your shoulders and back are screaming after a mile, you are carrying too much. You need to be able to walk with everything you own for extended distances without injury. Target 20 to 30 pounds maximum.
- **Comfort on the ground.** A sleeping pad is not optional — it is insulation. The ground pulls heat from your body faster than cold air does. Test whether a foam pad, an inflatable pad, or a combination of cardboard layers keeps you warm and comfortable enough to actually sleep.
- **Phone battery.** How long does your phone last with normal use? With GPS? With the screen on? How long does your portable battery charger last? Your phone is your lifeline — know exactly how many hours of power you have.

### The Weekend Drill

If you have a weekend to spare, run a full simulation. This is the most valuable exercise in this entire chapter.

**Friday 6:00 PM:** Pack your bag with everything you would carry. Nothing else. Walk out the front door and do not go back inside until Sunday morning. Set up camp in your backyard, a park, or a campground.

**Friday night through Saturday morning:** Sleep outside. Use only what you packed. Wake up and assess: Were you warm enough? Dry? Did you sleep at all? What woke you up? What did you wish you had?

**Saturday full day:** Live from your bag for an entire day. Where do you eat? Where do you charge your phone? Where do you use the bathroom? Where do you go when you have nothing to do? Walk the routes you would actually walk. Visit the resources you scouted. Spend the day experiencing what a full day without housing actually feels like.

**Saturday night:** Sleep outside again. The second night is usually better than the first because your brain has started to normalize the experience.

**Sunday morning debrief:** Go back inside. Sit down with your gear list and revise it based on what you learned. What did you use? What did you never touch? What did you desperately wish you had? What was too heavy? What broke?

### Dry Run Evaluation Checklist

After your dry run, rate each category and note what needs to change:

- **Warmth:** Were you warm enough to sleep? If not, what failed — bag, pad, clothing, shelter?
- **Dryness:** Did you stay dry? Would your setup survive rain? Heavy rain?
- **Sleep quality:** Did you actually sleep, or just lie there? What kept you awake — cold, noise, discomfort, anxiety?
- **Food:** Did you eat enough? Was your cooking system functional and discreet?
- **Water:** Did you have enough? Was your purification system workable?
- **Phone battery:** How much charge did you have left after 24 hours? 48 hours?
- **Physical comfort:** Any pain from sleeping on the ground? Sore shoulders from the pack? Blisters from walking?
- **Security feeling:** Did you feel safe? What would make you feel safer? What made you feel exposed?
- **What you forgot:** Write down everything you wished you had and did not. This is the most valuable part of the exercise.

### The Real Lesson

The dry run does two things that nothing else can do. First, it shows you what is wrong with your gear and your plan while you can still fix it. Discovering at 2 AM that your sleeping bag is not warm enough is a nuisance in your backyard and a medical emergency on a park bench in January.

Second — and this is the part that matters more — it reduces the fear. The first night outside is terrifying because your brain has no model for it. After a dry run, your brain has a model. It knows what the ground feels like, what the sounds are, what the cold is like, and that you survived all of it. The unknown is always scarier than the reality. A dry run converts the unknown into experience, and experience is the foundation of confidence.

You would not jump out of an airplane without training. Do not jump into homelessness without a practice run.

**Key Takeaways**

- **Scout everything now.** [211.org](https://211.org), [findhelp.org](https://findhelp.org), United Way, Salvation Army, Catholic Charities — map every resource.
- **Apply for benefits today.** SNAP (7-day expedited), Medicaid (same-day possible), unemployment (file day of layoff).
- **You do not need an address for SNAP.** Write “homeless” on the application. Use General Delivery for mail.
- **Establish a mailing address.** General Delivery (free), PO Box ($20–$50/6 months), shelter, or a friend’s address.
- **Be strategic about disclosure.** Use the scripts. Specific requests get better results than vague pleas.
- **Kids have rights.** McKinney-Vento requires schools to enroll immediately and provide transportation. Find the district liaison.
- **Being homeless is not neglect.** Document that you are feeding, supervising, and schooling your children.
- **Plan for pets now.** Foster programs, pet-friendly shelters, and friends are options. Never use a kill shelter.
- **Do a dry run.** One night in your backyard teaches more than any book. Test your gear, fix what fails, build confidence.
