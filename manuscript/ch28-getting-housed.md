# Chapter 28: Getting Back into Housing {#ch28}

*Renting with gaps, landlord conversations, deposits, and roommates.*

You found a place. Or rather, you found a listing. Between where you are now and a key in your hand, there is a gauntlet of applications, background checks, credit reports, and conversations that feel designed to disqualify you. The housing market does not care about your story. It cares about your credit score, your rental history, and your ability to produce a pay stub. If you are coming from homelessness, you may have none of those things.

This chapter is about navigating that gauntlet. Some of the strategies here are straightforward. Others require finesse, timing, and the kind of selective honesty that makes most people uncomfortable. The goal is not to deceive anyone. The goal is to present your situation in the way most likely to result in a yes, because a landlord who never gives you a chance cannot discover that you are a reliable tenant.

The rental market is not a level playing field. Landlords are risk-averse by nature and by economics. A bad tenant costs thousands of dollars and months of time. Your job is to reduce the perceived risk of renting to you. Every strategy in this chapter serves that single objective.

You will face rejection. Probably multiple rejections. Each one will feel personal, even though it is not. The landlord who says no is not judging your worth as a human being. They are making a business decision with incomplete information. Your job is to give them better information, or to find a landlord who weighs different factors.

## Renting with a Gap in History

A gap in your rental history is a red flag to landlords. It raises questions you may not want to answer. The good news is that gaps are common and there are established ways to address them.

The first option is honesty, carefully framed. "I went through a difficult period and was staying with family" is truthful and raises fewer concerns than "I was homeless." The stigma is real, and you are not obligated to share your full story with someone who holds power over your housing.

The second option is to provide alternative references. If you stayed in a shelter, some shelter staff will serve as references. If you had a caseworker, they can vouch for your reliability. Employers, faith leaders, and community members who know you can fill the reference gap that a previous landlord would normally occupy.

### Bad Credit and How to Work Around It

Your credit score may have taken damage during your period of homelessness. Unpaid bills, collections accounts, and gaps in credit activity all leave marks. Some strategies for navigating this reality:

- **Offer a larger security deposit.** Money talks. A landlord who is nervous about your credit may feel differently if you can put down an extra month's rent. Some deposit assistance programs will cover this.
- **Find landlords who do not run credit.** Individual landlords and small property owners are more likely to skip formal credit checks than large property management companies.
- **Get a co-signer.** If anyone in your network has good credit and is willing, a co-signer can make an otherwise rejected application viable.
- **Show proof of income.** Steady income, even if modest, can offset a poor credit score. Pay stubs, bank statements, or a letter from your employer carry weight.

### Renting with a Criminal Record

This is one of the hardest barriers. Many landlords conduct background checks, and a criminal record can be an automatic disqualification. However, some jurisdictions have passed "ban the box" laws that limit when and how landlords can consider criminal history. Know your local laws. Some programs specifically help people with records find housing. Ask your caseworker about these resources.

## Landlord Conversations

The conversation with a potential landlord is a negotiation, even when it does not feel like one. You are selling yourself as a tenant. The landlord is evaluating risk. Understanding what landlords actually worry about can help you address their concerns before they become objections.

Landlords worry about three things: will you pay rent on time, will you damage the property, and will you cause problems with other tenants. Everything you say and do in the application process should address these three concerns.

### What to Say

- **Lead with stability signals.** Employment, steady income, references from authority figures. Present these first.
- **Be honest but strategic.** You do not need to volunteer every detail of your history. Answer questions truthfully, but frame your answers around your current stability and future reliability.
- **Address the gap proactively.** If your application has obvious gaps, a brief, confident explanation is better than leaving the landlord to imagine the worst.
- **Ask about their concerns.** "Is there anything about my application I can address?" gives you a chance to respond to objections rather than being silently rejected.

## Security Deposits and Move-In Costs

The move-in cost is often the tallest wall between you and housing. First month's rent, last month's rent, security deposit, application fees. In an expensive market, you may need two to four thousand dollars before you can sign a lease.

Sources of deposit assistance include rapid rehousing programs, Salvation Army, St. Vincent de Paul, local churches, community action agencies, and some employer programs. Apply to multiple sources simultaneously. Do not wait for one to respond before applying to the next.

## Roommate Situations

Roommates reduce costs but increase complexity. If you are considering a roommate situation, protect yourself. Get everything in writing. Understand whose name is on the lease. Know what happens if the other person stops paying. A roommate agreement is not paranoia. It is basic risk management for someone who cannot afford another housing failure.

> **&#10004; Practical Tip**
> When viewing apartments, bring a folder with your documents organized: ID, proof of income, references, and a brief personal statement. Prepared applicants stand out. Landlords remember the person who had their paperwork ready.

## Where to Find Affordable Housing

The cheapest path back into housing is not an apartment. It is a room. A rented room in a shared house runs $400 to $800 per month in most cities. You get a bedroom with shared kitchen and bathroom. No credit check in many cases. Move-in cost is often just first month plus a small deposit. This is how most people actually exit homelessness when they do it without a program.

### Where to Search

- **Craigslist > Rooms & Shares.** Filter by price. Ignore anything that asks for money before viewing. Meet the person. See the room. Trust your gut.
- **Facebook Marketplace.** Search "room for rent" in your city. Facebook groups like "[Your City] Housing" or "[Your City] Rooms for Rent" often have listings that never make it to other platforms.
- **Socialserve.com.** Free affordable housing search engine. Covers most states. Filter by price, location, and accessibility. Includes subsidized and market-rate units.
- **HUD housing search (hud.gov).** Search for HUD-approved housing in your area. Includes public housing, Section 8 properties, and affordable developments.
- **Local housing authority website.** Every county has one. They maintain lists of affordable units, waitlist openings, and emergency housing.
- **Roomies.com and SpareRoom.** Roommate-matching platforms. Free to browse. You create a profile, search listings, and message potential roommates.

### Private Landlords vs. Corporate Management

Private landlords own one to five properties. They make their own decisions. They can waive a credit check, accept a letter instead of a pay stub, and take a chance on someone whose application would be auto-rejected by a property management company. Corporate management companies use algorithmic screening. If your credit score is below 600 or you have an eviction on record, you will not pass their system. Target private landlords. Look for "for rent" signs in windows, handwritten Craigslist ads, and properties listed by owner on Facebook.

## Security Deposit Assistance

You should not assume you need to save the entire deposit yourself. Multiple organizations provide deposit assistance. Apply to all of them at the same time.

- **Salvation Army:** Grants up to $1,500 for security deposits and first month's rent. Call your local branch and ask for the housing assistance coordinator.
- **Catholic Charities:** Emergency financial assistance for housing. You do not need to be Catholic. Contact your local diocese office.
- **Community Action Agencies:** Every county has one. They administer state and federal emergency assistance funds. Search "community action agency [your county]."
- **SSVF (veterans only):** Full move-in costs including deposit, first month, utilities. The fastest program in the system.
- **State and county emergency assistance:** Most states have emergency housing funds. Call 211 and ask specifically about deposit assistance.
- **Churches:** Many churches have discretionary funds for housing emergencies. Call and ask. You do not need to be a member. Start with larger churches; they typically have bigger budgets.

## Roommate Strategies

A roommate cuts your costs in half. It also doubles your risk if you choose the wrong person. After homelessness, another housing failure is not an inconvenience. It is a catastrophe. Vet your roommates like your housing depends on it, because it does.

### How to Find a Roommate

- **Craigslist > Rooms & Shares.** The largest roommate market. High volume, mixed quality. Respond to multiple ads.
- **Roomies.com / SpareRoom.** Structured profiles with lifestyle preferences. Better vetting than Craigslist.
- **Facebook groups.** Search "[Your City] Roommates" or "[Your City] Housing." Community-moderated groups tend to have more accountability.
- **Word of mouth.** Ask caseworkers, shelter staff, day center regulars, coworkers. A referral from someone you trust is worth ten Craigslist responses.

### How to Vet a Roommate

- **Meet in public first.** Coffee shop, library, day center. Never go to someone's home alone for a first meeting.
- **Ask for references.** A previous roommate or landlord. If they cannot provide one, that is information.
- **Ask about employment.** How do they pay rent? If the answer is vague, the risk is high.
- **Trust your gut.** If something feels off, it probably is. You have survival instincts that housed people do not. Use them.
- **Ask about substances.** Not to judge. To know. If you are in recovery, living with someone who uses is a relapse trigger. If you use, living with someone who does not may create conflict.

### The Written Agreement

This is non-negotiable. Get it in writing before you move in. It does not need to be a legal document. A signed piece of paper that both parties keep a copy of is enough. Cover these points:

- How much each person pays and when it is due
- How utilities are split
- Cleaning responsibilities (be specific: who cleans what, how often)
- Guest policy (overnight guests, frequency, duration)
- Move-out terms (how much notice, what happens to the deposit)
- What happens if one person cannot pay rent one month

> **&#9888; Warning**
> If your name is not on the lease, you have fewer legal protections. If your roommate's name is on the lease and they decide to kick you out, your options are limited. Try to get on the lease yourself, or at minimum keep a copy of your written agreement and proof of rent payments. Venmo or bank transfer receipts are better than cash for this reason.

## First Apartment Setup for $100

You do not need furniture. You need the minimum to sleep, eat, clean, and stay hygienic. Here is what that actually costs if you shop at Dollar Tree, Walmart, and Goodwill:

- **Air mattress:** $20 (Walmart). Get the twin size with a built-in pump. It will last three to six months while you find a real mattress.
- **Sheets and blanket:** $10 (Goodwill). Any flat sheet and a blanket. Wash them before use.
- **Pillow:** $5 (Walmart or Dollar Tree)
- **Towels (2):** $5 (Dollar Tree or Goodwill)
- **Dishes, cups, utensils:** $10 (Dollar Tree). One plate, one bowl, one cup, one set of utensils, one pot, one pan. That is all you need.
- **Basic cleaning supplies:** $10 (Dollar Tree). All-purpose cleaner, sponge, dish soap, trash bags, broom.
- **Toilet paper, soap, toothpaste, shampoo:** $15 (Dollar Tree)
- **Plastic drawers for clothes:** $15 (Walmart, 3-drawer unit)
- **Can opener and basic cooking tools:** $5 (Dollar Tree)

**Total: approximately $95.** That is your entire apartment setup. Everything else --- a couch, a table, a real bed --- comes later. Free furniture appears constantly on Craigslist free section, Facebook Marketplace free listings, and Buy Nothing groups. Check daily. People give away mattresses, dressers, tables, and couches every day. You just need to be able to pick them up.

### Free Furniture Sources

- **Habitat for Humanity ReStores:** Deeply discounted furniture and household items. Some items under $10.
- **Furniture banks:** Many cities have furniture banks that provide free furniture to people exiting homelessness. Ask your caseworker.
- **Buy Nothing groups:** Facebook-based local gifting communities. Join the one for your neighborhood.
- **Craigslist > Free section:** Check daily. Move fast. Free furniture goes within hours.
- **Move-out day at apartments:** The last day of the month, people leave furniture by the dumpster. Walk through apartment complex parking lots on the 30th and 31st.

> **&#9888; Warning**
> Rent-to-own furniture stores specifically target people in your situation. The total cost of a rent-to-own couch can be three to five times its retail price. A sleeping bag on the floor of your own apartment is better than a debt trap disguised as a sofa. Never rent to own. Ever.

**Key Takeaways**

- **Rented rooms ($400--$800/month) are the fastest path.** Lower barrier than a full apartment.
- **Target private landlords.** They make their own decisions. Corporate screening will reject you.
- **Apply to every deposit assistance program at once.** Salvation Army, Catholic Charities, Community Action, churches.
- **Vet roommates carefully.** Meet in public. Check references. Get everything in writing.
- **Set up your apartment for $100.** Air mattress, Dollar Tree basics, Goodwill towels. Everything else comes later free.
- **Never rent to own furniture.** A sleeping bag on your own floor beats a $3,000 couch payment plan.
