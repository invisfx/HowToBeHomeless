# Chapter 32: Climate Zones {#ch32}

*Homelessness is different everywhere. These profiles cover the survival variables that change by geography.*

Weather kills more homeless people than violence, drugs, or disease. It kills slowly through cumulative exposure, and it kills quickly through hypothermia and heat stroke. The strategies that keep you alive in Phoenix will get you killed in Minneapolis. The gear that saves you in Seattle is dead weight in Tucson. Geography is not background. It is the single most important variable in your survival equation.

This chapter profiles five broad climate zones across the United States. Each profile covers the primary environmental threats, the gear priorities specific to that region, the cultural and political landscape around homelessness, and the survival strategies that locals have developed through hard experience. These profiles are starting points, not comprehensive guides. Conditions vary by city, by neighborhood, and by season. But the fundamental patterns hold.

If you have any choice in where you are homeless, that choice matters more than almost any other decision you will make. Climate, services, enforcement culture, and cost of living vary so dramatically across the country that the same person with the same resources can be in a survivable situation in one city and a lethal one in another. If mobility is possible, read this chapter before you decide where to go. If you are stuck where you are, read your zone's profile carefully and plan accordingly.

## Desert Southwest

### Phoenix, Las Vegas, Tucson

The desert is survivable for eight months of the year and potentially lethal for four. Summer temperatures routinely exceed 110 degrees Fahrenheit, and the ground-level radiant heat in urban areas can be twenty degrees higher than the air temperature. Heat is the primary lethality vector in the desert Southwest.

- **Extreme heat as the primary lethality vector.** More homeless people die from heat exposure in Phoenix than from any other cause. The danger window is June through September, with July and August being the deadliest months. Pavement and concrete retain heat for hours after sunset.
- **Cooling centers, shade mapping, hydration calculus.** Know where every cooling center is before summer arrives. Map shaded areas by time of day. Your water needs in extreme heat are one gallon per day minimum, more if you are walking. Dehydration impairs judgment before it impairs your body.
- **Overnight viability vs. daytime danger.** Desert nights can be surprisingly comfortable, even cold in winter. The survival strategy inverts: sleep during the day in shade, move and handle business at night and early morning.
- **Anti-camping enforcement culture.** Cities in the desert Southwest have been among the most aggressive in the country at enforcing camping bans. Phoenix, Scottsdale, and Las Vegas regularly conduct sweeps. Know the enforcement patterns and have a plan for where to go when your spot is cleared.

## Pacific Northwest

### Portland, Seattle

The Pacific Northwest does not get as cold as the Midwest, but it compensates with relentless moisture. Rain that never quite stops, air that never quite dries, gear that never quite dries out. Hypothermia in the PNW does not require freezing temperatures. Wet cold at forty-five degrees can kill you.

- **Relentless wet cold --- hypothermia without freezing temperatures.** The combination of rain, wind, and temperatures in the 35--50 degree range is more dangerous than dry cold at lower temperatures. Wet clothing loses ninety percent of its insulating value.
- **Historically tolerant culture now shifting politically.** Portland and Seattle have historically been more tolerant of visible homelessness than most American cities. That tolerance is eroding rapidly as political attitudes shift and new enforcement measures take effect.
- **Gear priorities: waterproofing over warmth.** In the PNW, your number one gear priority is staying dry. A quality rain layer and a waterproof tarp are more important than a heavy sleeping bag. Wool and synthetic fabrics retain warmth when wet. Cotton is lethal.
- **Encampment culture and dynamics.** The PNW has some of the most established encampment cultures in the country. Understanding camp dynamics, contribution norms, and governance structures is essential if you plan to join one.

## The Upper Midwest & Northeast

### Chicago, Minneapolis, New York, Boston

Winter in the Upper Midwest and Northeast is an existential threat. Wind chills below negative thirty degrees are common in Chicago and Minneapolis. Frostbite can begin in fifteen minutes on exposed skin. Surviving an unsheltered winter in these cities requires either excellent gear, reliable access to warming centers, or both.

- **Winter as existential threat: frostbite, hypothermia, wind chill.** The cold in these cities is not uncomfortable. It is deadly. Your body loses heat faster than it can produce it, and the timeline from "cold" to "hypothermic" can be measured in hours.
- **Warming centers and the overnight survival circuit.** Most cities in this zone operate warming centers during cold weather emergencies. Knowing where they are, when they open, and how to get there is non-negotiable. The overnight survival circuit --- moving between heated spaces to stay alive until morning --- is a core skill.
- **NYC right-to-shelter vs. everywhere else.** New York City is one of the only cities in the country with a legal right to shelter. This does not mean the shelters are good. It means they cannot turn you away. Outside of NYC, shelter is first-come, first-served, and there are never enough beds.
- **Transit systems as de facto shelter.** Subway systems, commuter rail, and bus routes serve as informal shelter for thousands of people every night. Each system has its own rules, enforcement patterns, and risks. Learn them.

## The South

### Houston, Atlanta, Miami, New Orleans

The South presents a different survival calculus. Winters are mild enough that exposure deaths are rare, but summers bring a combination of heat and humidity that creates its own dangers. The region also faces unique weather threats that other zones do not.

- **Heat and humidity: a different calculus than dry desert.** Humid heat is more dangerous than dry heat because sweat does not evaporate effectively. Your body's cooling system fails. Heat exhaustion and heat stroke are real risks from May through October.
- **Hurricane season as a unique threat for unsheltered people.** June through November brings the risk of hurricanes and tropical storms. If you are unsheltered during a hurricane, you need an evacuation plan. Shelters of last resort open during major storms, but getting to them requires advance planning.
- **Houston Housing First model as a case study.** Houston has reduced its homeless population more successfully than almost any major American city, largely through aggressive Housing First implementation. If you are homeless in Houston, the infrastructure to help you exit is better than most places.
- **Lower cost of living but fewer services.** Southern cities generally have lower rents and lower costs of living, which can make the exit to housing easier. But they also tend to have fewer shelters, fewer services, and less public transit.

## Rural America

Rural homelessness is the most invisible form of homelessness in the country. There are no encampments, no panhandlers on corners, no visible crisis. Instead, there are people living in cars parked on forest roads, people doubling up in trailers, people camping on public land and hoping nobody notices. The lack of visibility means the lack of services.

- **The invisible homeless population.** HUD's point-in-time count dramatically undercounts rural homelessness because the methodology is designed for cities. People sleeping in the woods, in barns, or in abandoned structures are rarely counted. This invisibility means fewer resources allocated to rural areas.
- **No public transit, vast distances, fewer shelters.** In rural America, the nearest shelter may be fifty miles away. The nearest social services office may require a full-day trip. Without a vehicle, accessing help is nearly impossible. This isolation is the defining challenge of rural homelessness.
- **Community-based help vs. institutional support.** What rural areas lack in institutional services, they sometimes compensate for with community-based help: churches, neighbors, local charities. These informal networks can be lifelines, but they are inconsistent and sometimes come with strings attached.
- **Vehicle dependency and the consequences of breakdown.** If you are homeless in rural America, your vehicle is everything. When it breaks down, your housing, transportation, and storage all disappear simultaneously. Preventive maintenance and a mechanical breakdown plan are critical.

## Desert Southwest: Specific Heat Survival

When ambient temperature hits 110°F, you have two to three hours of direct sun exposure before heat stroke becomes likely. Heat stroke is a medical emergency. Your body stops sweating. Your core temperature spikes. Without intervention, you die. This is not theoretical. Phoenix recorded over 300 heat-related deaths among unsheltered people in a single year.

### The Hour-by-Hour Heat Protocol

- **5am--10am:** Move. Handle all outdoor business. Walk to appointments. Get food. This is your productive window.
- **10am--5pm:** Get inside. Cooling centers, libraries, malls, day centers. If you cannot get inside, find deep shade and do not move. Every step generates body heat.
- **5pm--8pm:** Cooling centers close. This is the danger gap. Pavement radiates stored heat. Ground-level temperatures can be 20°F higher than air temperature. Stay off concrete and asphalt.
- **After dark:** Temperatures drop 20--30 degrees. Desert nights can be comfortable. This is your second active window.

### Hydration Math

One gallon of water per person per day. Minimum. That is 128 ounces. A standard water bottle is 16.9 ounces. You need eight bottles per day. In extreme heat, you need more. Do not wait until you are thirsty. By the time you feel thirst, you are already dehydrated. Dehydration impairs judgment before it impairs your body. You will make bad decisions before you feel sick.

### Emergency Cooling

- Wet a bandana or t-shirt and wrap it around your neck. Evaporative cooling drops skin temperature immediately.
- Wet your hair if you have it. Your head radiates the most heat.
- If you find a hose, spray yourself. Soak your clothes. They will dry in 20 minutes in desert heat, and those 20 minutes of evaporative cooling can prevent heat exhaustion.
- Know the signs of heat stroke: skin hot and dry (not sweating), confusion, slurred speech, rapid heartbeat. Call 911. This kills.

## Pacific Northwest: Wet Cold Survival

Hypothermia starts at 50°F with rain and wind. Not freezing. Fifty degrees. Most people do not understand this. They think hypothermia requires snow. In the PNW, it requires Tuesday.

### The Wet Cold Protocol

- **Waterproof everything.** Your sleeping bag in a garbage bag. Your spare clothes in a garbage bag. Your documents in a Ziploc. If it gets wet, it is useless.
- **Change socks twice daily.** Wet feet develop trench foot in 48 hours. Trench foot is not a historical curiosity. It is a real, disabling condition that can require amputation. Carry spare socks. Dry your feet whenever you are inside.
- **Never wear cotton.** Cotton absorbs water, holds it, and leaches your body heat. Wool and synthetic fleece retain warmth when wet. A wet cotton hoodie is more dangerous than no hoodie at all.
- **The garbage bag rain layer.** Cut a hole for your head and two for your arms. It is not comfortable. It keeps you dry. A dry person at 45°F is fine. A wet person at 45°F is hypothermic in two hours.

## Northeast & Midwest: Winter Survival Specifics

At 0°F, frostbite starts on exposed skin in 30 minutes. At -10°F with wind, it starts in 10 minutes. Frostbite destroys tissue. You lose fingers, toes, ears. This is not an abstraction. Emergency rooms in Chicago, Minneapolis, and Detroit see hundreds of frostbite cases among homeless people every winter.

### The Layering System

- **Base layer:** Synthetic or wool, tight-fitting. Wicks moisture from your skin. Never cotton.
- **Mid layer:** Fleece or wool sweater. Insulation. Traps warm air against your body.
- **Outer layer:** Windproof and water-resistant. Blocks wind chill, which is the real killer.
- **Head and extremities:** You lose significant heat through your head, hands, and feet. A warm hat, insulated gloves, and two pairs of socks are not optional. They are survival gear.

### Warming Centers and the Overnight Circuit

Libraries close at 8 or 9pm. McDonald's at 11pm. Warming centers open during cold weather emergencies, typically when temperatures drop below a threshold (often 20°F or below). Subway stations are open 24 hours in cities that have them. The overnight survival circuit is moving between heated spaces to stay alive until morning. Know every heated space within walking distance of your sleeping spot. Map them before winter. When temperatures drop below zero, staying outside all night is not an option. It is a death sentence.

### Cheap Warmth

- **Hand and toe warmers:** $1 per pair at Dollar Tree, Walmart, or gas stations. Eight hours of heat. Put them in your gloves and inside your socks. On the coldest nights, put two in your sleeping bag.
- **Newspaper insulation:** Crumple newspaper and stuff it inside your jacket, under your hat, inside your shoes. Dead air trapped in crumpled paper insulates. It is not comfortable. It works.
- **Cardboard under you:** The ground steals heat faster than the air. Sleeping on cardboard instead of directly on concrete dramatically reduces heat loss.

## The South: Humidity and Hurricanes

Humid heat is more dangerous than dry desert heat at the same temperature. At 95°F with 80% humidity, the "feels like" temperature is 115°F. Your sweat does not evaporate. Your body's cooling system fails. Heat exhaustion and heat stroke hit faster and with less warning than in dry climates.

### Humidity-Specific Threats

- **Mosquito-borne illness.** Standing water plus humidity equals mosquitoes. Malaria is not a US risk, but West Nile virus, Zika, and Eastern Equine Encephalitis are. Use DEET-based bug spray. Sleep under a mosquito net if camping. Drain standing water near your sleeping spot.
- **Skin infections.** Heat and humidity cause skin to stay damp, which breeds fungal infections. Jock itch, athlete's foot, and cellulitis are common. Keep skin dry. Change clothes when possible. Powder works.

### Hurricane Evacuation for Homeless People

If you are unsheltered during hurricane season (June through November), you need a plan before the storm arrives. Most cities open shelters of last resort 24 to 48 hours before a major hurricane. These are typically schools, community centers, or arenas. They provide basic shelter but not food or comfort. Know where they are. When an evacuation order is issued, go. Do not wait for the wind to start. By then, transit shuts down and you are trapped.

> **&#9888; Warning**
> Storm surge kills more people in hurricanes than wind. If you are near the coast and a hurricane is approaching, move inland. Even a few miles makes the difference. Do not shelter under an overpass --- they channel wind and become death traps in severe storms.

## Natural Disasters When Homeless

Disasters do not care whether you have a roof. But they hit harder when you do not. Housed people shelter in place. You have to move. Every minute of delay increases your risk. Know the threats in your area before they arrive. Have a plan before the weather forecast tells you to panic.

### Hurricane

Shelters of last resort open for everyone during hurricanes. No ID required. No questions asked. They are usually schools, community centers, or arenas. Find out where they are before storm season. When a hurricane warning is issued, go immediately. Do not wait for the rain to start. Bring: your documents, your medications, your phone and charger, water, and food for 3 days. Know your evacuation zone BEFORE storm season --- text your ZIP code to 43362 (4FEMA) or check ready.gov.

### Flooding

NEVER camp in drainage channels, underpasses, dry creek beds, or flood plains. Flash floods kill with zero warning. A dry concrete wash can fill with six feet of water in minutes. If it starts raining hard upstream --- even if it is dry where you are --- move to high ground immediately. Six inches of moving water knocks you down. Two feet of moving water carries away a car. Do not walk through floodwater. Do not sleep near any waterway during rain season.

### Tornado

Get to a sturdy building immediately. Lowest floor, interior room, away from windows. A closet or bathroom in a concrete building is ideal. If you are caught outside with no building nearby, lie flat in a ditch or low area and cover your head with your arms. Do not shelter under an overpass --- wind accelerates through them. Cars and tents provide ZERO protection. A tornado will throw a car. Your tent is gone before you hear the sound.

### Extreme Cold (Polar Vortex)

Call 211 for warming centers. Emergency shelters open extra capacity during extreme cold events --- cities that normally have no room will make room when temperatures drop below zero. Frostbite starts at 0°F in 30 minutes on exposed skin. Wind chill makes it faster. At -20°F with wind, frostbite can start in 10 minutes. If you cannot get indoors: insulate from the ground first (cardboard, sleeping pad, anything between you and concrete), cover ALL exposed skin, keep moving. Do not drink alcohol --- it dilates blood vessels and accelerates heat loss even though it makes you feel warmer.

### Wildfire

Evacuate immediately. Do not shelter in place outdoors. Smoke inhalation kills before fire reaches you. If you can smell smoke, you are already breathing particulates that damage your lungs. Follow evacuation routes --- they are marked and broadcast on emergency channels. If you are trapped, find a paved road or large open area away from vegetation. Lie face down and cover your mouth with a wet cloth. Get to an evacuation shelter as fast as possible.

### FEMA Assistance

You qualify for disaster assistance even without a permanent address. Apply at disasterassistance.gov or call 1-800-621-3362. FEMA provides temporary housing, food assistance, and replacement of essential items. You can use a shelter address or a friend's address for your application. A caseworker at 211 can help you apply. Do not assume you do not qualify because you are homeless. You do.

**Key Takeaways**

- **Climate is the most important survival variable.** The strategies that work in one zone can be lethal in another.
- **Desert Southwest:** Heat kills. Stay hydrated, map shade and cooling centers, invert your schedule in summer.
- **Pacific Northwest:** Wet cold kills. Waterproofing is priority one. Avoid cotton.
- **Upper Midwest & Northeast:** Winter is existential. Know your warming centers. NYC has right-to-shelter.
- **The South:** Humidity makes heat more dangerous. Hurricane season requires a plan. Houston has strong Housing First programs.
- **Rural America:** Isolation is the enemy. Your vehicle is your lifeline. Community help exists but is inconsistent.
