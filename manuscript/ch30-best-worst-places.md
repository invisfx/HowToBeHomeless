# Chapter 30: The Best and Worst Places {#ch30}

*Where to go if you have a choice --- and where to avoid.*

Most homeless people do not choose where they are. You are homeless where you became homeless --- where the eviction happened, where the relationship ended, where the job disappeared. But if you have mobility --- a bus ticket, a working car, or nothing tying you to your current city --- geography is a survival variable you can control. And it is one of the most consequential decisions you will make.

Not all cities are equal. Some have robust shelter systems, affordable housing pipelines, and cultures that treat homeless people as neighbors in crisis. Others have hostile police enforcement, camping bans, and architecture designed to make your existence as uncomfortable as possible. The difference between the right city and the wrong one can be the difference between stabilizing in six months and spiraling for years.

This chapter ranks cities based on seven factors that matter most to someone experiencing homelessness. The data is from 2024--2026 point-in-time counts, HUD reports, municipal budgets, and published enforcement records. These conditions change --- always verify before relocating.

## The Seven Ranking Factors

- **Climate:** Can you survive outdoors year-round? Extreme heat kills faster than cold. Wet cold causes hypothermia without freezing temperatures.
- **Services:** Shelter beds per capita, food programs, free clinics, day centers. More beds means less competition.
- **Legal environment:** Camping bans, panhandling laws, police enforcement culture. Post-Grants Pass, this is changing fast.
- **Cost of living:** Can you realistically rebuild here? A city with great services but $2,500/month rent traps you in the system.
- **Public transit:** Can you get to services, jobs, and appointments without a car? Transit is infrastructure for survival.
- **Employment access:** Day labor availability, gig work platforms, staffing agencies. Immediate income options matter.
- **Housing First programs:** Is there an exit ramp? Cities with active Housing First initiatives have 70--90% retention rates vs. 30--50% for traditional programs.

## Tier 1: Best Cities

### Houston, TX --- The Model City

Houston reduced homelessness by 63% since 2012 using a Housing First approach. Over 30,000 people have been housed through The Way Home coalition, with 90% retaining housing for two or more years. The cost of living is lower than any coastal city, gig work is abundant, and police enforcement is moderate compared to West Coast cities.

- **Climate:** Hot summers (dangerous June--September), mild winters
- **Services:** Strong coordinated entry system, permanent supportive housing
- **Cost of living:** Below national average --- realistic path to renting
- **Transit:** Limited --- car-dependent city, bus system covers basics
- **Risk:** Funding uncertain. Without $50M+ annually, homelessness could spike 60% by end of 2026

### New York City --- Right to Shelter (For Now)

NYC is the only major city with a legal right to shelter. The system houses 85,000+ people nightly --- the largest shelter infrastructure in the country. The city spends $456 million on street homeless efforts, tripled since 2019. If you need a bed tonight, NYC legally must provide one.

- **Climate:** Cold winters, hot summers --- but shelter access offsets this
- **Services:** Unmatched. Hundreds of shelters, drop-in centers, outreach teams
- **Cost of living:** Extremely high --- hard to exit homelessness into housing
- **Transit:** Best in the country. 24/7 subway. Critical for survival.
- **Risk:** Right-to-shelter protections are actively being eroded. 30-day limits for some populations.

### Denver, CO --- Fair Treatment, Cold Winters

Denver stands out for how its systems interact with homeless people. Transit agencies partner with mental health crisis interventionists rather than police. Shelter networks are at capacity but functional. Enforcement is moderate. The culture leans toward treatment over criminalization.

- **Climate:** Cold winters are a serious barrier to outdoor survival
- **Services:** Good shelter network, crisis intervention partnerships
- **Cost of living:** Rising --- harder to rebuild than Houston
- **Transit:** Strong bus and light rail system

## Tier 2: Viable but Complicated

### Austin, TX --- Growing but Accessible

Austin has a strong gig economy, moderate cost of living compared to coastal cities, and expanding homeless services. The tech economy creates day labor and service work. The risk: rapid population growth is straining every system, and political attitudes toward encampments are hardening.

### Portland, OR --- Services Exist, Enforcement Shifting

Portland has historically been one of the most tolerant cities for homeless people, with a strong shelter system and outreach infrastructure. The climate is livable but wet --- gear investment in waterproofing is essential. The political climate has shifted significantly since 2023 toward enforcement and camp sweeps.

### San Francisco, CA --- Highest Spending, Highest Competition

San Francisco spends $141,852 per homeless person --- the highest documented per-capita spending in the country. The service infrastructure is massive. But so is the competition for it. The city is extremely expensive, crowded, and the gap between services and cost of living means getting housed here is nearly impossible without subsidized housing.

> **&#9654; Psychology: Your Brain Right Now: The Relocation Trap**
> Moving to a "better" city feels like a solution, but relocation has hidden costs. You lose whatever local knowledge, relationships, and routines you have built. You arrive in a new city as a stranger with no network. Research on homeless mobility shows that people who relocate without a specific plan (a shelter bed reserved, a job lined up, a contact waiting) often end up worse than they started. Relocate strategically, not impulsively.

## Places to Avoid

> **&#9888; Danger Zones**
> **Phoenix, AZ:** Heat kills. Over 300 heat-related homeless deaths per year. Temperatures exceed 110°F for weeks. Hostile enforcement. If you are in Phoenix in summer without air conditioning access, you are in a medical emergency.
>
> **Atlanta, GA:** Statewide camping ban. Hostile architecture --- spikes on ledges, boulders under overpasses. Police-first approach to homelessness.
>
> **Miami / Miami Beach, FL:** 114 anti-camping arrests in two months (June--July 2024). Aggressive enforcement in tourist areas. Panhandling heavily restricted.
>
> **San Diego, CA:** Despite a $105 million budget, police doubled enforcement teams dedicated to sweeping homeless people. National Coalition for the Homeless has documented systematic harassment.

## The Post-Grants Pass Reality

In June 2024, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled in *City of Grants Pass v. Johnson* that cities can criminalize sleeping outdoors even when no shelter is available. This reversed years of Ninth Circuit protections. Within six months:

- 100+ cities adopted new camping bans
- Arrests increased 68% in Los Angeles
- Arrests doubled in San Diego
- Arrests nearly tripled in Sacramento
- Texas, Missouri, and Tennessee passed statewide camping bans
- Panhandling bans expanded 103% nationally since 2006
- Vehicle-dwelling bans expanded 213% in the same period

**What this means for you:** Check local laws before traveling to any city. What was legal a year ago may now get you arrested. Call 211 in the destination city before you go.

> **&#10004; Before You Relocate**
> Call 211 in the destination city. Ask: Are shelters at capacity? Is there a waitlist? Are there camping bans? What services are available for someone arriving with nothing? Get answers before you spend your last money on a bus ticket.

> **&#9823; Game Theory: Right-to-Shelter --- A Closing Window**
> Only New York City and Massachusetts have a legal right to shelter. Both are actively eroding these protections. NYC has imposed 30-day limits for some populations. Massachusetts has capped family shelter stays. These rights exist on paper but are being narrowed in practice. Do not build a survival strategy around a right that may not exist by the time you arrive. Verify current policy before relocating.

## Mid-Size Cities Worth Considering

The big cities get all the attention. But mid-size cities often offer better odds: shorter waitlists, lower cost of living, growing job markets, and functional service systems without the crushing competition. If you have mobility, these cities are worth researching.

### Salt Lake City, UT --- The Housing First Pioneer

Salt Lake City was one of the first cities in America to implement Housing First at scale, reducing chronic homelessness by 91% between 2005 and 2015. The model has since been widely adopted, but Salt Lake City retains strong infrastructure. Cost of living is rising but still below coastal cities. The shelter system is functional. Winters are cold but manageable with gear. Strong LDS community charities supplement government services. The job market is growing, especially in tech and logistics.

### Charlotte, NC --- Housing First Saves Money

Charlotte invested in Housing First and documented $2.4 million in annual savings from reduced emergency room visits, hospitalizations, and jail stays. The cost of living is moderate. The job market is strong in banking, logistics, and construction. The shelter system is strained but functional. Winters are mild. Public transit is limited but expanding. The city is actively building affordable housing, which means more units in the pipeline than many comparable cities.

### Minneapolis, MN --- Strong Systems, Hard Winters

Minneapolis has one of the strongest shelter systems of any mid-size city. The Hennepin County system coordinates intake, services, and housing pathways effectively. Shelter capacity is usually available. The job market is robust. The catch is winter. November through March, temperatures regularly drop below zero. If you do not have reliable shelter access, Minneapolis winters will kill you. But if you can get into the system, the system works better here than in most places.

### Columbus, OH --- Affordable and Growing

Columbus has one of the lowest costs of living of any growing metro area. One-bedroom apartments average $800 to $1,000 per month. The job market is diversified across healthcare, education, logistics, and tech. The Community Shelter Board coordinates a functional homeless services system. Waitlists are shorter than coastal cities. Winters are cold but not extreme. If your goal is getting housed quickly in a city where you can actually afford rent, Columbus should be on your list.

## The Greyhound Strategy

Relocation is a real option. Not a fantasy of starting over. A calculated decision to move to a city where the math works better. Here is how to do it without making things worse.

### The Cost

Greyhound bus tickets cost $30 to $100 one-way depending on distance. FlixBus is often cheaper, $15 to $60 for the same routes. Book online at least a few days ahead for the lowest prices. You can carry two bags plus a personal item. That is enough to bring your essentials.

### Before You Buy the Ticket

- **Call 211 in the destination city.** Ask: Are shelter beds available? Is there a waitlist? What services exist for someone arriving with nothing? Get specific answers, not promises.
- **Reserve a shelter bed if possible.** Some shelters take reservations. Some do not. Knowing before you arrive is the difference between a plan and a gamble.
- **Research the job market.** Check Indeed, Craigslist, and staffing agency locations in the destination city. Are there jobs you can start within a week? Day labor agencies? Gig work availability?
- **Check the weather forecast.** If you are arriving without shelter access, arriving during a heat wave or cold snap can be a medical emergency.
- **Know someone if possible.** Even one contact --- a friend, a relative, a caseworker who offered to help --- dramatically improves your odds. Arriving in a strange city knowing nobody is survivable but harder.

### Relocation Assistance Programs

Some nonprofits and government programs provide relocation assistance. This can include bus tickets, initial shelter, and case management in the destination city. Ask your current caseworker, call 211, or contact the Salvation Army about relocation programs. The SSVF program (veterans only) includes relocation assistance as part of its services.

> **&#9888; Warning**
> Do not relocate impulsively. The research on homeless mobility is clear: people who relocate without a specific plan --- a shelter bed, a job lead, a contact --- often end up worse than where they started. You lose whatever local knowledge, relationships, and routines you have built. You arrive as a stranger in a system you do not know. Relocate strategically. A bad plan in a good city still beats no plan.

**Key Takeaways**

- **Houston** is the best-documented success story --- Housing First works, cost of living is manageable.
- **NYC** has the strongest shelter guarantee but the highest cost of living.
- **Avoid Phoenix in summer.** Heat is lethal.
- **Grants Pass changed everything.** 100+ cities now criminalize camping. Check laws before traveling.
- **Your best city depends on your situation.** Vehicle? Climate matters most. On foot? Services and transit. Rebuilding? Cost of living. Running from danger? Go anywhere safe first, optimize later.
