# Chapter 19: Physical Health {#ch19}

*Clinics, chronic conditions, dental emergencies, and keeping your feet under you.*

Homelessness is a full-body assault. The stress grinds your immune system down. The exposure wears on joints, skin, and lungs. The food — when you can get it — is high in sodium and low in everything your body actually needs. And the healthcare system, the one thing designed to help, is built around the assumption that you have an address, insurance, and the ability to follow up in two weeks. You have none of those things, so you need to learn how to work the system differently.

This chapter is not a medical manual. It is a field guide to getting care when the system was not designed for you. The good news: there are more free and low-cost medical resources than most people realize. The bad news: finding them, accessing them, and navigating the bureaucracy takes knowledge and persistence. Both of which you now have.

The single most important concept in this chapter is triage. Not every health issue can be addressed right now. Some things are urgent. Some things are important but can wait. Some things are chronic annoyances that you manage rather than solve. Learning to tell the difference — and acting accordingly — is the skill that keeps you functional.

Your body is your vehicle, your shelter system, and your tool kit. It is also the one thing you cannot replace. Everything in this chapter comes down to a single principle: maintain the machine, because when it breaks down on the street, the consequences cascade fast.

## Getting Medical Care with No Insurance

The American healthcare system has a shadow network built for people without insurance. It is not advertised, it is not intuitive, and it is not always easy to access — but it exists, and knowing how to use it can save your life.

### Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs)

FQHCs are the backbone of free healthcare for the uninsured. There are over 1,400 of them nationwide, and they are legally required to see you regardless of ability to pay. They use a sliding-scale fee structure based on income — if your income is zero, your cost is zero or close to it. Many offer dental, mental health, and pharmacy services in addition to primary care.

Find your nearest FQHC at findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov or call 211. Bring whatever ID you have, but lack of ID should not prevent you from being seen. Be direct: tell them you are uninsured and experiencing homelessness. They have heard it before.

### Free Clinics and Charitable Care

Free clinics operate outside the federal system, usually run by nonprofits or religious organizations. Quality varies widely. Some are staffed by volunteer physicians and offer excellent care. Others are well-meaning but limited. The National Association of Free and Charitable Clinics (nafcclinics.org) maintains a directory.

### The Emergency Room as Last Resort

Under EMTALA, every emergency room must stabilize you regardless of insurance or ability to pay. This is federal law. However, the ER is for emergencies — not for refilling prescriptions, managing chronic conditions, or treating things that have been building for weeks. ER visits generate bills, and while you may qualify for charity care or financial assistance after the fact, the paperwork is another burden you do not need.

Use the ER when something is genuinely life-threatening: chest pain, difficulty breathing, uncontrolled bleeding, high fever with altered mental status, or severe abdominal pain. For everything else, find a clinic.

## Managing Chronic Conditions

Diabetes, HIV, asthma, hypertension, epilepsy — chronic conditions do not pause because you lost your housing. They accelerate. The stress, the irregular meals, the exposure, the interrupted medication schedules — everything about homelessness makes chronic conditions worse.

The first priority is medication continuity. If you are on daily medications, protecting that supply is as critical as protecting your phone. Many pharmaceutical companies offer patient assistance programs that provide medications free to qualifying individuals. NeedyMeds.org and RxAssist.org are two directories worth bookmarking. Many FQHCs have in-house pharmacies with deeply discounted or free medications.

For insulin-dependent diabetics: some Walmart pharmacies sell ReliOn insulin over the counter for roughly $25 per vial without a prescription. This is not ideal — it is older insulin and dosing differs from modern analogs — but it is a lifeline in an emergency. Always consult a clinician when switching insulin types.

## Dental Emergencies

Dental problems are the great accelerator of homelessness. A toothache becomes an abscess. An abscess becomes sepsis. And long before it reaches that point, visible dental damage makes it harder to get hired, harder to be taken seriously, and harder to maintain self-respect.

Dental schools offer care at steep discounts — the trade-off is time, as students work more slowly under supervision. Community health centers with dental programs exist but often have long waitlists. For acute dental pain, clove oil (eugenol) is a temporary analgesic available at most pharmacies for a few dollars.

## Foot Care

Your feet are your transportation system. Neglecting them is the equivalent of a housed person neglecting their car. Change socks daily — wet socks cause blisters, and blisters cause infections. Wool or synthetic socks only; cotton holds moisture and accelerates skin breakdown. Moleskin on hot spots before they become blisters. Elevate your feet when resting. Inspect them nightly for cuts, fungus, or signs of trench foot. Treat problems early, because on the street, a foot injury can immobilize you completely.

> **✔ Practical Tip**
> Carry a small container of petroleum jelly. Applied to clean, dry feet before putting on socks, it reduces friction and prevents blisters. It also works on chapped lips, minor cuts, and dry skin — one product, multiple uses.

## ER vs. Urgent Care vs. Wait

Knowing which door to walk through saves you hours, money, and potentially your life. The wrong choice in either direction — going to the ER for something minor, or waiting on something that needs immediate intervention — creates problems that compound.

### Go to the ER Immediately

- **Chest pain or difficulty breathing.** Do not try to tough this out. Call 911 if you cannot walk there.
- **Severe or uncontrolled bleeding.** Apply pressure and get there.
- **Head injury with confusion, vomiting, or loss of consciousness.** Even if you feel fine afterward — brain bleeds can present hours later.
- **Allergic reaction with swelling of the face, tongue, or throat.** This is anaphylaxis. Minutes matter.
- **Suicidal thoughts with a plan.** The ER has psychiatric resources. You can also call 988.
- **Seizure (if you do not have a seizure disorder).** First-time seizures need workup.
- **Signs of stroke:** face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty. Call 911. Every minute of delay destroys brain tissue.

### Urgent Care Works For

Urgent care clinics cost $50–$150 without insurance — expensive, but far less than an ER bill. They handle: cuts needing stitches (not spurting blood), sprains and minor fractures, urinary tract infections, ear and sinus infections, rashes, minor burns, flu symptoms that are not life-threatening. Many offer payment plans. Some have financial assistance. Always ask before you leave.

### Wait and Use a Free Clinic

If it is not getting worse and it is not preventing you from functioning, a free clinic appointment in a few days is the right call. Chronic pain, skin conditions, medication refills, routine check-ups, STI testing — these are clinic problems, not emergency problems. The patience to wait saves you the burden of an ER bill you do not need.

> **✔ Practical Tip**
> Planned Parenthood is not just reproductive health. Many locations offer primary care, STI testing, UTI treatment, and wellness exams on a sliding scale. Men can be seen at many locations. Call your nearest clinic to ask what services they offer.

## Managing Medications Without Insurance

Losing insurance does not have to mean losing your medications. There is an entire ecosystem of discount programs, assistance programs, and workarounds that most people never learn about until they need them. Learn them now.

### GoodRx

Download the free GoodRx app or go to goodrx.com. Search your medication. It shows prices at every pharmacy near you with coupons that save 30–80% off retail. No insurance needed. No sign-up required. Just show the coupon on your phone at the pharmacy counter. This is not a scam — it is a legitimate discount aggregator used by millions of people, including many with insurance whose copays are higher than the GoodRx price.

### $4 Generic Lists

Walmart, Costco, and several other chains offer 30-day supplies of common generic medications for $4 and 90-day supplies for $10. The list covers hundreds of drugs including antibiotics, blood pressure medications, diabetes medications, antidepressants, and pain relievers. You do not need a Costco membership to use the Costco pharmacy. Walk in, go to the pharmacy counter, and pay the listed price.

### Patient Assistance Programs (PAPs)

Nearly every major pharmaceutical company operates a patient assistance program that provides brand-name medications free to people who cannot afford them. The application process is bureaucratic but worth the effort for expensive medications. Start at needymeds.org or rxassist.org — both are free directories that match your medications to available programs. Many require a doctor's signature, which your FQHC clinician can provide.

### 340B Drug Pricing

FQHCs participate in the 340B Drug Pricing Program, which requires drug manufacturers to sell medications to these clinics at deeply discounted prices. If you are being seen at an FQHC, ask if they have an in-house pharmacy or a 340B partner pharmacy. Your medications may cost a fraction of what you would pay at a retail pharmacy. Some FQHC pharmacies provide medications free to patients with zero income.

### Insulin Specifically

If you are insulin-dependent and between insurance: Walmart sells ReliOn insulin (NPH and Regular) over the counter for about $25 per vial. This is older-generation insulin and the dosing protocol differs from modern analogs like Humalog or Novolog. Do not switch without consulting a clinician. But in an emergency, $25 insulin keeps you alive. Lilly, Novo Nordisk, and Sanofi all have patient assistance programs for their modern insulins — apply immediately and use the Walmart option as a bridge.

## The Foot Care Protocol

Your feet carry you to every resource in this book. A foot problem that a housed person would treat with rest and elevation becomes a cascading crisis on the street. You cannot rest your feet when your feet are your transportation. This section is your field manual for keeping them functional.

### Daily Routine

1. **Change socks every day.** Wool or synthetic blends only. Never cotton. Cotton holds moisture against your skin and accelerates blisters, fungal infections, and trench foot. If you only own cotton socks, get to a shelter or donation center and ask for wool or synthetic socks. This is that important.
2. **Air dry your feet whenever you can.** Take your shoes off when you are sitting somewhere safe. Spread your toes. Let air circulate. Moisture is the enemy.
3. **Inspect your feet every night.** Look between your toes. Check the soles. Look for redness, blisters, cracks, discoloration, or swelling. Catch problems early when they are small.
4. **Apply petroleum jelly to clean, dry feet** before putting on socks. This reduces friction and prevents blisters. It also creates a moisture barrier.
5. **Rotate shoes if possible.** Even two pairs, alternating daily, lets each pair dry completely. Wet shoes breed fungus.

### Blister Protocol

At the first sign of a hot spot (a warm, red area before the blister forms), apply moleskin or duct tape over it. If a blister has already formed: clean the area with soap and water or an alcohol wipe. Drain it by puncturing the edge with a sterilized needle (hold a flame to it for five seconds). Press the fluid out gently. Do not remove the skin — it protects the raw tissue underneath. Apply antibiotic ointment and cover with a bandage. Change the bandage daily.

### Trench Foot

Trench foot happens when your feet stay wet and cold for extended periods. Symptoms: numbness, tingling, pain, swelling, blotchy skin. If caught early, it is reversible: dry your feet completely, warm them gradually (not with direct heat), elevate them, and keep them dry going forward. If the skin turns dark or blisters appear, seek medical care — advanced trench foot can lead to tissue death and amputation.

### Fungal Infections

Athlete's foot is nearly universal on the street. Over-the-counter antifungal cream (clotrimazole or miconazole) costs $3–$5 at Dollar Tree or Walmart. Apply twice daily to clean, dry feet for two weeks minimum, even after symptoms improve. Antifungal foot powder in your socks and shoes helps prevent recurrence. If the infection spreads to your toenails (thickening, discoloration), you need prescription medication from a clinic.

## Common Problems and What to Do

Street life produces a predictable set of health problems. Knowing what each one looks like and how urgently it needs attention keeps minor issues from becoming emergencies.

### Infections

Any cut, scrape, or wound that becomes red, swollen, warm to the touch, or starts producing pus is infected. Clean it, apply antibiotic ointment, cover it, and get to a clinic within a day or two. But if you see **red streaks** radiating outward from the wound, that means the infection has entered your lymphatic system. This is an emergency. Go to the ER immediately. Untreated, this becomes sepsis, which kills.

### Dental Abscess

A dental abscess is a pocket of infection at the root of a tooth. It feels like the worst toothache of your life, often with swelling in your jaw or cheek. If the swelling is near your eye or throat, go to the ER — the infection can spread to your brain or obstruct your airway. For non-emergency dental pain: clove oil (eugenol, $3–$5 at any pharmacy) applied directly to the tooth provides temporary relief. Ibuprofen reduces inflammation. Get to a dental clinic as soon as possible — antibiotics are usually needed.

### Skin Conditions

**Scabies:** Intense itching, especially at night. Tiny burrows visible between fingers, on wrists, and around the waistline. Highly contagious through skin contact and shared bedding. Treatable with permethrin cream (prescription, but available free at clinics). Wash all clothing and bedding in hot water.

**Lice:** Head lice cause intense scalp itching. Body lice live in clothing seams, not on your body. Both are treatable — head lice with over-the-counter permethrin shampoo ($8–$12), body lice by washing and hot-drying all clothing. Shelters often have lice treatment protocols — this is not shameful, it is one of the most common shelter health issues.

### Dehydration

Dehydration sneaks up on you, especially in summer or when you are walking long distances. Warning signs: dark yellow urine, dry mouth, headache, dizziness, fatigue, confusion. By the time you feel thirsty, you are already mildly dehydrated. If you cannot keep water down (vomiting), or if confusion sets in, go to an ER — IV fluids may be needed. Prevention: drink water consistently throughout the day, not just when thirsty. Carry your water bottle and fill it at every opportunity.

### Remote Area Medical Events

Remote Area Medical (RAM) hosts free pop-up clinics in cities across the country, providing dental, vision, and medical care at no cost with no insurance required. These events happen on weekends and serve hundreds of people. Arrive early — lines form before dawn. Check ramusa.org for upcoming events in your area. A single RAM event can get you dental work, prescription glasses, and a medical check-up that would cost thousands in the regular system.

## Common Injuries and Infections

Street life produces a predictable set of injuries. Cuts and scrapes from rough sleeping surfaces. Burns from improvised cooking. Skin infections from inadequate hygiene access. Upper respiratory infections from shelters with poor ventilation. Knowing what to watch for and when something crosses from manageable to urgent can keep a minor problem from becoming a hospitalization.

### When It Is Urgent

- **Red streaks radiating from a wound:** This indicates the infection is spreading into the lymphatic system. Seek care immediately.
- **Fever above 103°F that does not respond to ibuprofen:** This may indicate systemic infection.
- **Wound with visible pus, increasing redness, or foul smell:** Infection is progressing and may require antibiotics.
- **Cough lasting more than two weeks:** Tuberculosis screening is warranted, especially in shelter environments.
- **Any animal bite:** Rabies is 100% fatal once symptomatic. Do not wait.

### Basic Wound Care

Clean the wound with clean water — not alcohol, not hydrogen peroxide, which damage tissue. Apply antibiotic ointment if available. Cover with a clean bandage and change it daily. If you do not have first aid supplies, most fire stations will provide basic wound care at no charge.

**Key Takeaways**

- **FQHCs are your primary care.** Free, sliding-scale, legally required to see you. Find one.
- **Medication continuity is critical.** Patient assistance programs and FQHC pharmacies can bridge the gap.
- **Dental problems accelerate fast.** Dental schools and community health centers are your best options.
- **Your feet are your vehicle.** Wool socks, daily inspection, treat problems early.
- **Know when to go to the ER.** Red streaks, high fever, animal bites, chest pain — do not wait.
