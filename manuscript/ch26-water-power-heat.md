# Chapter 26: Water, Power, and Heat {#ch26}

*The three resources housed people take for granted that you now need to produce yourself.*

In a house, you turn a tap and water comes out. You plug into a wall and electricity flows. You adjust a thermostat and the temperature changes. On the street, you produce all three of these resources yourself, or you go without. This chapter is the operations manual for water acquisition and purification, portable power generation, and heat management — the three pillars of physical sustainability outside the built environment.

Each of these resources follows the same strategic pattern: know where to find it, know how to store it, know how to produce it yourself when the easy sources dry up. The person who can reliably access clean water, keep a phone charged, and stay warm through a cold night has solved the three problems that most commonly drive people off the street and into shelters they would rather avoid or emergency rooms they cannot afford.

Some of what follows is simple and immediately applicable. Some of it is more advanced and only relevant if you are in a long-term outdoor situation or living in a vehicle. Read what applies to your circumstances and bookmark the rest — your situation may change.

## Water

Clean water is the most fundamental survival resource. You can go weeks without food. You can go three days without water — less in heat. And contaminated water can make you sicker than no water at all. Understanding how to find, store, and purify water is essential knowledge.

### Water Filtration

Three approaches, each with trade-offs:

- **Sawyer Squeeze ($35–$45):** Filters bacteria and protozoa. Attaches to standard water bottles. Filters up to 100,000 gallons before replacement. The best balance of cost, weight, and effectiveness for most situations.
- **LifeStraw ($15):** Personal filter you drink through directly. Filters up to 1,000 gallons. Lighter and cheaper than the Sawyer, but less versatile — you cannot filter water into a container for later use. Best as a backup or ultralight option.
- **Chemical treatment:** Iodine tincture ($8–$12, 2% solution, 5 drops per liter, wait 30 minutes) or household bleach (2 drops per liter, wait 30 minutes). Lighter than any filter, effective against bacteria and viruses, but leaves a taste and does not remove sediment. Iodine tincture doubles as a wound disinfectant and fire starter. Bleach must be unscented, regular strength (8.25% sodium hypochlorite).

### Finding Water

Exterior spigots on commercial buildings, churches, and parks are the most reliable free water sources in urban environments. Time of day matters: early morning and late evening minimize the chance of confrontation. Gas stations and fast food restaurants have bathroom sinks. Libraries have water fountains. Many cities have public drinking fountains in parks, though many are shut off in winter.

For bulk water storage, collapsible water jugs (available at camping stores for $5–10) hold 2–5 gallons and fold flat when empty. In a pinch, any clean container works: repurposed juice jugs, soda bottles, even heavy-duty ziplock bags.

### Purification by Boiling

Boiling is the most reliable purification method when you have the means. Bring water to a rolling boil for a minimum of one minute. At elevations above 6,500 feet, boil for three minutes. Boiling kills bacteria, viruses, and protozoa. It does not remove chemical contaminants or sediment — if the water is cloudy, filter it through a cloth first.

### Signs of Contaminated Water

Do not drink water that has unusual color, odor, or surface film. Stagnant water near industrial sites, agricultural runoff areas, or downstream from mining operations may contain chemicals that filtration and boiling cannot remove. When in doubt, find another source.

## Power

### Solar Panels

Portable solar panels in the 10–20W range are a game-changer for anyone spending significant time outdoors. A 20W panel can charge a phone in 2–3 hours of direct sunlight and can keep a battery pack topped off indefinitely. Position the panel perpendicular to the sun and adjust it throughout the day for maximum output. Overcast days produce 10–25% of full-sun output — still useful, but plan accordingly.

### Generators

Generators are only practical for vehicle dwellers or established camps. They produce significant noise, which attracts attention you probably do not want. Propane generators are safer and less smelly than gasoline models. Never run a generator inside an enclosed space — carbon monoxide kills without warning. Consider the legal and social implications: many jurisdictions prohibit generators in public spaces, and the noise can get you reported and relocated.

### Battery Banks and Vehicle Systems

Size your battery bank to your needs. A 10,000 mAh pack handles a phone. A 20,000 mAh pack handles a phone and a headlamp for a week. Charge your highest-priority device first. For vehicle dwellers: running accessories off your starter battery risks stranding you. A battery isolator ($30–$80) separates your house battery from your starter battery, letting you run lights, fans, and chargers without risking your ability to start the engine.

## Heat

### The Layering System

Cold management is not about one warm garment — it is about a system. Three layers, each with a specific job:

- **Base layer (wicking):** Synthetic or wool, worn against skin. Pulls moisture away from your body. Cotton is the enemy — it absorbs moisture and holds it against your skin, accelerating heat loss.
- **Mid layer (insulation):** Fleece, down, or synthetic fill. Traps body heat in dead air space. This is where most of your warmth comes from.
- **Outer layer (shell):** Wind and waterproof. Blocks wind chill and keeps rain off your insulating layers. A wet mid layer loses up to 90% of its insulating value.

### Parabolic Reflectors

Parabolic reflectors focus sunlight to a point, generating enough concentrated heat to boil water, cook food, and sterilize tools. Commercial solar cookers are available, but you can build one from a discarded satellite dish lined with reflective tape or aluminum foil, or from a car windshield sunshade shaped into a cone. In direct sun, a well-made reflector can reach temperatures above 350°F at the focal point.

### Fresnel Lens

A page-size Fresnel lens costs about $3 and fits flat in your bag. In direct sunlight, it can start a fire in seconds and boil a small container of water in minutes. It is one of the highest-utility, lowest-weight, lowest-cost items you can carry. Available at office supply stores, educational supply catalogs, or online.

## Water Purification Methods — Ranked

Not all purification is equal. Here is each method ranked by reliability, cost, and practicality for street use.

1. **Boiling (free, most reliable).** One minute at a rolling boil kills all biological contaminants — bacteria, viruses, and protozoa. Three minutes above 6,500 feet elevation. Requires a heat source (stove, fire, Fresnel lens) and a metal container. Best method when you have the means. Limitation: does not remove chemical contaminants or sediment.
2. **Sawyer Squeeze filter ($35–$45, best all-around).** Filters bacteria and protozoa. Screws onto standard water bottle threads. Filters up to 100,000 gallons before replacement — effectively a lifetime supply. Backflush with the included syringe to maintain flow rate. Does not filter viruses (rare concern in US municipal water areas). Weight: 3 ounces.
3. **LifeStraw ($15, personal use).** Drink directly through it like a straw. Filters bacteria and protozoa. Cannot filter water into a container for storage — you must drink at the source. Best as a backup or for hiking/travel. Filters up to 1,000 gallons before replacement.
4. **Iodine ($8–$12).** Iodine is a multi-use survival essential — not just for water. For water purification: 5 drops of 2% tincture per liter, wait 30 minutes. Kills bacteria, viruses, and most protozoa. Vitamin C tablets neutralize the taste. Beyond water, iodine tincture doubles as a wound disinfectant — apply directly to cuts, scrapes, and blisters to prevent infection. It also works as a fire starter (the alcohol base is flammable). Carry the liquid tincture over tablets for versatility. Do not use long-term if you have thyroid conditions. Do not use if pregnant.
5. **Household bleach (free to $3).** Regular unscented bleach, 8.25% sodium hypochlorite. Two drops per quart of clear water, four drops if cloudy. Wait 30 minutes. Should smell slightly of chlorine after treatment — if not, repeat the dose and wait another 30 minutes. Effective against bacteria and viruses. Compact: a small bottle treats hundreds of gallons.

## Solar Charging — Energy Independence

A portable solar panel eliminates your dependence on wall outlets. Here is what you need to know.

- **Panel size:** A 20W panel ($30–$50 on Amazon or Walmart) charges a phone in 2–3 hours of direct sunlight. A 10W panel ($20–$30) takes 4–5 hours. Below 10W is too slow to be practical.
- **Positioning:** Face the panel south (in the Northern Hemisphere). Direct sunlight only — shade, even partial, cuts output dramatically. Best charging window: 10am–2pm. Adjust the angle throughout the day to face the sun directly.
- **Connect to a battery bank, not directly to your phone.** Solar output fluctuates with clouds and angle. A battery bank absorbs the inconsistency and delivers steady power to your phone. Solar panel charges battery bank during the day. Battery bank charges phone at night.
- **Overcast days:** Expect 10–25% of full-sun output. Still useful, but plan to supplement with wall charging.
- **Durability:** Folding panels with fabric cases survive backpack life better than rigid panels. Look for water-resistant models.

## Staying Warm Without Fire

Fire is not always legal, practical, or safe. But cold will still try to kill you. Here is how to stay warm without it.

### The Layering System — Detailed

- **Base layer (wicking):** Synthetic (polyester) or merino wool. Worn against your skin. Its job is to pull sweat away from your body. Wet skin loses heat 25 times faster than dry skin. Cotton fails here catastrophically — it absorbs moisture and holds it against you. If you own cotton long underwear, replace them.
- **Mid layer (insulating):** Fleece, down jacket, or synthetic-fill jacket. This is where your warmth comes from — it traps body heat in dead air space. Thicker is warmer. Two thin mid layers are more versatile than one thick one (you can remove one when active).
- **Outer layer (shell):** Windproof and waterproof. Blocks wind chill (which can make 40°F feel like 20°F) and keeps rain off your insulating layers. A wet mid layer loses up to 90% of its insulating value. Even a cheap rain poncho works as a shell in a pinch.

### Emergency Heat Solutions

- **Emergency Mylar blanket ($1):** Reflects up to 90% of your radiant body heat back to you. Wrap it around your sleeping bag or around your body under your outer layer. Noisy and fragile, but extraordinarily effective for the weight and cost. Carry two.
- **Cardboard under your sleeping bag:** Ground cold is the primary heat thief when sleeping outdoors. You lose more heat downward than upward. Three layers of corrugated cardboard have an R-value of 3–4 per inch. Free. Available behind any retail store. Replace when wet.
- **Newspaper insulation:** Stuff crumpled newspaper inside your jacket, between your layers, and inside your sleeping bag. The air pockets in crumpled newspaper provide genuine insulation. It is not comfortable and it rustles, but it works when the temperature drops and you do not have better gear.
- **Stuff your sleeping bag tightly.** Your body heats the air inside the bag. A bag that is too large has too much air to heat. Fill dead space with clothing, stuffing the foot box especially.

### Fresnel Lens — The $3 Superpower

A page-size Fresnel lens weighs almost nothing, costs about $3, and fits flat in your bag or book. In direct sunlight, it focuses light to a point hot enough to ignite tinder in seconds and boil small amounts of water in minutes. It is one of the highest-utility, lowest-weight, lowest-cost items you can carry. Available at dollar stores, office supply stores, Amazon, or educational catalogs. Practice with it before you need it — angle and distance to the focal point take a few tries to learn.

### Fire

Fire is the original survival tool, but on the street it comes with legal and safety constraints that vary dramatically by environment. In urban areas, open fires are usually illegal and will draw police attention. In rural or wooded areas, fire restrictions may be seasonal. Know your local rules before lighting anything.

When fire is an option: build it small, contain it in a fire ring or metal container, keep water nearby for extinguishing, and never leave it unattended. A Dakota fire hole (a small pit with an air channel) produces less visible flame and smoke than a surface fire, which matters when discretion is important.

### Insulation Hacks

Cardboard is one of the most underrated insulation materials available. It has an R-value of 3–4 per inch, which means a few layers of flattened cardboard under your sleeping bag provide meaningful insulation from ground cold. Ground cold is the primary heat thief when sleeping outdoors — you lose more heat downward into the ground than you lose upward into the air.

- **Cardboard:** Layer under your sleeping bag and pad. Free, widely available, effective. Replace when it gets wet.
- **Newspaper:** Stuffed inside clothing, it provides surprisingly effective insulation. Crumple it rather than layering it flat — the air pockets are what insulate.
- **Mylar emergency blankets:** These $1–$2 sheets reflect up to 90% of radiant body heat. Wrap one around your sleeping bag or tape one to the underside of your tarp as a radiant barrier. They are noisy and fragile but extraordinarily effective for their weight and cost.
- **Chemical hand warmers vs. reusable hand warmers:** Disposable chemical warmers ($1 each, 8–10 hours of heat) are effective but expensive over time. Reusable sodium acetate warmers ($5–$10, reactivated by boiling) pay for themselves in a few weeks of cold weather.

**Key Takeaways**

- **Water:** Sawyer Squeeze is the best all-around filter. Boil for 1 minute minimum. Do not drink discolored or odorous water.
- **Power:** A 10–20W solar panel and a battery bank give you energy independence. Charge opportunistically.
- **Heat:** Layer (wicking, insulating, shell). Insulate from the ground with cardboard. Cotton kills.
- **Fresnel lens:** $3, weighs nothing, starts fires and boils water in direct sun.
- **Cardboard is your friend.** R-value of 3–4 per inch. Free. Everywhere. Use it.

## Smokeless Fire and Advanced Heat

If you have been outside for weeks or months, you already know basic fire. What follows are the techniques that separate people who are surviving from people who are thriving. These methods use less fuel, produce less smoke, attract less attention, and work in conditions where a standard campfire fails.

### The Dakota Fire Hole

This is the single most important fire technique for stealth camping in urban or semi-urban environments. The Dakota tribe used it for thousands of years. The military uses it for tactical cooking. It produces almost zero smoke, uses half the wood of a surface fire, resists wind and rain, and can be completely concealed when you are done.

**How it works:** You dig two holes connected by an underground tunnel. The fire burns in one hole. The tunnel draws air into the base of the fire from the second hole, creating a superheated, oxygen-rich burn that consumes smoke before it reaches the surface.

### Step by Step

1. Find soft ground — dirt, sand, or loose soil. This does not work on concrete, asphalt, or rocky ground.
2. Dig the main fire pit: 12 inches deep, 10 inches wide.
3. Dig the air intake hole 12 inches away from the main pit.
4. Dig an angled tunnel connecting the bottom of the air intake hole to the bottom of the fire pit.
5. Orient the air intake hole so it faces INTO the prevailing wind. The wind pushes air down the tunnel and feeds the fire.
6. Line the bottom of the fire pit with rocks if available. They retain heat and radiate it upward for more efficient cooking.
7. Use small, dry sticks and hardwood. The hotter and faster the burn, the less smoke. Avoid green wood or damp wood.
8. Cook directly over the hole or set a grill grate, wire rack, or pot across the top of the pit.
9. When finished: let coals die completely, fill both holes with the excavated dirt, scatter leaves and debris over the top. No visible evidence.

### Hardwood vs. Softwood

Hardwood (oak, maple, hickory) burns hotter and longer with significantly less smoke. Softwood (pine, cedar, spruce) catches fire more easily but produces more smoke and burns faster. Strategy: use softwood kindling to start the fire, then feed it hardwood for cooking. If you do not know the difference, hardwood has broad leaves; softwood has needles.

### DIY Rocket Stove from Cinder Blocks

Stack four cinder blocks in an L-shape: two on the bottom (horizontal feed channel), two on top (vertical chimney). Fire goes in the bottom of the L. Pot sits on top of the chimney. This design forces all heat upward through a narrow channel, making it extremely fuel-efficient — it runs on twigs and small sticks. Hot enough to boil water in five minutes. Build one at your campsite and leave it in place. Cinder blocks are free behind construction sites and hardware stores.

### Candle Heating for Tents

A single candle inside an inverted terra cotta clay pot creates a radiant heater that warms a small tent 10–15°F above ambient. Place the candle on a flat, fireproof surface (a plate, a flat rock, a piece of metal). Invert the clay pot over the candle, leaving a gap at the bottom for air intake. The pot absorbs the candle heat and radiates it slowly into the tent.

> **⚠ Warning**
> **Carbon monoxide risk.** Any open flame in an enclosed space produces carbon monoxide, which kills without warning. Always leave ventilation open — crack the tent door or leave a vent flap open. Never fall asleep with an open flame burning. Never use a candle heater in a fully sealed space.

### Fire Starting Without Matches

**Fresnel lens ($3):** A page-size magnifying sheet from any dollar store. Focus sunlight to a point on dry tinder — it ignites in seconds. Weighs nothing, fits flat in your bag. Works only in direct sunlight. **Magnesium fire starter ($5):** Scrape magnesium shavings into a pile on your tinder, then strike the ferrocerium rod with the back of a knife. Sparks ignite the magnesium, which burns at 5,400°F. Works in any weather, including rain and wind. Worth every penny.

## Food Preservation and Cooling Without Electricity

Keeping food safe without refrigeration is one of the hardest long-term challenges of outdoor living. These techniques have kept food edible for centuries before electricity existed. They still work.

### Stream and River Cooling

Running water in streams and rivers stays between 50–60°F year-round in most climates. Submerge sealed food containers — ziplock bags, jars, sealed tupperware — in the current. Tie them to a rock, a tree root, or a stake driven into the bank so they do not drift away. A mesh bag or net bag works well for holding multiple containers. This keeps dairy, meat, and produce safe for days. It is a natural refrigerator that costs nothing.

### Underground Cache

The temperature 18–24 inches underground stays 55–60°F year-round regardless of surface temperature. Dig a hole, line it with rocks or sink a five-gallon bucket into the ground. Store food in sealed containers inside it. Cover with a board, then scatter leaves and dirt over the top to conceal it and insulate it further. Mark the location so you can find it. This is your pantry.

### Evaporative Cooler (Zeer Pot)

This is a 5,000-year-old technology that still works. Take two unglazed clay pots (terra cotta, $3–5 each at any garden center), one smaller than the other. Place the small pot inside the large pot. Fill the gap between them with wet sand. Place food inside the inner pot. Cover with a wet cloth. As the water in the sand evaporates, it pulls heat from the inner pot, cooling the contents 15–20°F below ambient temperature. Re-wet the sand twice a day. Works best in dry climates where evaporation is fastest. In humid climates, the effect is reduced but still measurable.

### Vacuum Sealing Without a Machine

The water displacement method removes nearly all air from a ziplock bag without any equipment. Put your food in the bag. Seal the zipper 90% closed, leaving a one-inch gap. Slowly lower the bag into a pot, bucket, or sink full of water up to the seal line. The water pressure pushes all the air out through the gap. Seal the last inch while the bag is still submerged. You now have a near-vacuum seal. This dramatically extends the shelf life of any food by removing the oxygen that bacteria need to grow.

### Salt and Smoke Preservation

Salt draws moisture out of meat, creating an environment where bacteria cannot survive. Coat strips of meat heavily in salt on all surfaces. Hang the strips in direct sunlight or near a fire (a Dakota fire hole works perfectly for smoking) for 12–24 hours. Smoked and salted meat lasts one to two weeks without refrigeration. This is how humans preserved meat for thousands of years before refrigerators existed.

### Drying Food

Slice fruit, vegetables, or meat as thin as possible. Lay the slices on a dark surface in direct sunlight — a car dashboard works perfectly, as does a dark-colored rock or a piece of sheet metal. Turn the slices every few hours for even drying. Fully dried food lasts weeks to months. Rehydrate by soaking in water for 30 minutes before eating. Jerky, dried fruit, and dried vegetables weigh almost nothing and pack enormous calories per ounce.

### Citrus Preservation

Lemon juice and lime juice are natural preservatives. Squeeze over cut fruit and vegetables to slow browning and inhibit bacterial growth. Vinegar works the same way. A small bottle of lemon juice ($1 at Dollar Tree) extends the life of fresh food by days.

### Build Your Food Cache Around Shelf-Stable Foods

These items last without refrigeration and form the backbone of a long-term food supply:

- **Peanut butter:** months. High calorie, high protein, needs no preparation.
- **Honey:** forever. Literally does not expire. Energy-dense.
- **Hard cheese in wax coating:** weeks to months unrefrigerated.
- **Beef jerky:** months. Lightweight, calorie-dense protein.
- **Canned goods:** years. Heavy but reliable. Pop-top cans if you lack a can opener.
- **Dried beans and rice:** years. Require cooking but extremely cheap and calorie-dense.
- **Oats:** months. Eat cold with water or cook. Cheap, filling, nutritious.
